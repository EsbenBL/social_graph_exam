'''Robert C. Lieberman''' (born September 26, 1964) is an American political scientist and the former [[Provost (education)|provost]] of the [[Johns Hopkins University]].<ref name=jhu>{{cite web|author=Dennis O'Shea |url=http://hub.jhu.edu/2013/04/29/robert-lieberman-provost |title=Columbia's Robert Lieberman named 14th provost of Johns Hopkins |publisher=Johns Hopkins University |date=April 29, 2013 |access-date=October 2, 2013}}</ref> A scholar of [[American political development]], Lieberman focuses primarily on race and politics and the American welfare state.

==Biography==
Robert Charles Lieberman was born in [[Boston]], [[Massachusetts]], in 1964. He received his [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] degree from [[Yale University]] in 1986 and his [[Ph.D.]] from [[Harvard University]] in 1994.<ref name="hub.jhu.edu">Rienzi, Greg. [http://hub.jhu.edu/gazette/2013/october/features-question-and-answer-new-provost-lieberman "One-on-one with Robert Lieberman, the new Johns Hopkins provost"], ''JHU Gazette'', Baltimore, 1 October 2013. Retrieved on 15 May 2015.</ref> From 1994 to 2013 he taught at [[Columbia University]], where he served as chairman of the international and public affairs department from 2007 to 2012 and interim dean of the [[School of International and Public Affairs, Columbia University|School of International and Public Affairs]] (SIPA) from 2012 to 2013.<ref>{{cite web|author=Margaret Mattes |url=http://www.columbiaspectator.com/2012/02/27/new-sipa-dean-focusing-globalizing-curriculum|title=New SIPA dean focusing on globalizing curriculum |publisher=[[Columbia Daily Spectator]] |date=February 27, 2012 |access-date=October 2, 2013}}</ref> He was instrumental in recruiting leading faculty to SIPA, restructuring the curriculum, and convening an international conference on the future of global public policy education.<ref name="Columbia University Alliance">{{cite web|url=http://alliance.columbia.edu/robert-c-lieberman|title= Columbia University Alliance}}</ref> In 2013, he was named the 14th provost of [[Johns Hopkins University]], concurrently joining the faculty of the department of [[Political Science]] at the Johns Hopkins [[Johns Hopkins University School of Arts and Sciences|Zanvyl Krieger School of Arts & Sciences]] on July 1, 2013.<ref name="jhu"/><ref>{{cite web|title=JHU Department of Political Science Faculty|url=http://politicalscience.jhu.edu/people/}}</ref> In this role, Lieberman was responsible for "promoting and coordinating the university’s teaching and research mission" across the university's nine academic divisions.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://web.jhu.edu/administration/provost/about|title=About the Provost}}</ref> He also had oversight for research at a university that for thirty-five years has led the country in higher education research spending.<ref>Rosen, Jill. [http://hub.jhu.edu/gazette/2015/march-april/currents-jhu-first-again-in-research-spending "JHU first in research spending for 35th straight year"], ''The Gazette'', Baltimore, 1 March 2015. Retrieved on 15 May 2015.</ref><ref>Anderson, Nick. [https://www.washingtonpost.com/local/education/johns-hopkins-again-tops-in-university-research-spending/2013/12/09/6bc681ca-60eb-11e3-bf45-61f69f54fc5f_story.html "Johns Hopkins again No. 1 in university research spending"], ''[[The Washington Post]]'', Washington, D.C., 9 December 2013. Retrieved on 12 March 2015.</ref> In August 2016 he stepped down as provost and was appointed Krieger-Eisenhower Professor in the Johns Hopkins Department of Political Science. He now lives in Baltimore, Maryland in the Roland Park neighborhood with his family. His wife is Lauren Osborne, and their children include a son Benjamin and twins, Martha and Aaron.

==Research==
Lieberman has written extensively on American political development, social welfare policy, issues of race and politics in America, institutional racism, and the welfare state.<ref name="Columbia University Alliance"/><ref>{{cite web|url=http://hub.jhu.edu/2015/02/17/racial-inequality-lieberman-harris-foreign-affairs|title=Racial inequality remains a 'defining feature of American life,' Johns Hopkins provost writes}}</ref> He has received support from the [[National Science Foundation]], [[Russell Sage Foundation]], the [[German Marshall Fund]], and the [[American Philosophical Society]],<ref name="hub.jhu.edu"/> and awards such as the [[American Political Science Association]]’s [[Leonard D. White Award]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.apsanet.org/content.asp?contentid=752|title=Leonardo D. White Award}}</ref> the [[Social Science History Association]]’s President’s Book Award,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://ssha.org/awards/award-winners/178-recipients-of-the-ssha-presidents-book-award|title=Recipients of the SSHA Presidents Book Award}}</ref> [[Harvard University Press]]’s Thomas J. Wilson Prize,<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.hup.harvard.edu/news/thomas-j-wilson-prize.html|title=The Thomas J. Wilson Memorial Prize}}</ref> and [[Columbia University]]’s Lionel Trilling Book Award.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.college.columbia.edu/academics/Trilling|title=Past Winners of the Lional Trilling Book Award}}</ref>
