'''Shanto Iyengar''' is an American political scientist and professor of political science at [[Stanford University]]. He is also the [[Harry Chandler|Harry]] & [[Norman Chandler]] Professor of Communication at Stanford, the director of Stanford's Political Communication Lab, and a senior fellow at the [[Hoover Institution]].<ref name=pcl>{{Cite web |url=https://pcl.stanford.edu/people/siyengar/ |title=Shanto Iyengar |website=Stanford Political Communication Lab |access-date=2017-10-08}}</ref>

==Biography==
Iyengar received his bachelor's degree from [[Linfield College]] in 1968 and his Ph.D. from the [[University of Iowa]] in 1972. In 1973, he joined the faculty of [[Kansas State University]] as an assistant professor, where he remained until 1979. He taught at [[Yale University]] as an assistant professor from 1983 to 1985, and then taught at [[Stony Brook University]] and the [[University of California, Los Angeles]] before joining Stanford's faculty in 1998.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://files-politicalscience-stanford-edu.s3-us-west-2.amazonaws.com/s3fs-public/iyengar_2017-02-14_cv.pdf |title=Shanto Iyengar CV}}</ref>

==Research==
Iyengar is known for his work on the role of the [[news media]] in contemporary politics.<ref name=pcl/> This includes work he conducted with [[Dartmouth College]] political scientist Sean J. Westwood, in which they analyze partisan divisions in American politics.<ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/06/15/upshot/how-we-became-bitter-political-enemies.html |title=How We Became Bitter Political Enemies |last=Badger |first=Emily |date=2017-06-15 |work=The New York Times |access-date=2017-10-08 |last2=Chokshi |first2=Niraj |language=en-US |issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |url=https://www.npr.org/sections/theprotojournalist/2014/10/15/356101335/what-is-really-tearing-america-apart |title=What Is Really Tearing America Apart |last=Weeks |first=Linton |date=2014-10-15 |work=NPR |access-date=2017-10-08 |language=en}}</ref>
