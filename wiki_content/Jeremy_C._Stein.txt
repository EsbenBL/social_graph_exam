'''Jeremy Chaim Stein''' (born October 17, 1960) an American macroeconomist. He is the [[Moise Safra|Moise Y. Safra]] Professor of Economics at [[Harvard University]], the chair of the Department of Economics at Harvard, and an [[investment]] industry consultant. Stein was [[American Finance Association#Past, present and future presidents|president of the]] [[American Finance Association]] in 2008, and a member of the [[Federal Reserve Board of Governors|Board of Governors]] of the [[Federal Reserve System|U.S. Federal Reserve]] from 2012 to 2014.

==Early life and education==
Stein was born to a secular [[Jewish]] family,<ref>[http://access.cjh.org/home.php?type=extid&term=1525679#1 Center for Jewish History: "AHC interview with Elly Stein"] 2012 | minute 27:30| ''"So I am not religious even though I am nostalgic for religion...My husbands very non-religious...My son married a non-Jewish woman. My grandchildren will tell you if you ask them: I'm a little Jewish and a little Christian"''</ref> the son of mathematician [[Elias M. Stein]] and Elly Intrator.<ref name=StAndrews>[http://www-history.mcs.st-and.ac.uk/Biographies/Stein.html University of St Andrews, Scotland - School of Mathematics and Statistics: "Elias Menachem Stein" by J.J. O'Connor and E F Robertson] February 2010</ref> Both his parents were Jewish refugees during [[World War II]] who immigrated to the United States.<ref name=StAndrews /><ref>[http://access.cjh.org/home.php?type=extid&term=1525679#1 Center for Jewish History: "AHC interview with Elly Stein"] 2012</ref> He graduated [[Latin honors|summa cum laude]] with an A.B. in economics from [[Princeton University]] in 1983 after completing a 118-page senior thesis titled "Pseudo-Securities: An Analysis of Financially Hedged Positions."<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Stein|first=Jeremy C.|date=1983|title=Pseudo-Securities: An Analysis of Financially Hedged Positions|url=http://dataspace.princeton.edu/jspui/handle/88435/dsp01rx913q79m}}</ref> While at Princeton, he was co-captain of the men's gymnastics team, specializing in rings.<ref name=wsj20130318>{{cite news|last=McGrane|first=Victoria|title=Inside a Warier Fed, Watch the New Guy|newspaper=[[The Wall Street Journal]]|date=March 18, 2013|author2=Hilsenrath, Jon|pages=C1, C7}}</ref> In 1986, he earned a PhD in economics from [[MIT]]. His 1998 paper "Risk management, capital budgeting, and capital structure policy for financial institutions: an integrated approach" with Kenneth A. Froot won the [[Jensen Prize]].<ref>Froot, K.A., and J. Stein. [https://scholar.harvard.edu/kenfroot/publications/risk-management-capital-budgeting-and-capital-structure-policy-financial  “Risk Management, Capital Budgeting and Capital Structure Policy for Financial Institutions: An Integrated Approach.”] Journal of Financial Economics 47, no. 1 (1998): 55–82.</ref>

==Career==
After serving a one-year internship at Goldman Sachs Group Inc.,<ref name=wsj20130318/> he became an assistant professor of finance at the [[Harvard Business School]] from 1987-1990, and finance faculty of MIT’s Sloan School of Management for ten years after that. Stein rejoined Harvard in 2000.<ref name="bg">{{cite web|url=https://www.bostonglobe.com/business/2011/12/27/obama-nominates-harvard-jeremy-stein-for-fed-board/Ko0kJROFWU0D3G44fD0V7N/story.html|title=Harvard professor to be nominated to Federal Reserve|last=Wallack|first=Todd|date=27 December 2011|publisher=Boston Globe|accessdate=27 December 2011}}</ref>

Stein received the [[Fama-DFA Prize]], which is an annual prize given to authors with the best capital markets and asset pricing research papers published in the [[Journal of Financial Economics]] for 2002.<ref>{{cite journal|author1=Joseph Chen|author2-link=Harrison Hong|author2=Harrison Hong|author3=Jeremy C. Stein|title=Breadth of ownership and stock returns|url=https://scholar.harvard.edu/stein/publications/breadth-ownership-and-stock-returns|doi=10.1016/S0304-405X(02)00223-4|journal=Journal of Financial Economics|volume= 66| issue =  2–3|pages=171–205|year=2002|citeseerx=10.1.1.297.3149}}</ref>

He designed quantitative asset-management strategies for [[Guggenheim Partners]] from 2005 to 2007, was also a senior adviser to the Treasury secretary and was on the staff at the [[United States National Economic Council|National Economic Council]] in 2009.<ref name=NYT01/>

===Federal Reserve Board===
On December 27, 2011, President Barack Obama announced that he planned to nominate Stein to fill one of the two vacancies on the seven-member Federal Reserve Board. Stein's nomination was filibustered by Republicans in the United States Senate. On May 15, 2012, Senate Majority Leader [[Harry Reid]] motioned to invoke [[cloture]] and break the filibuster on both the nominations of Stein and of [[Jerome Powell|Powell]].<ref>Beth, Richard S., [https://www.senate.gov/CRSReports/crs-publish.cfm?pid=%270E%2C*P%2C%3B%3C%20P%20%20%0A "Cloture Attempts on Nominations: Data and Historical Development"], ''senate.gov'', June 26, 2013.</ref>  On May 17, 2012, a Senate floor vote was held on Stein's nomination with a required 60-vote threshold for confirmation.  Senators voted 70-24 to confirm Stein.<ref>Smith, Donna, [https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-fed-nominees-idUSBRE84G0ZC20120517 "Senate votes put Fed board at full strength"], ''reuters.com'', May 17, 2012.</ref>

On April 3, 2014 Stein announced that he would resign his position at the Federal Reserve and return to Harvard by May 28.<ref>{{cite news| url=http://www.marketwatch.com/story/jeremy-stein-to-resign-from-federal-reserve-2014-04-03?reflink=MW_news_stmp |author=Steve Goldstein| title=Jeremy Stein to resign from Federal Reserve |publisher=Market watch, [[Wall Street Journal]] |date=April 3, 2014|accessdate=April 3, 2014}}</ref>

=== After the Fed ===

In addition to the post at Harvard, in March 2015, Stein began working as a consultant for [[hedge fund]] [[BlueMountain Capital]].<ref name=NYT01>Stevenson, Alexandra, [http://mobile.nytimes.com/2015/03/25/business/dealbook/former-fed-board-member-to-advise-hedge-fund.html "Former Fed Board Member to Advise Hedge Fund"], New York ''Times'', March 23, 2015. Retrieved 2015-03-24.</ref>

=== Research ===

Stein's research covers subjects including behavioral finance and market efficiency; corporate investment and financing decisions; risk management; capital allocation inside firms; banking; financial regulation; and monetary policy. <ref>{{Cite web|url=https://scholar.harvard.edu/stein/home|title = Jeremy Stein}}</ref>

Stein worked as a research associate at the National Bureau of Economic Research. He is also a member of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences. <ref>{{Cite web|url=https://scholar.harvard.edu/stein/home|title = Jeremy Stein}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Stein is married with three children and lives in Boston.  His daughter Carolyn is an economist at [[University of California, Berkeley|University of California - Berkeley]].<ref name=wsj20130318/> He is a registered Democrat.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2011/12/28/business/economy/obama-to-nominate-economics-professor-and-ex-treasury-official-to-fed-board.html|title=Obama to Nominate Two for Vacancies on Fed Board|newspaper=The New York Times|date=27 December 2011|last1=Appelbaum|first1=Binyamin}}</ref>
