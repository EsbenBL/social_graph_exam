'''Victoria Schuck''' (1909–1999) was an [[United States|American]] [[political scientist]] who was the president of [[Mount Vernon Seminary and College|Mount Vernon College]] from 1977 to 1983.<ref name=portrait/> As an expert on the [[political participation]] of women and women as political candidates, she contributed to the development of the study of women and politics as a subfield of political science. She also specialized in the state politics of [[New England]], and the politics of [[South Vietnam]]. As one of the first 80 women to earn a PhD in political science, Schuck published extensively on the status of women in the profession. In total she published more than 80 articles or monographs, and co-edited several academic books. Schuck spent most of her career at [[Mount Holyoke College]], where she was a Professor of Political Science from 1940 until 1977, and prior to that she was a professor at [[Florida State University]].<ref name=mh15/>

==Early life and education==
Schuck was born on February 15, 1909, in [[Oklahoma City]], Oklahoma, to Anthony B. Schuck and Anna Prieb Schuck.<ref name=mh15/> She was raised in [[San Marino, California]].<ref name=mh15/> She attended [[Stanford University]], where she earned a BA degree in 1930, an MA in 1931, and a PhD in 1937, all in political science.<ref name=mh15/> In 2003, a publication by [[George Washington University]] estimated that this made her "one of the first 80 or so women to earn a Ph.D. in political science".<ref name=portrait>{{cite journal |title=Portrait of Victoria Schuck Unveiled at Mount Vernon Campus of George Washington University |journal=PS: Political Science & Politics |volume=36 |issue=1 |date=January 2003 |doi=10.1017/S1049096503001872}}</ref>

==Career==
Schuck was a professor of political science at Florida State University (then Florida State College for Women<ref name=mh15/>) from 1937 until 1940, when she joined the faculty at Mount Holyoke College.<ref name=wapo/> During [[World War II]], Schuck worked as a principal program analyst at the [[Office of Price Administration]] in the [[Office for Emergency Management]] of the [[Federal government of the United States]].<ref name=wapo/> In 1977, she left Mount Holyoke to become president of Mount Vernon College, where she remained until 1983.<ref name=portrait/><ref name=wapo/> Mount Vernon College which was purchased in 1999 by George Washington University, and became the Mount Vernon Campus of The George Washington University.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Mikhalevsky |first=Nina |year=2001 |title=Dear Daughters: A History of Mount Vernon Seminary and College |location=Washington, DC |publisher=Mount Vernon Seminary and College Alumnae Association}}</ref> Schuck was also a visiting professor at [[Smith College]] during the 1948–1949 school year, a visiting professor at Stanford University in 1952, and a visiting scholar at the [[Brookings Institution]] during 1967–1968.<ref name=mh15/>

Schuck was an early researcher in the topic of women and politics. Her studies of women and politics included research on women who were politicians, as well as on women's political participation as citizens.<ref>{{cite journal |author1=Mary L. Shanley |author2=Victoria Schuck |title=In Search of Political Woman |journal=Social Science Quarterly |volume=55 |issue=3 |pages=632 |date=1 December 1974}}</ref> Schuck also published papers regarding the situation for women in political science during the second half of the 20th century.<ref>{{cite journal |first=Schuck |last=Victoria |title=Women in Political Science: Some Preliminary Observations |journal=PS: Political Science & Politics |volume=2 |issue=4 |pages=642–653 |year=1969 |doi=10.2307/417989}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal |first=Victoria |last=Schuck |title=Sexism and Scholarship: A Brief Overview of Women, Academia, and the Disciplines |journal=Social Science Quarterly |volume=55 |issue=3 |pages=563 |year=1974}}</ref>

Schuck specialized particularly in the state politics of New England, especially [[Massachusetts]].<ref name=portrait/> She also conducted comparative politics work on elections and political participation. Notably, she received funding in 1996 and 1971 to observe elections in South Vietnam.<ref name=mh15/> As part of that project, she also researched the [[South Vietnam#Government|Constitution of South Vietnam]].<ref name=mh15/> Following the [[Watergate scandal]], Schuck wrote a review of scholarly works on the topic.<ref name=mh15/>

In addition to her scholarly articles, Schuck was an editor of the 1979 book ''Women Organizing: An Anthology'', and the 1980 book ''New England Politics''.<ref name=wapo/> During the 1970s, she was a political science book reviewer for the ''Key Reporter'' magazine of the [[Phi Beta Kappa]] honor society.<ref name=wapo/>

From 1978 to 1980, Schuck was a member of the D.C. Commission on Post-Secondary Education.<ref name=wapo/> She also served on the House Commission on Administrative Review of the [[United States House of Representatives]], on the [[Presidential Commission (United States)|United States Presidential Commission]] on Registration and Voter
Participation formed by [[John F. Kennedy]],<ref name=mh15/> and as a United States representative to the conference of the [[United Nations Decade for Women]] in [[Nairobi]].<ref name=wapo/> Schuck was also a nongovernmental representative to the [[United Nations Commission on the Status of Women]].<ref name=wapo/>

==Recognition==
Schuck was involved in the development of women and politics as a subfield of political science, including close involvement with the founding of the Women's Caucus of the American Political Science Association in 1969 and membership on the Association's 1971 Committee on the Status of Women.<ref name=portrait/>

Schuck is the namesake of the [[American Political Science Association]]'s [[Victoria Schuck Award]], an annual award that is granted each year to the author or authors of the best book published in the previous year on the topic of women and politics.<ref name=apsa>{{cite web |url=https://www.apsanet.org/PROGRAMS/APSA-Awards/Victoria-Schuck-Award |title=Victoria Schuck Award |publisher=American Political Science Association |year=2018 |access-date=20 April 2020}}</ref> This award is one of the few awards that is granted directly by the American Political Science Association and not by one of its area-specific sections.<ref name=str>{{cite journal |first=Sue |last=Tolleson-Rinehart |title=The Victoria Schuck Award: A Sonata for Gender Politics Scholarship |journal=[[Politics & Gender]] |volume=7 |issue=2 |pages=294–302 |date=6 June 2011 |doi=10.1017/S1743923X11000146}}</ref> The prize was established in 1986, after Schuck advocated for the establishment of a book prize for women and politics scholarship.<ref name=str/> Schuck endowed it with an initial fund of $3000, to be given in increments of $500 per annual award, and to be funded thereafter by the Association through contributions.<ref name=str/> By 2020 the award carried a prize of $750.<ref name=apsa/>

Stanford University endows a Victoria Schuck Faculty Scholar Chair in Political Science,<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/faculty/avidit-acharya |title=Avidit Acharya |publisher=Stanford University |year=2020 |access-date=20 April 2020}}</ref> funded through the Victoria Schuck Faculty Scholar Fund.<ref>{{cite book |author1=Justin Grimmer |author2=Sean J. Westwood |author3=Solomon Messing |date=23 November 2014 |title=The Impression of Influence: Legislator Communication, Representation, and Democratic Accountability |publisher=Princeton University Press |page=xiii}}</ref>

In 2003, a portrait of Schuck was hung at the Mount Vernon Campus of George Washington University to commemorate her years as president of Mount Vernon College.<ref name=portrait/>
