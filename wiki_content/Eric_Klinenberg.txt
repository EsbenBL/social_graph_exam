'''Eric M. Klinenberg''' (born November 14, 1970) is an American sociologist and a scholar of urban studies, culture, and media. He is best known for his contributions as a public sociologist.

==Biography==

Klinenberg was born in [[Chicago]] to a family of [[Czech-Jewish]] origin.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.economist.com/open-future/2019/05/31/populism-can-be-beaten-back-by-libraries-really|title=Populism can be beaten back by libraries. Really|newspaper=The Economist|date=31 May 2019}}</ref> He attended the [[Francis W. Parker School (Chicago)|Francis W. Parker School]] and later earned a bachelor of arts degree from [[Brown University]] (1993), followed by a master's degree (1997) and  PhD (2000) from the [[University of California, Berkeley]]. He is currently Professor of Sociology, Public Policy, and Media, Culture, and Communication at [[New York University]], as well as the editor of the journal ''[[Public Culture]]''. In 2012, Klinenberg became the director of the Institute for Public Knowledge at New York University.  In 2013, he was appointed research director of the Rebuild by Design competition.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.rebuildbydesign.org/about/#research-advisory-group|title=Rebuild by Design – About|work=Rebuild by Design}}</ref>

==Publications==
Klinenberg's first book, ''Heat Wave: A Social Autopsy of Disaster in Chicago'', was published by the [[University of Chicago Press]] in 2002. The book is an account and analysis of the [[1995 Chicago heat wave]]. The book won several scholarly prizes, including the American Sociological Association Robert Park Book Award, the Urban Affairs Association best book award, the British Sociological Association book prize, the Mirra Komarovsky Book Prize, and honorable mention for the C Wright Mills Award, and was a Favorite Book selection by the ''[[Chicago Tribune]]''.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/H/bo3641018.html |title=Archived copy |access-date=2014-07-31 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140721080357/http://www.press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/H/bo3641018.html |archive-date=2014-07-21 }}</ref> A theatrical adaptation of the book premiered in Chicago in 2008.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.playbill.com/article/two-il-troupes-conjure-heat-wave-drama-of-chicago-weather-disaster-feb-21-com-147791|title=Two IL Troupes Conjure Heat Wave, Drama of Chicago Weather Disaster, Feb. 21|date=2008-02-21}}</ref>

His second book, ''Fighting for Air: The Battle to Control America’s Media'', was called "politically passionate and intellectually serious,",<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.cjr.org/review/owning_up_a_new_book_stops_sho.php|title=Owning Up: A New Book Stops Short of Deepening the Discourse on Media Concentration|work=Columbia Journalism Review}}</ref> "a must-read for those who wonder what happened to good radio, accurate reporting and autonomous public interest".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://newyork.timeout.com/arts-culture/books/5278/fighting-for-air|title=Fighting for Air|work=Time Out New York}}</ref>

His third book, ''Going Solo: The Extraordinary Rise and Surprising Appeal of Living Alone'', was published in February 2012 by Penguin Press.<ref>{{cite news|title=America: Single, and Loving It|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2012/02/12/fashion/America-Single-and-Loving-It.html|access-date=11 February 2012|newspaper=New York Times|date=10 February 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|last=Stromberg|first=Joseph|title=Eric Klinenberg on Going Solo: The surprising benefits, to oneself and to society, of living alone|url=http://www.smithsonianmag.com/science-nature/Eric-Klinenberg-on-Going-Solo.html|access-date=11 February 2012|newspaper=Smithsonian Magazine|date=February 2012}}</ref> ''Going Solo'' has been translated into several languages, including Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Russian, Turkish, and Hungarian, and has generated widespread debate. In a cover story, ''Time'' magazine featured ''Going Solo'' as "the number one idea that is changing our lives."<ref>{{cite news|url=http://content.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,2108055,00.html|title=1. Living Alone Is The New Norm|date=12 March 2012|work=TIME.com|first=Eric|last=Klinenberg}}</ref>

In 2013, Klinenberg wrote an influential article in the New Yorker on [[Hurricane Sandy]] and [[climate change adaptation]], in which he explained the role of social infrastructure in protecting cities and communities.<ref>{{cite magazine |last=Klinenberg |first=Eric |date=January 7, 2013 |title=Dept. of Urban Planning: Adaptation |magazine=[[The New Yorker]] |volume=88 |issue=42 |pages=32–37 |url=http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2013/01/07/adaptation-2 |access-date=2014-10-24}}</ref>

Klinenberg co-wrote a book about romance with comedian [[Aziz Ansari]], ''[[Modern Romance: An Investigation]]'', published in June 2015.<ref>{{cite web|title=Modern Romance: An Investigation|url=http://book.azizansari.com|publisher=Aziz Ansari|access-date=6 April 2015}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.glamour.com/sex-love-life/blogs/smitten/2014/03/aziz-ansari-modern-romantics-r.html|title=Read Hilarious Highlights From Aziz Ansari's "Modern Romantics" Questions on Reddit|author=Glamour Magazine|work=Glamour Magazine|date=5 March 2014}}</ref>

In 2018, Klinenberg published a book on the role of social infrastructure in American culture titled ''Palaces for the People: How Social Infrastructure Can Help Fight Inequality, Polarization, and the Decline of Civic Life''.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2018/09/14/books/review/palaces-for-the-people-eric-klinenberg.html|title=The Key to Happiness Might Be as Simple as a Library or a Park|work=New York Times|date=14 September 2018|access-date=31 January 2021|last1=Buttigieg|first1=Pete}}</ref> Klinenberg analyzes the role of public spaces such as libraries, parks, gardens, and universities among other investments help to strengthen and heal communities and build [[social capital]].

In addition to his books and scholarly articles, Klinenberg has contributed to ''[[The New York Times Magazine]]'', ''[[Rolling Stone]]'', ''[[The London Review of Books]]'', ''[[The Nation]]'', ''[[The Washington Post]]'', ''[[Mother Jones (magazine)|Mother Jones]]'', ''[[The Guardian]]'', ''[[Le Monde diplomatique]]'', ''[[Slate (magazine)|Slate]]'', ''[[Playboy]]'', the radio program ''[[This American Life]]'' <ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.thisamericanlife.org/radio-archives/episode/346/home-alone|title=Home Alone - This American Life|work=This American Life|date=2007-12-21}}</ref> and the television program ''[[Real Time with Bill Maher]]''.
