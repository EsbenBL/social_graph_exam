'''Norman Jay Ornstein''' ({{IPAc-en|ˈ|ɔr|n|s|t|iː|n}}; born October 14, 1948) is an American [[political scientist]] and an Emeritus scholar at the [[American Enterprise Institute]] (AEI), a [[Washington, D.C.]] conservative [[think tank]]. He is the co-author (along with [[Thomas E. Mann]]) of ''[[It's Even Worse Than It Looks|It's Even Worse Than It Looks: How the American Constitutional System Collided With the New Politics of Extremism]]''.

== Biography ==
Norman Jay Ornstein was born in [[Grand Rapids, Minnesota]]<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.slphistory.org/ornsteinnorm/ |work=St. Louis Park Historical Society |title=Norm Ornstein |date=2017 |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref> on October 14, 1948.<ref>{{cite web |title=Ornstein, Norman J. |publisher=[[Library of Congress]] |work=Name Authority File |url=http://id.loc.gov/authorities/names/n50002184.html |date=April 2, 2015 |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref> His father was a traveling salesman, and the family spent much of Norman's childhood in Canada. He was a child prodigy, graduated from high school when he was fourteen and from college when he was eighteen.<ref>Steven Waldman. "The King of Quotes." Washington Monthly, December 1986, p. 35.</ref> He received his BA from the [[University of Minnesota]],<ref>{{cite web |title=Pitt's Honors College to Host Thomas E. Mann and Norman J. Ornstein |date=November 17, 2008 |url=http://www.news.pitt.edu/news/pitts-honors-college-host-thomas-e-mann-and-norman-j-ornstein-discussion-2008-presidential-elec |work=[[University of Pittsburgh]] |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref> and subsequently, received a [[Ph.D.]] in political science from the [[University of Michigan]] in 1974.<ref>Steve Goldberg. "Dr. Quote Can Be a Reporter's Best Friend." Richmond (VA) Times-Dispatch, December 5, 1986, p. A-14.</ref> By the mid-1970s, he had become a professor of political science at Catholic University in Washington, D.C. and was establishing a reputation as an expert on the [[United States Congress]].<ref>Thomas Southwick. "O'Neill's Role as House Speaker." Nashua (NH) Telegraph, January 3, 1977, p. 14.</ref>

Ornstein studies American politics and is a frequent contributor to ''[[The Washington Post]]'' and many magazines, such as ''[[The Atlantic]]'' and the ''[[National Journal]]''.<ref name="washdiplo">{{cite web|last=Coleman|first=Michael|title=Scholar Pins Congressional Dysfunction Squarely on GOP|work=[[Washington Diplomat]]|access-date=December 21, 2013|date=November 29, 2013|url=https://www.washdiplomat.com/index.php?option=com_content&view=article&id=9789:scholar-pins-congressional-dysfunction-squarely-on-gop&catid=1511&Itemid=428}}</ref> He wrote a weekly column for ''[[Roll Call]]'' from 1993 until April 10, 2013, and was co-director, along with [[Thomas E. Mann]], of the AEI-[[Brookings Institution|Brookings]] Election Reform Project. He helped draft key parts of the [[Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act]] of 2002, also known as the [[John McCain|McCain]]-[[Russ Feingold|Feingold]] Act.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.csmonitor.com/2003/1211/p01s01-usju.html |last=Richey |first=Warren |title=Court Upholds 'Soft Money' Ban |work=[[The Christian Science Monitor]] |date=December 11, 2003 |access-date= March 11, 2011}}</ref> Ornstein is a registered Democrat,<ref name="GOPbroke">{{cite web |first1=Thomas E. |last1=Mann |first2=Norman J. |last2=Ornstein |title=How the Republicans Broke Congress |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2017/12/02/opinion/sunday/republicans-broke-congress-politics.html|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20171203045741/https://www.nytimes.com/2017/12/02/opinion/sunday/republicans-broke-congress-politics.html|archive-date=December 3, 2017|work=[[The New York Times]] |date=December 2, 2017 |access-date=December 3, 2017}}</ref> but considers himself a centrist, and has voted for individuals from both parties.<ref name=neocon>{{cite news|last=Ornstein|first=Norman|title=My Neocon Problem|url=http://www.tnr.com/article/my-neocon-problem|access-date=May 25, 2011|work=[[The New Republic]]|date=September 10, 2007}}</ref>

Ornstein is a member of the advisory board of the [[Future of American Democracy Foundation]],<ref>[http://thefutureofamericandemocracyfoundation.org/index.html Future of American Democracy Foundation website]</ref> a [[nonprofit]], nonpartisan foundation in partnership with [[Yale University Press]] and the Yale Center for International and Area Studies "dedicated to research and education aimed at renewing and sustaining the historic vision of American democracy".<ref>[http://yalepress.yale.edu/yupbooks/SeriesPage.asp?series=127 The Future of American Democracy Series]</ref> He also served on the advisory board of the [[Institute for Law and Politics]] at the [[University of Minnesota Law School]].<ref>{{cite web |title=Advisory Board |url=http://politics.law.umn.edu/advisory-board.html |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120309051014/http://politics.law.umn.edu/advisory-board.html |archive-date=March 9, 2012 |work=[[Institute for Law and Politics]] |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref> Ornstein is also a member of the board of directors of the nonpartisan election reform group Why Tuesday?. He is on the advisory council of the cross-partisan grassroots campaign [[Represent.Us]],<ref>{{cite web |title=About Us: Advisory Council |url=https://represent.us/about/ |work=[[Represent.Us]] |date=2017 |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref> where he served as a  consultant in the crafting of the [[American Anti-Corruption Act]].

''[[Foreign Policy (magazine)|Foreign Policy]]'' named Ornstein, along with [[Thomas E. Mann]], one of its 2012 [[FP Top 100 Global Thinkers|Top 100 Global Thinkers]] "for diagnosing America's political dysfunction".<ref>{{cite web |url=https://foreignpolicy.com/articles/2012/11/26/the_fp_100_global_thinkers?page=0,34 |title=The FP Top 100 Global Thinkers |date=26 November 2012 |work=Foreign Policy |access-date=November 28, 2012 |archive-date=November 28, 2012 |archive-url=https://www.webcitation.org/6CViUyRpk?url=http://www.foreignpolicy.com/articles/2012/11/26/the_fp_100_global_thinkers?page=0,33 |url-status=live }}</ref>

As of 2013, Ornstein has become known for "blistering critiques of Congress", which he has been following for the past three decades.<ref name="washdiplo"/><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2014/03/02/opinion/sunday/the-dying-art-of-legislating.html?hp&rref=opinion|title=The Dying Art of Legislating|work=The New York Times|date=March 1, 2014|access-date=March 2, 2014}}</ref>

Ornstein supports legal recognition of same-sex marriages.<ref>{{cite web |first=Norman |last=Ornstein |display-authors=etal |title=Freedom to Marry, Freedom to Dissent: Why We Must Have Both |url=http://www.realclearpolitics.com/articles/2014/04/22/freedom_to_marry_freedom_to_dissent_why_we_must_have_both_122376.html |work=[[RealClearPolitics]] |date=April 22, 2014 |access-date=July 23, 2017}}</ref>

He opposed President [[Donald Trump]].<ref>{{cite web |first=Katherine |last=Brewer |title=What Happens After Trump? |url=http://www.wbur.org/freakout/2017/10/12/after-trump-norm-ornstein-bob-corker-huey-long |work=[[WBUR-FM|WBUR]] |date=October 12, 2017 |access-date=October 29, 2018}}</ref> He also criticized the [[Electoral College]], saying that the more presidents are elected without the popular vote, "the more you get the sense that voters don’t have a say in the choice of their leaders".<ref>{{cite news |last1=Astor |first1=Maggie |title=The Electoral College Is Close. The Popular Vote Isn’t. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/article/popular-vote-electoral-college.html?action=click&module=Spotlight&pgtype=Homepage |access-date=7 November 2020 |work=The New York Times |date=6 November 2020}}</ref>

==Personal==
Ornstein is married to Judith L. Harris, a [[litigation]] [[lawyer|attorney]] specializing in regulatory matters. He is a long-time friend of former U.S. Senator and comedian [[Al Franken]].<ref name=neocon/> A fictional version of Ornstein appears in Franken's political spoof novel, ''[[Why Not Me? (novel)|Why Not Me?]]'', as the campaign manager for Franken's improbable presidential run.<ref>{{cite web |last=O'Rourke |first=P. J. |url=https://www.nytimes.com/books/99/02/14/reviews/990214.14orourt.html?_r=1 |title=If Elected, I Will Not Serve for Long (Book review) |work=The New York Times |date=February 14, 1999 |access-date=December 3, 2017}}</ref>

Ornstein and his wife, as well as their younger son Danny, established the Matthew Harris Ornstein Memorial Foundation in honor of the couple's eldest son, who died in 2015 at age 34 from accidental [[carbon monoxide poisoning]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.mornstein.org/ |website=MHO Foundation |language=en |access-date=2020-04-14 |title=The Matthew Harris Ornstein Memorial Foundation}}</ref><ref>Norman J. Ornstein, [https://www.nytimes.com/2015/11/17/opinion/how-to-help-save-the-mentally-ill-from-themselves.html How to Help Save the Mentally Ill From Themselves], ''New York Times'' (November 17, 2015).</ref><ref>Rosa Caragena, [https://www.washingtonian.com/2020/04/30/the-deeply-personal-story-behind-the-pbs-documentary-the-definition-of-insanity/The Deeply Personal Story Behind the PBS Documentary "The Definition of Insanity": Norm Ornstein and Judy Harris have their own tragic connection to mental illness.], ''Washingtonian'' (April 30, 2020).</ref>
