'''Louis Auguste Say''' (9 March 1774 in [[Lyon]] &ndash; 6 March 1840 in [[Paris]]) was a [[French people|French]] businessman and [[economics|economist]]. He founded large sugar refineries in [[Nantes]] and Paris, and the sugar company "Say", known after 1972 as [[Béghin-Say]]; as of 2002 it is a subsidiary of [[Tereos]].

==Early life and family background==
Say was born on 6 March 1774 in Lyon, France.<ref name="marcpenouil">Penouil, Marc. [http://www.persee.fr/web/revues/home/prescript/article/reco_0035-2764_1967_num_18_1_407750 "Economistes de jadis: Louise Say"], ''[[Revue économique]]'', 1967, vol. 18, issue 1, pp. 98-122</ref><ref name="deleconomiecoloniale">Célimène, Fred and Legris, André. [https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=n2QkA7LOJC8C&pg=PA57&lpg=PA57&dq=%22Louis+Say%22+sucre&source=bl&ots=ImbiShWd3X&sig=IODeUyEDYf8D_pqRavQdBgPMhno&hl=en&sa=X&ei=4h1hVcS0CNSQ7AbI14DoCg&ved=0CGoQ6AEwCw#v=onepage&q=%22Louis%20Say%22%20sucre&f=false ''De l'économie coloniale à l'économie mondialisée - Aspects multiples de la transition (XXe et XXIe siècles)''], Editions Publibook, 2011, p. 57</ref><ref name="correspondancefamiliale">[http://correspondancefamiliale.ehess.fr/index.php?2348 Say, Jean-Baptiste (1767-1832), son frère Louis, et leurs familles], [[EHESS]]</ref> His father, Jean-Etienne Say, was a Swiss-born silk trader.<ref name="evertschoolr">Schoorl, Evert. [https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=XS2x66XO4eoC&pg=PA3&dq=%22Louis+Say%22&hl=en&sa=X&ei=oeNnVcKhJaWC7gbt7oPYAQ&ved=0CCYQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&q=%22Louis%20Say%22&f=false ''Jean-Baptiste Say: Revolutionary, Entrepreneur, Economist''], New York: Routledge, 2012, p. 3</ref> His mother was Françoise Brun de Castanet.<ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/> He had a brother, [[Jean-Baptiste Say]], who later became a classical liberal economist.<ref name="marcpenouil"/><ref name="deleconomiecoloniale"/>

His paternal family were Protestants from [[Nîmes]] who were exiled in [[Geneva]], Switzerland after the repeal of the [[Edict of Fontainebleau|Revocation of the Edict of Nantes]] in 1685.<ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/> His paternal great-grandfather, also named Louis Say, moved first to [[Amsterdam]], where he was a member of the [[Walloon Church, Amsterdam|Walloon Church]], before settling in Geneva in 1694.<ref name="evertschoolr"/> His paternal grandfather, Jean Say, became a Swiss citizen.<ref name="evertschoolr"/>

==Business career==
Say began his career as a broker in Paris.<ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/> He then moved to [[Abbeville]], where he worked in the [[Calico (textile)|calico]]-whitening industry.<ref name="marcpenouil"/><ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/>

In 1813, Say asked [[Jules Paul Benjamin Delessert]] to recommend his cousin, Armand Delessert, who was the owner of a [[beetroot]] [[sugar refinery]] in [[Nantes]].<ref name="deleconomiecoloniale"/> Shortly after, Say moved to Nantes and took over the refinery.<ref name="marcpenouil"/><ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/> After the government changed the law on tariffs in 1814,<ref name="marcpenouil"/> Say switched to using [[sugarcane]] in 1815.<ref name="deleconomiecoloniale"/> He later let his son Horace taken over the refinery.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> The company was known as Louis Say et Cie, later known as [[Béghin-Say]], now a subsidiary of [[Tereos]].<ref name="lonelyplanetloire">Williams, Nicola and Booner, Virginie. [https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=PASIo7gR7Q4C&pg=PA30&lpg=PA30&dq=%22Louis+Say%22+sugar&source=bl&ots=KKp8YEUjP-&sig=LqTJdNl5_HH3Rz6RVhxsW_FV_b4&hl=en&sa=X&ei=o1hpVd6XNcmBU9-BgagB&ved=0CC0Q6AEwAw#v=onepage&q=%22Louis%20Say%22%20sugar&f=false ''The Loire''], Lonely Planet, 2002, p. 30</ref>

In 1832, with Constant Duméril, Say also opened a beetroot sugar factory in [[Ivry-sur-Seine]], known as the "Raffinerie de Jamaïque" (English: "refinery from Jamaica").<ref name="emergencemichael">Michael Stephen Smith, [https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=zs26hd5keYkC&pg=PA276&lpg=PA276&dq=%22Louis+Say%22+sugar&source=bl&ots=pqKlW0Y0Uq&sig=MZ7-UwHyPtaB1LxZQcF73z2ID3M&hl=en&sa=X&ei=o1hpVd6XNcmBU9-BgagB&ved=0CCcQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&q=%22Louis%20Say%22%20sugar&f=false ''The Emergence of Modern Business Enterprise in France, 1800-1930''], Cambridge, Massachusetts: Harvard University Press, 2006, p. 276</ref>

==Writings on economics==
Partly in reaction to his brother, Say became an economist at the age of forty-four, after he had become wealthy.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> For historian Marc Penouil, he was an "amateur" in this field.<ref name="marcpenouil"/>

Say joined the Société Académique de Loire-Inférieure.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> He also met [[David Ricardo]] in England.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> He wrote four books about political economy between 1818 and 1836.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> He disagreed with his brother, Jean-Baptiste, about classical liberalism.<ref name="christophebormans">Bormans, Christophe. [https://books.google.co.uk/books?id=F60c1Dvz0B4C&pg=PA40&dq=Louis+Auguste+Say&hl=en&sa=X&ei=hONnVbXdF-LY7AbI54G4Dg&ved=0CEwQ6AEwBg#v=onepage&q=Louis%20Auguste%20Say&f=false ''L'indispensable de la pensée économique''], Levallois-Perret: Studyrama, 2002, p. 40</ref> Say focused on the relationship between worth and usefulness.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> He drew distinctions between national wealth and individual wealth.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> Contrary to classical liberals, he was in favour of tariffs as a way to encourage production.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> However, he was vehemently opposed to taxes.<ref name="marcpenouil"/>

==Personal life and death==
Say married Constance Maressal in 1809.<ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/> They had four sons: Gustave, Achille, Constant, and Louis Octave Say.<ref name="correspondancefamiliale"/>

Say died on 6 May 1840 in Paris.<ref name="marcpenouil"/><ref name="deleconomiecoloniale"/> He was sixty-six years old.<ref name="marcpenouil"/>

==Legacy==
Say's nephew, Horace Say, became a classical liberal economist.<ref name="marcpenouil"/> His grandnephew, [[Léon Say]], served as the French Minister of Finance from 1872 to 1873, 1875 to 1877, 1877 to 1879, and again in 1882. His granddaughter, Princess [[Marie Say]] married first [[Henri Amédée de Broglie]], and later [[Luís Fernando de Orleans y Borbón]].
