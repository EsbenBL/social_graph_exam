'''Ronald Dietrich Asmus''' (June 29, 1957 – April 30, 2011)<ref>[https://www.nytimes.com/2011/05/04/world/europe/04asmus.html Obituary of Ronald Asmus, New York Times]</ref> was a [[United States]] diplomat and political analyst. He, as U.S. Deputy [[Assistant Secretary of State]] for European Affairs (1997–2000), was instrumental in the expansion of [[NATO]] to include former members of the [[Eastern bloc]] and acted as a leading policy designer in the U.S.–Europe relations.<ref name="rferl">{{cite news|title=Ron Asmus, R.I.P.|url=http://www.rferl.org/content/ron_asmus/24090068.html|accessdate=3 May 2011|newspaper=Radio Free Europe/Radio Liberty|date=May 3, 2011}}</ref><ref name="state">{{cite news|last=Clinton|first=Hillary Rodham|title=Passing of Ronald Asmus|url=https://www.state.gov/secretary/rm/2011/05/162451.htm|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110505140119/http://www.state.gov/secretary/rm/2011/05/162451.htm|url-status=dead|archive-date=May 5, 2011|accessdate=3 May 2011|newspaper=U.S. State Department|date=May 3, 2011}}</ref>

==Biography==
Asmus was born to a family of [[Germany|German]] immigrants who came to [[Milwaukee, Wisconsin]] after [[World War II]]. He grew up in Milwaukee and [[Mequon, Wisconsin]] graduating from [[Homestead High School (Mequon, Wisconsin)|Homestead High School]].<ref>[http://www.jsonline.com/news/obituaries/121064148.html Ronald Asmus, Milwaukee Sentinel Journal]</ref> Asmus was an early and strong proponent of expanding NATO into [[Eastern Europe]]. He was U.S. Deputy Assistant Secretary of State for European Affairs in the [[Clinton Administration]] from 1997 to 2000. He played an important role in the [[1999 Washington summit]] of NATO, when Poland, the Czech Republic and Hungary joined the Alliance.<ref name="state"/> He was also a senior analyst and fellow at [[Radio Free Europe]], [[RAND]] and the [[Council on Foreign Relations]]. For his diplomatic work, Asmus was decorated by the US Department of State and the governments of Estonia, Georgia, Italy, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland, and Sweden.<ref name="rferl"/><ref name="GMF">{{cite web|title=Ronald D. Asmus|url=http://www.americanacademy.de/home/distinguished-visitors/current-visitors/person///ronald_d_asmus/414/detail/|work=Lecturer Class of Spring 2010|publisher=The American Academy|accessdate=3 May 2011}}</ref>

Asmus held a PhD in European Studies, a master's degree in Soviet and East European studies from the [[Paul H. Nitze School of Advanced International Studies]] of the [[Johns Hopkins University]], and a BA in political science from the [[University of Wisconsin&ndash;Madison]]. He was the executive director of the Brussels-based Transatlantic Center and was also responsible for strategic planning at the [[German Marshall Fund]] of the United States.<ref name="GMF"/>

Asmus authored ''Opening NATO's Door: How the Alliance Remade Itself for a New Era'' (Columbia, 2002), about the push to open NATO to Eastern European countries, and ''A Little War that Shook the World'' (Palgrave Macmillan, January 2010), about the [[2008 South Ossetia war|conflict]] between [[Russia]] and [[Georgia (country)|Georgia]] in 2008. Being one of the most persistent advocates for the integration of Georgia into the European Union and NATO, Asmus viewed the conflict in terms of a larger Russia–West relations and argued that it was Georgian independence, and its Westward orientation, which angered Russia and set the groundwork for war.<ref name="rferl"/>

Asmus died of a lung cancer-related illness in Brussels on April 30, 2011. The U.S. Department of State,<ref name="state"/> governments of the [[Baltic states]]<ref>{{cite news|title=Baltics mourn Ronald Asmus|url=http://www.baltictimes.com/news/articles/28592/|accessdate=3 May 2011|newspaper=The Baltic Times|date=May 3, 2011}}</ref> and Georgia<ref>{{cite web|title=Traditional briefing of the Spokesperson for the President of Georgia|url=http://www.president.gov.ge/index.php?lang_id=ENG&sec_id=227&info_id=6373|publisher=Administration of the President of Georgia|accessdate=3 May 2011}}</ref> expressed their condolences over the death of Asmus.
