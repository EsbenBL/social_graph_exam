'''George Andrew Lundberg''' (October 3, 1895 – April 14, 1966) was an American [[sociologist]].<ref>{{citation|contribution=Lundberg, George. A. (1895–1966)|first=Martyn|last=Hammersley|title=Blackwell Encyclopedia of Sociology|doi= 10.1111/b.9781405124331.2007.x|editor-last=Ritzer|editor-first=George|publisher=Blackwell Publishing|year=2007|hdl=10138/224218|isbn=9781405124331|hdl-access=free}}.</ref>

==Background==
Lundberg was born in [[Fairdale, North Dakota]].  His parents, Andrew J. Lundberg and Britta C. Erickson, were immigrants from [[Sweden]]. Lundberg received his bachelor's degree from the [[University of North Dakota]] in 1920, a master's degree from the [[University of Wisconsin]] in 1922, and a doctorate in 1925 from the [[University of Minnesota]],<ref>{{cite web
|url= http://www.asanet.org/about/presidents/George_Lundberg.cfm|title= George Andrew Lundberg |publisher= The American Sociologist (pp 212–213).
|author= Robert E. L. Faris |date= August 1966 |access-date= May 1, 2016}}</ref> where he studied under {{ill|Luther Lee Bernard|de}} and [[F. Stuart Chapin]].

==Career==
Following his doctorate, he began a faculty position at the [[University of Washington]], but left after a year for postdoctoral studies at [[Columbia University]], and then took a position as an associate professor at the [[University of Pittsburgh]]. In 1930, he became director of the Bureau of Social Research at the Pittsburgh Federation of Social Agencies, but he soon left Pittsburgh for a faculty position at Columbia. In 1934 he worked with the [[Federal Emergency Relief Administration]], and soon thereafter moved to [[Bennington College]] in [[Vermont]], where he was professor of sociology and [[statistics]]. After holding additional faculty positions at the University of Minnesota, [[Brigham Young University]], and [[Stanford University]], he joined the [[University of Washington]] in 1945 as professor and chair, and remained there for the rest of his career.<ref name="christopher">{{citation|title=In Memoriam: George A. Lundberg|first=S. C.|last=Christopher|journal=International Journal of Comparative Sociology|volume=8|issue=1|year=1967|pages=118–120|doi=10.1177/002071526700800108|s2cid=144683885}}.</ref>

Lundberg served at the 33rd President of the [[American Sociological Association|American Sociological Society]].<ref name="christopher"/><ref>{{citation|title=Dr. Lundberg Elected Sociological President|journal=[[Christian Science Monitor]]|date=October 5, 1942}}.</ref><ref>[http://www.asanet.org/cs/root/leftnav/governance/past_officers/presidents/george_a_lundberg Biography] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090108090152/http://www.asanet.org/cs/root/leftnav/governance/past_officers/presidents/george_a_lundberg |date=2009-01-08 }} at American Sociological Association web site, retrieved 2009-07-19.</ref> He was also president of the [[Pacific Sociological Association]],<ref>[http://www.pacificsoc.org/2005/06/past_officers_e.html Past officers and editors] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080519221742/http://www.pacificsoc.org/2005/06/past_officers_e.html |date=2008-05-19 }}, Pacific Sociological Association, retrieved 2009-07-19.</ref> the [[Eastern Sociological Society]], and the [[Sociological Research Association]], and was the editor of the journal ''Sociometry'' from 1941 to 1947.<ref name="christopher"/>

Lundberg died on April 14, 1966, in [[Seattle, Washington]], "following surgical treatment for a condition not ordinarily considered dangerous".<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Faris|first1=Robert E. L.|title=George Andrew Lindberg|journal=The American Sociologist|date=August 1966|pages=212–213|issn=0003-1232}}</ref>

==Awards and honors==
Lundberg was a [[Fellow]] of the [[American Association for the Advancement of Science]], and was awarded the Distinguished Achievement Medal of the University of Minnesota and an honorary doctorate in 1958 from the University of North Dakota.<ref name="christopher"/> After his death, a conference of the Pacific Sociological Association was held in his honor.<ref>{{citation|title=The Behavioral sciences: essays in honor of George A. Lundberg|editor-first=Alfred|editor-last=De Grazia|publisher=Behavioral Research Council|year=1968}}. Reviewed by Joseph S. Roucek (1969), [https://www.jstor.org/stable/446367 ''The Western Political Quarterly'' '''22'''(3): 680], and by [[Peter Abell]] (1970), [https://www.jstor.org/stable/588285 ''The British Journal of Sociology'' '''21'''(1): 118].</ref>

==Contributions==
Lundberg's most lasting impression was made in his work entitled, ''Can Science Save Us?''. However, Lundberg focused much of his research on the applications, limits, [[delimit]]s, [[operational definition]]s, and [[linguistics]].<ref>Don Martindale, The Nature and Types of Sociological Theory, 1981, pg.127</ref> Lundberg's approach to sociology is usually categorized as  [[neo-positivism]]. Lundberg was critical of the [[Chicago School (sociology)|Chicago School of sociology]].<ref>{{Cite book|title = The Dilemma of Qualitative Method: Herbert Blumer and the Chicago Tradition|last = Hammersley|first = Martyn|publisher = Routledge|year = 1989|location = London|pages = Ch.4}}</ref> He felt that their methodologies were not precise enough to generate reliable results.<ref>Warshay, Lee. Methodological Issues: Science (Empiricism and Neo-Positivism) and Humanism (Subjective and Objective idealism). Manuscript. Wayne State University, Detroit.</ref>
