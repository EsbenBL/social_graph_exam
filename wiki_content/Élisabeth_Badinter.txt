'''Élisabeth Badinter''' (née '''Bleustein-Blanchet'''; 5 March 1944, [[Boulogne-Billancourt]])<ref name="Curr Bio">{{cite book|title=Current Biography Yearbook 2011|year=2011|publisher=H.W. Wilson|location=Ipswich, MA|isbn=9780824211219|pages=[https://archive.org/details/currentbiography0000unse_z0h6/page/33 33–36]|chapter=Badinter, Elisabeth|chapter-url=https://archive.org/details/currentbiography0000unse_z0h6/page/33}}</ref> is a French philosopher, author and historian.

She is best known for her philosophical treatises on feminism and women's role in society. She is an advocate of [[liberal feminism]] and women migrant workers' rights in France. Badinter is described as having a commitment to Enlightenment rationalism and universalism.<ref name=":0" /> She advocates for a "moderate feminism".<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|title=Elizabeth Badinter|url=https://www.jewishvirtuallibrary.org/elizabeth-badinter|access-date=2020-12-16|website=www.jewishvirtuallibrary.org}}</ref> A 2010 [[Marianne (magazine)|''Marianne'' news magazine]] poll named her France's "most influential intellectual", primarily on the basis of her books on women's rights and motherhood.<ref>{{cite magazine|last=Kramer|first=Jane|url=http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2011/07/25/110725fa_fact_kramer|title=Against Nature: Elisabeth Badinter's contrarian feminism|magazine=The New Yorker|date=25 July 2011|authorlink=Jane Kramer}}</ref> 

Badinter is the largest shareholder of [[Publicis]] Groupe, a multinational advertising and public relations company, and the chairwoman of its [[supervisory board]].<ref name=":1" /><ref>[http://www.metronews.fr/info/lutter-contre-le-voile-et-etre-chargee-de-la-com-de-l-arabie-saoudite-le-troublant-melange-des-genres-d-elisabeth-badinter/mpde!vpd3bjn54L5uA Lutter contre le voile et… être chargée de la com’ de l’Arabie Saoudite: le troublant mélange des genres d’Elisabeth Badinter], ''Metronews'', 5 April 2016.</ref> She received these shares in an inheritance from her father, [[Marcel Bleustein-Blanchet]], who founded the company. <ref>https://www.forbes.com/profile/elisabeth-badinter/#4f911b0250b7</ref> According to ''[[Forbes]]'', she is one of the wealthiest French citizens with a fortune of around US$1.8&nbsp;billion in 2012.<ref>  [https://www.forbes.com/billionaires/list/#tab:overall_country:France The World's Billionaires List], ''Forbes'', March 2012.</ref>

==Early life==
Badinter was born in [[Boulogne-Billancourt]] to Sophie Vaillant and [[Marcel Bleustein-Blanchet]], founder of [[Publicis Groupe|Publicis]].<ref>{{Cite news|last=Jr|first=Robert Mcg Thomas|date=1996-04-13|title=Marcel Bleustein-Blanchet Dies; Paris Advertising Giant Was 89 (Published 1996)|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1996/04/13/world/marcel-bleustein-blanchet-dies-paris-advertising-giant-was-89.html|access-date=2020-12-16|issn=0362-4331}}</ref> Sophie Vaillant was the granddaughter of [[Édouard Vaillant]], a French political leader and social activist. Elizabeth’s mother was raised as a Roman Catholic in a middle class upbringing, and later converted to Judaism following her marriage. She raised Élisabeth in the Jewish faith.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Elisabeth Badinter: The Celebrity French Intellectual Every Jew (And Feminist) Should Know|url=http://jewishweek.timesofisrael.com/elisabeth-badinter-the-celebrity-french-intellectual-every-jew-and-feminist-should-know/|access-date=2020-12-16|website=jewishweek.timesofisrael.com|language=en-US}}</ref>

Elisabeth and her two sisters were raised by parents who believed in the equality of the sexes.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=https://jwa.org/encyclopedia/article/badinter-elisabeth|title=Elisabeth Badinter profile|publisher=jwa.org|accessdate=5 February 2017}}</ref> She received her secondary education from ''L'école alsacienne'', a private school in Paris. During adolescence, Badinter read [[Simone de Beauvoir]]'s [[the Second Sex]], which profoundly influenced her views, inspiring her pursuit of a doctorate in philosophy at [[University of Paris|Sorbonne University]]. She is a specialist in French history of the [[Age of Enlightenment]].<ref>{{Cite magazine|url=http://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2011/07/25/against-nature|title=Against Nature|magazine=The New Yorker|date=17 July 2011|accessdate=5 February 2017}}</ref>

==Career==
After her studies, Badinter taught at the [[École Polytechnique]].<ref>{{cite news| url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2010/feb/12/france-feminism-elisabeth-badinter | location=London | work=The Guardian | first=Lizzy | last=Davies | title=French philosopher says feminism under threat from 'good motherhood' | date=12 February 2010}}</ref> Her first book titled, ''L'Amour en plus,'' was published in 1980 and raises the question of whether maternal love is an exclusively natural instinct or a tendency reinforced in the cultural context, in which the behaviour of motherly affection is expected.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.lemonde.fr/idees/article/2016/06/19/elisabeth-badinter-la-griffe-de-la-republique_4953637_3232.html|title=Elisabeth Badinter, la griffe de la République|last=Renterghem|first=Marion Van|date=19 June 2016|newspaper=Le Monde.fr|access-date=9 February 2017|language=fr|issn=1950-6244}}</ref>

In her critical work, ''L'un est l'autre'', published in 1987, Badinter reflects upon the complementarities of masculine and feminine traits in gendered identities and the conflicts that arise when these complementarities are subjected to oppression. Badinter concludes that a new era of gendered resemblances will lead to a change in gender identities and a revolution of moral values.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Collard|first=Chantal|year=1987|title="Anthropologie comme mythe d'origine du rapport entre les sexes." Compte Rendu: Elisabeth BADINTER : L'un est l'autre. Des relations entre hommes et femmes, Odile Jacob, Paris, 1986, 367 p.|url=https://www.erudit.org/revue/as/1987/v11/n1/006395ar.pdf|journal=Anthropologie et Sociétés|volume=11 N.1|pages=161–167|publisher=erudit.org|doi=10.7202/006395ar|doi-access=free}}</ref>

Her 2003 treatise, ''La fausse route'', addresses misandry and victimisation of women by French contemporary feminists. "The systematic denial of women's power and violence, the constant portrayal of women as oppressed and therefore innocent is deepening the crevasses of a divided humanity: the victims of masculine oppression on one side and the almighty executioners on the other. "<ref>{{Cite book|title=Fausse route|last=Badinter|first=Elisabeth|publisher=Editions Odile Jacob|year=|isbn=|location=|pages=113|quote=|via=}}</ref> She criticizes [[Gender essentialism|essentialists]] within the feminist movement, arguing that, "Man is not the enemy."<ref name=":0" /> 

==Political activism==
During the 1989 [[Islamic scarf controversy in France|Islamic scarf controversy]] in France, Badinter, [[Régis Debray]], [[Alain Finkielkraut]], Elisabeth de Fontenay and Catherine Kintzler wrote an open letter to the then Minister of Education, [[Lionel Jospin]], demanding to not let students who refuse to take off their headscarves go to school.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://www.crif.org/fr/revuedepresse/elisabeth-badinter-la-griffe-de-la-republique|title=Elisabeth Badinter, la griffe de la République|date=20 June 2016|newspaper=Crif – Conseil Représentatif des Institutions Juives de France|accessdate=9 February 2017|language=fr}}</ref> Badinter believes that the French public education system should be free of any religious affiliation and that neutrality in public institutions of a secularist state must prevail over expressions of individuality in them.<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/world/2004/feb/01/france.schoolsworldwide|title=France divided as headscarf ban is set to become law|last=Smith|first=Alex Duval|date=1 February 2004|newspaper=The Guardian|accessdate=9 February 2017|issn=0261-3077}}</ref> In an interview with the [[Financial Times]], she says, "The religions of the Book have always fought against what would liberate women or facilitate their lives ... We talk about Muslim radicals all the time but it’s more general."<ref>{{Cite web|last=Chassany|first=Anne-Sylvaine|date=2013-12-27|title=Lunch with the FT: Élisabeth Badinter|url=https://www.ft.com/content/2697285e-67e7-11e3-a905-00144feabdc0|url-status=live|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170130044937/https://www.ft.com/content/2697285e-67e7-11e3-a905-00144feabdc0|archive-date=2017-01-30|access-date=2020-12-16|website=www.ft.com|language=en-GB}}</ref>

==Personal life==
In 1966, she married lawyer [[Robert Badinter]], who became [[Minister of Justice (France)|Minister of Justice]] under Mitterrand.<ref name=":0" /> Élisabeth and Robert Badinter have one daughter and two sons.
