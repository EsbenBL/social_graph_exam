'''Bethany Lee Albertson''' is an American Political psychologist. She is an [[Associate professor]] of political science at the [[University of Texas at Austin]]. Her co-authored book ''Anxious Politics: Democratic Citizenship in a Threatening World'' received the Robert E. Lane Award for being the best book in political psychology published in 2015.

==Education==
Albertson attended [[Loyola Marymount University]] for her [[Bachelor of Arts]] degree in 1999, earning a Hansard Fellowship.<ref>{{cite web |title=Hansard Fellowship Recipients |url=https://bellarmine.lmu.edu/ils/activities/hansardfellowshiprecipients/ |website=bellarmine.lmu.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020}}</ref> She moved to Chicago to earn her [[Master's degree]] and PhD at the [[University of Chicago]].<ref>{{cite web |title=Bethany Albertson |url=https://sites.utexas.edu/balberts/ |website=sites.utexas.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020}}</ref>
 
==Career==
Albertson joined the department of political science at the [[University of Washington]] as an [[assistant professor]] in 2006.<ref>{{cite web |title=DEPARTMENT OF POLITICAL SCIENCE NEW APPOINTMENTS |url=https://www.washington.edu/regents/meetings/meetings06/september/items/acad/a-1.pdf |website=washington.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |page=5 |date=September 21, 2006}}</ref> During her tenure at the school, she taught courses in American politics, voting and elections, and political psychology.<ref>{{cite web |title=WISER Affiliated Faculty |url=https://depts.washington.edu/uwiser/faculty.shtml |website=depts.washington.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020}}</ref> She also analyzed data prior to the [[2008 United States presidential election]] of [[Barack Obama]] with psychology professor [[Anthony Greenwald]]. Together, they found that data from the Implicit Association Test was not a consistent or reliable representation of Democratic voters.<ref>{{cite web |title=Study suggests polls may overestimate support for Obama, underestimate backing for Clinton among Democrats |url=https://www.washington.edu/news/2007/12/18/study-suggests-polls-may-overestimate-support-for-obama-underestimate-backing-for-clinton-among-democrats/ |website=washington.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=December 18, 2007}}</ref> Albertson spent three years at the University of Washington before moving to Texas to live with her recently tenured husband, Josh, and their son.<ref name = "chronicle">{{cite web |last1=Albertson |first1=Bethany |title=Operation Keep My Job |url=https://www.chronicle.com/article/Operation-Keep-My-Job/235535 |website=chronicle.com |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=March 2, 2016}}</ref>

Albertson joined the faculty at the [[University of Texas at Austin]] as an assistant professor<ref>{{cite web |title=The Burdine Chronicles |url=https://liberalarts.utexas.edu/government/_files/burdine-chronicles/BC_April2010.pdf |website=liberalarts.utexas.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |page=6 |date=April 2010}}</ref> but was worried about her chances at tenure during her 3rd year review.<ref name = "chronicle"/> In 2014, Albertson received the Josefina Paredes Endowed Teaching Award<ref>{{cite web |title=Liberal Arts Faculty Receive Teaching Awards, Fellowships |url=https://liberalarts.utexas.edu/public-affairs/news/liberal-arts-faculty-receive-teaching-awards-fellowships |website=liberalarts.utexas.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=April 18, 2014}}</ref> and the Society for Political Methodology's Harold F. Gosnell Prize.<ref>{{cite web |title=Bethany Albertson Wins Methodology Award |url=https://liberalarts.utexas.edu/government/news/bethany-albertson-wins-methodology-award |website=liberalarts.utexas.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=June 9, 2014}}</ref> The following year, she co-published a book with [[Shana Kushner Gadarian]] titled ''Anxious Politics: Democratic Citizenship in a Threatening World,'' which detailed how anxiety can influence political elections. They found that when a threat is present, citizens tend to rely on government officials as "experts" and vote towards bills that are focused on protecting against said threat.<ref>{{cite journal |last1=Clifford |first1=Scott |title=Review of Anxious Politics: Democratic Citizenship in a Threatening World |journal=The Journal of Politics |date=2016 |volume=78 |issue=4 |doi=10.1086/688312 |s2cid=157823403 |url=https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/688312?mobileUi=0 |accessdate=June 7, 2020}}</ref> Beyond acts of terrorism, they also examined how the [[2009 swine flu pandemic]] and the fictional smallpox outbreak, which they later re-examined in a modern context during the [[COVID-19 pandemic]].<ref>{{cite web |last1=Coaston |first1=Jane |title=How anxiety changes political behavior |url=https://www.vox.com/2020/4/28/21231869/coronavirus-anxiety-politics-partisanship-research |website=vox.com |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=April 28, 2020}}</ref> Their book received the Robert E. Lane Award for being the best book in political psychology published in 2015.<ref>{{cite web |title=Bethany Albertson's book selected for Robert E. Lane Award |url=https://liberalarts.utexas.edu/government/news/bethany-albertson-s-book-selected-for-robert-e-lane-award |website=liberalarts.utexas.edu |accessdate=June 7, 2020 |date=June 8, 2016}}</ref> She was eventually promoted to [[Associate professor]] and awarded tenure in 2016.<ref name = "chronicle"/>
