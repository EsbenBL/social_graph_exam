'''Charles Wolf Jr.''' (August 1, 1924 – October 24, 2016) was a senior economic advisor at the [[RAND Corporation]] (June 23, 1955 – October 24, 2016). He was also a senior research fellow at the [[Hoover Institution]] at [[Stanford University]] and was on the advisory board of the Center for International Business and Economic Research at the [[UCLA Anderson School of Management]]. He was a board member of Capital Income Builder and of Capital World Growth and Income, Inc., and a member of the [[American Economic Association]], the [[Econometric Society]], the [[Council on Foreign Relations]], and the [[International Institute for Strategic Studies]] in London.<ref>[http://triblive.com/opinion/pagebooks/8639116-74/wolf-essays-china#axzz3fKrb97Wi "An Economist Looks in the Mirror."] TribLive. July 4, 2015</ref> He is noted for predicting the economic collapse of the [[Soviet Union]] in the 1980s.<ref name="nyt">{{cite news|last1=Roberts|first1=Sam|title=Charles Wolf Jr., Who Clashed With Pentagon Papers Leaker, Dies at 92|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2016/11/11/us/charles-wolf-jr-died-pentagon-papers.html?_r=0|accessdate=13 November 2016|work=The New York Times|date=10 November 2016}}</ref>

Wolf’s research focused on how economic forces shape the international political environment and how economic instruments can advance U.S. and Western interests.<ref>[http://www.hoover.org/profiles/charles-wolf-jr Hoover: Charles Wolf, Jr.]</ref><ref>[http://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2013/06/28/business/economy-business/fragmented-or-lacking-institutions-hinder-economies-from-recovering/#.VZIR2HXIl4s The Japan Times: News]</ref> This way of thinking has been a complement to the political and military analyses that typically dominate these discussions. His research also contributed to the respective competencies of market forces and governments.<ref>[http://www.weeklystandard.com/blogs/supply-side-foreign-policy_541156.html Supply-Side Foreign Policy] The Weekly Standard. February 7, 2011.</ref>

==Career==
Wolf served as a Foreign Service Officer with the [[United States Department of State]] (1945–1947, 1949–1953). In the early 1950s, he was a visiting professor of economics and Asian studies at [[Cornell University]] and an assistant professor of economics and Far East studies at the [[University of California, Berkeley]]. He joined the RAND Corporation in 1955 and headed the Economics Department from 1967 to 1981. In 1965 his name appeared on a list of academics involved with [[Project Camelot]].<ref>''Behavioral Sciences and the National Security: Report No. 4, Together With Part IX of the Hearings on Winning the Cold War: The U.S. Ideological Offensive by the Subcommittee on International Organizations and Movements of the Committee on Foreign Affairs'', House of Representatives, December 6, 1965</ref> He served as founding dean of the [[Frederick S. Pardee RAND Graduate School]] from 1970 to 1997; he taught at the school until his death.<ref>[https://www.rand.org/about/people/w/wolf_jr_charles.html Charles Wolf, Jr. Profile]</ref><ref>[https://www.rand.org/content/dam/rand/pubs/corporate_pubs/CP800/CP828/RAND_CP828.pdf "Honoring the Career of Charles Wolf, Jr.] Speech at RAND. June 8, 2015.</ref> He died after going into [[cardiac arrest]] on October 24, 2016 at the [[Ronald Reagan UCLA Medical Center]] in Los Angeles.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.rand.org/news/press/2016/10/25.html|title = Charles Wolf Jr. Dies at 92. Founding Dean of RAND's Graduate School Pioneered Modern Policy Analysis}}</ref>

==Awards==
In 2007, Wolf received the [[Order of the Rising Sun]], Gold Rays with Neck Ribbon, from the government of [[Japan]]. He was recognized for helping to nurture U.S. public opinion that was favorable to Japan.<ref>[http://sitrep.globalsecurity.org/charles-wolf-jr/ Charles Wolf, Jr. Global Security]</ref>

==Education==
Wolf received BS and PhD degrees in economics from [[Harvard University]].
