'''Covington Scott Littleton''' (July 1, 1933 – November 25, 2010) was an American anthropologist who was Professor and Chair of the Department of Anthropology at [[Occidental College]]. A co-founder of the ''[[Journal of Indo-European Studies]]'', Littleton was an expert on [[Indo-European mythology]] and [[Shinto]], on which he was the author of numerous works.

==Biography==
Covington Scott Littleton was born in [[Los Angeles]], California on July 1, 1933, and grew up in [[Hermosa Beach, California]].{{sfn|''Los Angeles Times''|2010}} He was the son of Covington Scott (an investigator and writer) and Adelaide Littleton. Littleton served in the [[United States Army]] in [[Korea]] during the [[Korean War]], having lied about his age in order to get into the army.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}}

Attending [[El Camino College]] from 1952 to 1954, Littleton subsequently studied at the [[University of California, Los Angeles]], where he received his [[A.B.]] (with the highest honors) in 1957, [[M.A.]] in 1962, and [[Ph.D]] in anthropology in 1965.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}} He was the [[Commencement speech|Graduate Commencement Speaker]] at University of California, Los Angeles in 1965.{{sfn|Malcor|2011|pp=230-231}}

Littleton's 1965 PhD dissertation was published in 1966 under the title ''The New Comparative Mythology: An Anthropological Assessment of the Theories of Georges Dumézil''. He played an important role in disseminating Dumézil's theories [[Trifunctional hypothesis]] to an [[English language|English]]-speaking audience.{{sfn|Malcor|2011|pp=230-231}} Littleton's work on the theories of Dumézil is still considered the most comprehensive source on the subject.{{sfn|Miller|2011|pp=496-498}}

Littleton was a research assistant in folklore center at University of California, Los Angeles from 1961 to 1962. Since 1962, Littleton worked as [[Occidental College]] as Assistant Professor of Anthropology (1962–68), Associate Professor of Anthropology (1968–80), Chair of the Department of Sociology and Anthropology (1967–72, 1974–93), and Professor of Anthropology and Chair of the Department of Anthropology (1994–98).{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}} He continued to lecture at Occidental until 2002.{{sfn|''Los Angeles Times''|2010}} Littleton was a member of the Editorial Board of the ''[[Journal of Indo-European Studies]]'' from its inception, and served as its Mythology Editor since 2000.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}}{{sfn|Malcor|2011|pp=230-231}}

Littleton specialized in the study of religion, mythology and folklore, particularly [[Shinto]], [[King Arthur]] and [[Indo-European mythology]]. He was the author of numerous works on these subjects.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}} He also authored a personal memoir and a science fiction novel.{{sfn|''Los Angeles Times''|2010}} Altogether, Littleton authored hundreds of articles, reviews, monographs and other publications throughout his career.{{sfn|Malcor|2011|pp=230-231}}

Littleton was the member of a large number of scholarly organizations. He was a [[Fulbright Program|Fulbright Scholar]] and a member of [[Phi Beta Kappa]].{{sfn|Malcor|2011|pp=230-231}}

Littleton died of pneumonia after complications from heart surgery at [[Pasadena, California]] on November 25, 2010.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}}{{sfn|''Los Angeles Times''|2010}}

==Personal life==
Littleton married Mary Ann Wuest on 26 August 1961, with whom he had two daughters, Leslie and Cynthia. He was a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] and an avid runner, cycler, swimmer, photographer, and reader of science fiction.{{sfn|''Contemporary Authors''|2011}}
