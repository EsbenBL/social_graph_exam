'''John Milton Yinger''' (July 6, 1916 – July 28, 2011<ref>[https://oncampus.oberlin.edu/source/articles/2011/08/01/j-milton-yinger-1916-2011 ''Emeritus Sociology Professor J. Milton Yinger, 1916–2011''], Oberlin College, Office of Communications, Aug. 1, 2011</ref>) was an American [[sociologist]] who was president of the [[American Sociological Association]] 1976–1977. Yinger received his Ph.D. from the [[University of Wisconsin, Madison]], in 1942, and was Emeritus Professor of Sociology at [[Oberlin College]].<ref name="hartsem">{{cite web|url=http://hirr.hartsem.edu/ency/Yinger.htm |title=Content Pages of the Encyclopedia of Religion and Social Science |publisher=hirr.hartsem.edu |access-date=2014-10-08 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20131011222828/http://hirr.hartsem.edu/ency/Yinger.htm |archive-date=2013-10-11 }}</ref>

== Biography ==
Yinger was born in [[Quincy, Michigan]], in 1916.<ref name="google">{{cite book|title=Seventy-fifth Anniversary Record|author=John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation|date=1967|publisher=John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=jJDWAAAAMAAJ|access-date=2014-10-08}}</ref> His parents, George and Emma Bancroft Yinger, were both [[Methodist]] ministers. He grew up with five brothers and two sisters. Under the direction of his father, he and various combinations of his siblings sang in hundreds of concerts as the [[Yinger Singers]]. Yinger studied [[sociology]] at [[DePauw University]], and continued to sing with his siblings. At university, was part of the athletics track team, running flat races and [[hurdles]], at one point racing against [[Jesse Owens]]. After graduating from DePauw, Yinger received a master's degree from [[Louisiana State University]] and a doctorate from the [[University of Wisconsin]]. While he was a student at Wisconsin, he met his future bride, [[Winnie McHenry]]. There were married in 1941 and remained so for 61 years, until she died in 2002.<ref name="asa">{{Cite web |url=http://www.asanet.org/about-asa/asa-story/asa-history/past-asa-officers/past-asa-presidents/j-milton-yinger |title=J. Milton Yinger |website=American Sociological Association|date=5 June 2009 }}</ref>

=== Career ===
Yinger began his professional career at [[Ohio Wesleyan University]]. He moved to [[Oberlin College]] in 1947 and remained there until he retired in 1987. In addition to being a professor of sociology and [[anthropology]], Yinger was an author, writing 13 books and a number of journal articles. His textbook co-authored with George E. Simpson, ''Racial and Cultural Minorities'', went through five editions and won the 1959 [[Anisfield-Wolf]] award for the best scholarly work on race relations. The award was shared with [[Martin Luther King, Jr.]].<ref name="asa" /> Yinger's writing appeared in the 1960 [[American Sociological Review]], in which he originated the concept of a "contraculture".<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Yinger|first=J. Milton|date=October 1960|title=Contraculture and Subculture|journal=American Sociological Review|volume=25|issue=5|pages=625–635|doi=10.2307/2090136|jstor=2090136}}</ref> He defined this as a group whose values contain "as a primary element, a theme of conflict with the values of the total society." Yinger's work on this topic culminated with the 1982 publication of his book, ''Countercultures: The Promise and Peril of a World Turned Upside Down''.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://faculty.maxwell.syr.edu/jyinger/jmiltonyinger/books/countercultures.htm|title=John Yinger, Trustee Professor, Economics, Public Administration and International Affairs, Center for Policy Research|date=18 March 2009}}</ref> Yinger was elected president of the [[American Sociological Association]] for 1976–77. He received honorary degrees from DePauw and [[Syracuse University]] and was a [[Guggenheim Fellowship|Guggenheim]] Fellow, a Fellow of the [[National Endowment for the Humanities]], and a Fellow of [[Clare Hall, Cambridge|Clare Hall]] at [[Cambridge University]].<ref name="asa" />

== Personal life ==
Yinger had three children: Susan, John, and Nancy. He had five grandchildren and four great-grandchildren. He died in [[Oberlin, Ohio]] on July 28, 2011, with his daughter, Susan, at his side.
