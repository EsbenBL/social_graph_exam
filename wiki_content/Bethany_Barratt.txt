'''Bethany Barratt''' (born August 18, 1972) is an [[Americans|American]] [[political scientist]] and [[author]].  She writes and researches about [[human rights]]<ref name="ZarefskyBenacka2008a">{{cite book|author1=David Zarefsky|author2=Elizabeth Benacka|title=Sizing Up Rhetoric|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=uUpiAAAAMAAJ|year=2008|publisher=Waveland Press|isbn=978-1-57766-532-8}}</ref><ref>"Handbook of Canadian Foreign Policy." Reviewed by Robin S. Gendron. ''Humanities and Social Sciences Online''.</ref> and their effects on [[foreign policy]].<ref name="ZarefskyBenacka2008">{{cite book|author1=David Zarefsky|author2=Elizabeth Benacka|title=Sizing Up Rhetoric|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=uUpiAAAAMAAJ|year=2008|publisher=Waveland Press|isbn=978-1-57766-532-8|page=234}}</ref><ref>[https://www.deepdyve.com/lp/sage/book-review-review-essay-public-opinion-and-foreign-policy-through-a-onhzqSt0X3 "Book Review of Lessons from the Iraq War"]. ''DeepDyve''.</ref> She is a [[Professor]] of [[Political Science]] at [[Roosevelt University]] in Chicago, Illinois, and the director of its Joseph Loundy Human Rights Project, which engages undergraduates in comparative cross-national research on a variety of human rights challenges, especially as they occur in [[Urban area|urban settings]].{{citation needed|date=January 2018}}.<ref>[http://www.triblocal.com/schaumburg/2011/11/08/roosevelt-exhibit-highlights-false-convictions/ "Roosevelt exhibit highlights false convictions"] By Kate Thayer ''TribLocal'' reporter Nov. 8, 2011</ref>

==Career==
Barratt is the [[author]] of ''Human Rights and Foreign Aid: For Love or Money?'' (2007, [[Routledge]]),<ref>{{cite book|last=Barratt|first=Bethany|title=Human Rights and Foreign Aid: For Love or Money|year=2007|publisher=Routledge|location=London}}</ref> ''Public Opinion and International Intervention: Lessons from the [[Iraq War]]'' (coedited with Peter Furia and Richard Sobel (2011, Potomac),<ref name="Datta2014">{{cite book|author=Monti Narayan Datta|title=Anti-Americanism and the Rise of World Opinion: Consequences for the US National Interest|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=pDUZAwAAQBAJ&pg=PA117|date=3 April 2014|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=978-1-107-03232-3|pages=117–}}</ref> ''The Politics of [[Harry Potter]]'', (2011, [[Palgrave Macmillan|Palgrave]]),<ref name="HantkeMonnet2015">{{cite book|author1=Steffen Hantke|author2=Agnieszka Soltysik Monnet|title=War Gothic in Literature and Culture|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Tqk0CwAAQBAJ&pg=PA227|date=7 December 2015|publisher=Routledge|isbn=978-1-317-38324-6|pages=227–}}</ref> and ''Human Rights In The [[Post-9/11 World]]: A Sourcebook'',(2013, [[Open Society Foundations|Open Society Foundation]]),<ref>{{cite book|last=Barratt|first=Bethany|title=Human Rights in the Post-9/11 World: A Sourcebook|year=2013|publisher=Open Society Foundation|location=New York}}</ref> as well as articles in several [[peer-reviewed journal]]s.

In 2011, Barratt was the co-organizer of a [[photography|photographic]] exhibit at [[Roosevelt University]] entitled "The Innocents: Headshots", highlighting 45 cases of [[wrongful conviction]]s.<ref>[http://www.dailyherald.com/article/20111113/news/711139859/ "Roosevelt exhibit spotlights wrongful convictions "]. ''Daily Herald''.</ref> Barratt is currently{{when|date=January 2018}} the director of the Joseph Loundy Human Rights Project.
