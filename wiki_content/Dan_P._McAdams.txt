'''Dan P. McAdams''' (born February 7, 1954) is a professor in the Department of Psychology at [[Northwestern University]].<ref name="DansPage">{{cite web|url=http://www.wcas.northwestern.edu/psych/people/faculty/faculty_individual_pages/McAdams.htm |title=Faculty Profiles|publisher=Department of Psychology, Northwestern University |accessdate=January 22, 2013}}</ref>

He was raised in [[Gary, Indiana]], where he attended nearby [[Valparaiso University]]. In 1979 he was awarded a  Ph.D. from the [[Harvard Department of Social Relations]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://groups.northwestern.edu/upa/faculty.html |title=faculty profiles |publisher=UNDERGRAD PSYCH ASSOC |accessdate=January 26, 2013 |url-status=dead |archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20130102110821/http://groups.northwestern.edu/upa/faculty.html |archivedate=January 2, 2013 }}</ref>

McAdams is the author of ''The Person: An Introduction to the Science of Personality Psychology'',<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.amazon.com/Dan-P.-McAdams/e/B001IO9RC4 |title=Dan P. McAdams Author Page|publisher=Amazon.com |accessdate=January 22, 2013}}</ref> a classroom textbook.<ref name="hevern" /> He co-edited, with [[Amia Lieblich]] and [[Ruthellen Josselson]], the eleven-book series "The Narrative Study of Lives".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sesp.northwestern.edu/foley/ |title=Foley Center|publisher=Foley Center, Northwestern University |accessdate=January 22, 2013}}</ref> He is a member of ''The Human Capital and Economic Opportunity Working Group'' at the [[Becker Friedman Institute for Research in Economics]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://bfi.uchicago.edu/humcap/members/mcadams_d.shtml |title=The Human Capital and Economic Opportunity Working Group|publisher=Becker Friedman Institute |accessdate=January 22, 2013}}</ref>

==Three Levels of Personality==
His three level model of personality
<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.sesp.northwestern.edu/docs/publications/557464623490a3fc35faeb.pdf |title=What Do We Know When We Know a Person? 
|publisher=Journal of Personality, 1995 |accessdate=January 22, 2013}}</ref> was used in Jonathan Haidt's ''[[The Happiness Hypothesis]]''<ref>{{cite book|title=The Happiness Hypothesis: Finding Modern Truth in Ancient Wisdom|pages=142}}</ref> The three levels are :
#  Dispositional traits, a person's general tendencies. For example, the [[Big Five personality traits]] lists: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism.
#  Characteristic adaptations, a person's desires, beliefs, concerns, and coping mechanisms.
#  Life stories, the stories that give a life a sense of unity, meaning, and purpose. This is known as [[Narrative identity]].
