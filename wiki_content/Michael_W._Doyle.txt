'''Michael W. Doyle''' is an American [[international relations]] scholar who is a theorist of the liberal "[[Democratic peace theory|democratic peace]]" and author of ''Liberalism and World Politics''.<ref>{{cite journal|doi=10.2307/1960861|first=Michael W.|last=Doyle|title=Liberalism and World Politics|journal=The American Political Science Review|volume=80|issue=4|date=Dec 1986|pages=1151–1169|jstor=1960861}}</ref>  He has also written on the comparative history of empires and the evaluation of UN peace-keeping. He is a [[University Professor (Columbia)|University professor]] of International Affairs, Law and Political Science at [[Columbia University - School of International and Public Affairs]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=Michael W. Doyle {{!}} Columbia SIPA|url=https://sipa.columbia.edu/faculty-research/faculty-directory/michael-doyle|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=|website=}}</ref> He is the former director of Columbia Global Policy Initiative.  He co-directs the Center on Global Governance at [[Columbia Law School]].<ref>{{Cite web|last=|first=|date=|title=Center's Team {{!}} Center on Global Governance|url=https://global-governance.law.columbia.edu/content/centers-team|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=|website=}}</ref>

==Early life==
Michael W. Doyle was born in [[Honolulu, HI]] and graduated from [[Jesuit High School (Tampa)|Jesuit High School]] in [[Tampa, FL]]<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.aapss.org/academy-fellows/fellows-a-z/michael-doyle-1 |title=Archived copy |access-date=2017-03-25 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151006215902/http://www.aapss.org/academy-fellows/fellows-a-z/michael-doyle-1 |archive-date=2015-10-06 }}</ref> He earned his AB, AM, and PhD in political science, all from [[Harvard University]].<ref>{{Cite web|date=2016-08-08|title=Michael Doyle|url=https://www.aapss.org/fellow/michael-doyle/|access-date=2020-07-28|website=AAPSS|language=en-US}}</ref>

==Career==
Doyle has taught at the [[University of Warwick]], Johns Hopkins University, [[Princeton University]], and Yale Law School.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Michael W. Doyle|url=https://www.weforum.org/people/michael-w-doyle/|access-date=2020-07-28|website=World Economic Forum|language=en}}</ref>  At Princeton University, he directed the [[Center of International Studies]] and chaired the Editorial Board and the Committee of Editors of ''[[World Politics]]''.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Michael W. Doyle {{!}} Princeton Politics|url=https://politics.princeton.edu/people/michael-w-doyle|access-date=2020-07-28|website=politics.princeton.edu}}</ref> He has long been a member and is the former chair of the board of the International Peace Institute.  He was also a member of the External Research Advisory Committee of the UNHCR and the Advisory Committee of the Lessons-Learned Unit of the Department of Peace-Keeping Operations (UN).  He is a member of [[Council of Foreign Relations]], New York.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Council on Foreign Relations|url=https://www.cfr.org/membership/roster|access-date=2020-07-28|website=Council on Foreign Relations|language=en}}</ref>

===Kant's Perpetual Peace===
In his 1983 essay ''Kant, Liberal Legacies and Foreign Affairs'',<ref>{{cite journal|first=Michael W.|url=http://www.politics.ubc.ca/fileadmin/user_upload/poli_sci/Faculty/price/Debating_the_Democratic_Peace__Doyle.pdf|last=Doyle|title=Kant, Liberal Legacies and Foreign Affairs|series=I|journal=Philosophy and Public Affairs|year=1983|issue=12|pages=205–235, 323–353|volume=and II|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140216082244/http://www.politics.ubc.ca/fileadmin/user_upload/poli_sci/Faculty/price/Debating_the_Democratic_Peace__Doyle.pdf|archive-date=2014-02-16}}</ref> Doyle builds on [[Immanuel Kant]]'s views on various issues; especially noted are his views on [[liberal internationalism]].  Doyle discusses the two legacies of [[modern liberalism]]: the pacification of [[International relations|foreign relations]] among liberal states (see below) and international imprudence.

===Awards and honors===
In 2001, Doyle was elected a fellow of the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]]<ref>{{Cite web|title=Michael W. Doyle|url=https://www.amacad.org/person/michael-w-doyle|access-date=2020-07-28|website=American Academy of Arts & Sciences|language=en}}</ref> and, in 2009, to the [[American Philosophical Society]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=APS Member History|url=https://search.amphilsoc.org/memhist/search?creator=michael%20doyle;smode=advanced;f1-mem=Resident|access-date=2020-07-28|website=search.amphilsoc.org}}</ref>  In 2009, he received the [[American Political Science Association]]'s [[Charles E. Merriam Award for Outstanding Public Policy Research|Charles E. Merriam Award]], which is biennially given to "a person whose published work and career represent a significant contribution to the art of government through the application of social science research."<ref>{{Cite journal|date=October 2011|title=Gazette|url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/ps-political-science-and-politics/article/gazette/1FD4EE3D30D44202A9683F216A4E917D/core-reader|journal=PS: Political Science & Politics|language=en|volume=44|issue=4|pages=885–898|doi=10.1017/S1049096511001582|issn=1537-5935|doi-access=free}}</ref> In 2011, Doyle received the Hubert H. Humphrey Award from the American Political Science Association for "notable public service by a political scientist."<ref>{{Cite journal|date=October 2011|title=Gazette|url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/ps-political-science-and-politics/article/gazette/1FD4EE3D30D44202A9683F216A4E917D/core-reader|journal=PS: Political Science & Politics|language=en|volume=44|issue=4|pages=885–898|doi=10.1017/S1049096511001582|issn=1537-5935|doi-access=free}}</ref> In 2012, he was named the Daniel Patrick Moynihan Fellow of the [[American Academy of Political and Social Science]].<ref>{{Cite web|date=2016-08-08|title=Michael Doyle|url=https://www.aapss.org/fellow/michael-doyle/|access-date=2020-07-28|website=AAPSS|language=en-US}}</ref> In 2014, he received an honorary degree from the University of Warwick.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Michael W. Doyle, creator of Doyle's Law, discusses the history of international relations, liberalism and world politics|url=http://www2.warwick.ac.uk/newsandevents/expertcomment/michael_w_doyle_creator_doyles_law_history_international_relations_liberalism_world_politics|access-date=2020-07-28|website=University of Warwick|language=en-GB}}</ref>

===Public service===
Doyle served as Assistant Secretary-General and Special Advisor to United Nations Secretary-General [[Kofi Annan]].<ref>{{Cite news|last=Gladstone|first=Rick|date=2019-02-19|title=America's U.N. Ambassador Post Is Empty. Is That a Problem?|language=en-US|work=The New York Times|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/02/19/world/americas/us-un-ambassador-empty.html|access-date=2020-07-28|issn=0362-4331}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=Who Is The New U.N. Secretary-General?|url=https://www.wbur.org/hereandnow/2016/10/06/new-un-secretary-general|access-date=2020-07-28|website=www.wbur.org|language=en}}</ref>  In the Secretary General's Executive Office, he was responsible for strategic planning, including the Millennium Development Goals, outreach to the international corporate sector through the [[United Nations Global Compact|Global Compact]], and relations with Washington.  He is the former chair of the Academic Council on the United Nations System.

He was also the chair of [[United Nations Democracy Fund]]<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.un.org/democracyfund/|title=UNDEF}}</ref> from 2007 to 2013, elected by the members and appointed by UN Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon.

=== Model International Mobility Convention ===
As the director of the Columbia Global Policy Initiative's,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://globalpolicy.columbia.edu/projects/international-migration|title=International Migration|last=|first=|date=|website=globalpolicy.columbia.edu |access-date=}}</ref> Doyle convened the group of experts who developed the Model International Mobility Convention.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://globalpolicy.columbia.edu/mobility-convention|title=Model International Mobility Convention|last=|first=|date=25 Aug 2017|website=Columbia Global Policy Initiative|access-date=}}</ref>

Now a Carnegie Council project, MIMC is building a Network that will encourage support for and develop the convention in order to address emerging international mobility challenges, including pandemic disease and climate stress.<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://www.carnegiecouncil.org/|title=Carngie Council}}</ref>

The Model International Mobility Convention fills a gap in international law by covering the multiple forms of international mobility, ranging from visitors through labor migrants to forced migrants and refugees. It proposes a comprehensive framework for international mobility with the goal of establishing a cumulative set of rights afforded to internationally mobile people (and the corresponding rights and responsibilities of states).<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://jtl.columbia.edu/model-international-mobility-convention/|title=Model International Mobility Convention|date=2017-12-13|work=Columbia Journal of Transnational Law|access-date=2018-01-17|language=en-US}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Doyle is married to [[Amy Gutmann]], President of the [[University of Pennsylvania]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Meet President Gutmann {{!}} Penn Office of the President|url=https://president.upenn.edu/node/3|access-date=2020-07-28|website=president.upenn.edu|language=en}}</ref> Their daughter, [[Abigail Doyle]], is a professor of chemistry at Princeton.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://chemistry.princeton.edu/faculty/doyle|title=Princeton University Department of Chemistry Abigail Doyle}}</ref>
