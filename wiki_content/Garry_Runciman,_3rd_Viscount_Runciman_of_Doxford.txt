'''Walter Garrison Runciman, 3rd Viscount Runciman of Doxford''', {{post-nominals|country=GBR|commas=on|CBE|FBA|size=100%}} (10 November 1934{{spnd}}10 December 2020), usually known informally as '''Garry Runciman''',<ref name=death/> was a [[United Kingdom|British]] historical [[sociologist]]. A senior research fellow at [[Trinity College, Cambridge]] Runciman wrote several publications in his field. He also sat on the [[Bank of England]]'s Securities and Investment Board and chaired the British Government's [[Royal Commission on Criminal Justice]] (1991–1993).

== Background ==
Runciman was the son of [[Leslie Runciman, 2nd Viscount Runciman of Doxford]], by his second wife Katherine Schuyler Garrison. British historian [[Steven Runciman|Sir Steven Runciman]] was his uncle.{{fact|date=October 2021}} He was educated at [[Eton College]], where he was an Oppidan Scholar, and [[Trinity College, Cambridge]].<ref>{{Who's Who|surname=RUNCIMAN OF DOXFORD|othernames=3rd Viscount (Walter Garrison Runciman (Garry))|id=U33424|volume=2018|edition=online}} {{subscription required}}</ref>

Runciman inherited the viscountcy on the death of his father in 1989.

== Career ==
Runciman joined the faculty of [[Trinity College, Cambridge]] in the 1950s as a historical sociologist and became a junior research fellow after submitting a thesis entitled ''Plato's Later Epistemology''. In the 1960s he became primarily a sociologist.<ref name=death/> He became a senior research fellow in 1971, researching in the field of comparative and historical sociology.<ref name=death/><ref name=trin/> Runciman's principal research interest was the application of neo-Darwinian evolutionary theory to cultural and social selection.<ref name=trin>[http://www.trin.cam.ac.uk/index.php?pageid=538 Bio at Trinity College website], trin.cam.ac.uk; Accessed 12 July 2014</ref>

He held honorary degrees from [[King's College London]] and the Universities of [[University of Edinburgh|Edinburgh]], [[University of Oxford|Oxford]], and [[University of York|York]]. He was also an Honorary Foreign Member of the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]] and an Honorary Bencher of [[Inner Temple]]. He was elected to the [[British Academy]] in 1975 and served as its president from 2001 to 2005.<ref>[http://www.britac.ac.uk/fellowship/directory/archive.asp?fellowsID=453 British Academy Fellows Archive] {{webarchive |url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110606091331/http://www.britac.ac.uk/fellowship/directory/archive.asp?fellowsID=453|date=6 June 2011}} Accessed 30 September 2008</ref> Runciman was also an honorary fellow of [[Nuffield College, Oxford]].<ref name=death/>

== Official and parliamentary work ==
Runciman was invited by the Governor of the [[Bank of England]] to serve on the Securities and Investment Board (later to become the [[Financial Services Authority]]), from which he retired in 1998.<ref>London Review of books, 21 January 2016</ref>

Runciman chaired the British Government's [[Royal Commission on Criminal Justice]], established in 1991 and which continued [[John May (judge)|Sir John May]]'s inquiry into the convictions of the [[Maguire Seven]] and encompassed further miscarriages of justice. It reported to parliament in 1993. As a result, the Criminal Appeal Act 1995 established the [[Criminal Cases Review Commission]] as an executive Non-Departmental Public Body.<ref>[http://www.ccrc.gov.uk/about/about_28.htm CCRC Website] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20070702200318/http://www.ccrc.gov.uk/about/about_28.htm|date=2 July 2007}}, ccrc.gov.uk; Accessed 12 July 2014.</ref><ref name=death/>

Runciman was a member of the [[House of Lords]] as a [[hereditary peer]] from the time he inherited the viscountcy on 1 September 1989. He spoke 26 times in the chamber until 11 November 1999 when he lost his right to sit there when the bulk of the hereditary peers were removed by the [[House of Lords Act 1999]].<ref>{{cite web|title=Mr Walter Runciman|url=https://api.parliament.uk/historic-hansard/people/mr-walter-runciman-2/index.html|publisher=Hansard|access-date=13 December 2020}}</ref> Runciman sat for a subsequent [[By-elections to the House of Lords#2010|by-election to the Lords in 2010]] to fill the [[Crossbencher#United Kingdom|Crossbench]] hereditary seat vacancy following the death of the [[Mark Colville, 4th Viscount Colville of Culross|Viscount Colville of Culross]]; the House seat went to the [[Nicholas Trench, 9th Earl of Clancarty|Earl of Clancarty]].

== Publications ==
Runciman's first major publication was ''Relative Deprivation and Social Justice: a Study of Attitudes to Social Inequality in Twentieth-Century Britain''.<ref>Routledge, 1966, reprinted by Gregg Revivals in 1993</ref> Since then, he has published ''A Critique of Max Weber's Philosophy of Social Science'',<ref>Cambridge University Press, 1972</ref> ''A Treatise on Social Theory'',<ref>Cambridge University Press, Vol. 1 1983, Vol. 2 1989, Vol. 3 1997</ref> and ''The Social Animal''.<ref>HarperCollins, 1998</ref><ref name=death/> In 2004, he edited and contributed to a British Academy occasional paper ''Hutton and Butler: Lifting the Lid on the Workings of Power'', which deals with the events surrounding Britain's participation in the invasion of Iraq and the way in which it was presented to the British public.<ref name=death/>

==Marriage and children==
Runciman married [[Ruth Runciman|Ruth Hellman]] on 17 April 1963.  She was made a [[Order of the British Empire|Dame Commander of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire]] (DBE) in 1998.<ref>[http://www.thepeerage.com/p56342.htm#i563420 the peerage.com, entry for Ruth Hellman]</ref> They had three children:<ref>[http://www.thepeerage.com/p56342.htm the peerage.com, entry for 3rd Viscount Runciman of Doxford]</ref>

* [[The Honourable|Hon]] Lisa Runciman (born 18 August 1965)
* [[David Runciman, 4th Viscount Runciman of Doxford|David Walter Runciman, 4th Viscount Runciman of Doxford]] (born 1 March 1967)
* Hon Catherine Runciman (born 18 July 1969)

Runciman died on 10 December 2020.<ref name=death>{{cite web|title=Walter Garrison Runciman, CBE, FBA, 3rd Viscount Runciman of Doxford (1934–2020)|url=https://www.trin.cam.ac.uk/news/walter-garrison-runciman-cbe-fba-3rd-viscount-runciman-of-doxford-1934-2020/|publisher=Trinity College, Cambridge|access-date=13 December 2020}}</ref> His heir, the 4th Viscount, is a political scientist and writer who teaches at [[Cambridge University]] as a Professor of Politics.<ref name=death/><ref>http://www.burkespeerage.com/; [http://www.thepeerage.com/ Runciman]</ref>
