'''Branko Horvat''' (24 July 1928 – 18 December 2003) was a [[Croatia]]n [[economist]] and [[politician]].<ref name=":0">{{Cite journal|first=Vladimir|last=Stipetić|author-link=Vladimir Stipetić|date=2003-12-20|title=Branko Horvat and Economic science Contribution to research his life and works|url=https://hrcak.srce.hr/103493|journal=Zbornik Radova Ekonomskog Fakulteta U Rijeci : časopis Za Ekonomsku Teoriju I Praksu|language=en|volume=21|issue=2|issn=1331-8004}}</ref>

Horvat was born in [[Petrinja]] on 24 July 1928. In 1944 during [[World War II in Yugoslavia|World War II]], Horvat and his father Artur Horvat joined the [[Yugoslav Partisans|Partisan]] movement in Croatia.<ref name=":0" /><ref name="jj">{{harvtxt|Romano|1980|p=392}}</ref> He worked a long time at the Institute of Economic Sciences, the former Planning Institute of the [[Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia]]. He was the editor of the journal ''Economic Analysis and Worker’s Self-Management'',<ref name=":0" /> and collaborator of the journal [[Praxis school|''Praxis'']],<ref>{{Cite journal|last=de Souza|first=Luiz Gustavo da Cunha|year=2016|title=Aproximação a um grupo quase esquecido: os marxistas iugoslavos do Praxis|url=https://periodicos.ufsc.br/index.php/emdebate/article/view/1980-3532.2016n15p4|journal=Revista Em Debate|language=pt|edition=15|pages=04–22|via=UFSC}}</ref> to which he contributed much from an economic viewpoint, though he was never a member of the group. He was also a member of the Economic Institute of [[Zagreb]].<ref name=":0" />

Horvat tried to unite democratic forces on a common platform, but without much success. He was highly critical of the economic policy of the [[Franjo Tuđman]] government (as he was before of the communist).<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Yoji|first=Koyama|date=2003-12-15|title=TRANSITION IN CROATIA|url=https://hrcak.srce.hr/index.php?show=clanak&id_clanak_jezik=40505|journal=Ekonomski Pregled|language=en|volume=54|issue=11–12|issn=0424-7558}}</ref> A [[Democratic socialism|democratic socialist]], he advocated a model of [[market socialism]], dubbed the ''Illyrian model'', where firms were [[Worker cooperative|owned and self-managed by their workers]] and competed with each other in [[Open market|open and free markets]].<ref>{{Cite news|url=https://pastdaily.com/2018/02/26/sava-kosanovic-yugoslavia-cold-war-1949-past-daily-reference-room/|title=Sava Kosanovic, Yugoslavia And The Cold War – 1949 – Past Daily...|last=gordonskene|date=2018-02-27|work=Past Daily|access-date=2018-07-19|language=en-US}}</ref> In 1992 he founded and became president of the [[Social Democratic Union (Croatia)|Social Democratic Union]]. Horvat organized a Balkan Conference with the primary aim of restoring cooperation between Yugoslav forces.<ref>{{Cite book|title=Yugoslavia : a state that withered away|last=Dejan.|first=Jović|date=2009|publisher=Purdue University Press|isbn=9781557534958|location=West Lafayette, Ind.|pages=135|oclc=225531506}}</ref>

His most widely known study is ''The Political Economy of Socialism'' (published in 1982 in English, in 1984 in [[Croatian language|Croatian]], and in 2001 in [[Chinese language|Chinese]]). He was nominated for the [[Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences|Nobel Prize in Economics]] in 1983.<ref>{{Cite book|title=The global crisis of 2008 and Keynes's general theory|last=Fikret|first=Čaušević|isbn=9783319114514|location=Cham|pages=70–71|oclc=891652669|date = 2014-09-16}}</ref>

Branko Horvat's wife, Ranka Peašinović, was a professor at the [[University of Zagreb]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.enciklopedija.hr/natuknica.aspx?id=47236|title=Peašinović, Ranka {{!}} Hrvatska enciklopedija|website=www.enciklopedija.hr|language=hr|access-date=2018-07-19}}</ref>

A street in [[Pristina]], [[Kosovo]] was named in Horvat's honour.<ref>[https://opendata.prishtinaonline.com/en/dataset/emraterrugeve/resource/3bd294a5-b0a1-4481-a17c-5cca12dcfbda Street Names PrishtinaOpenData], Municipality of Prishtina</ref>
