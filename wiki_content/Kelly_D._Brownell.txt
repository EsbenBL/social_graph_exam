'''Kelly David Brownell''' (born October 31, 1951)<ref>[http://connvoters.com/by_number/0011/40519_kelly_david_brownell.html State of Connecticut voter registration information]</ref> is a [[Clinical psychology#Professional practice|clinical psychologist]] and scholar of public health and public policy at [[Duke University]] whose work focuses on [[obesity]] and [[food policy]]. He is a former [[Dean (education)|dean]] of Duke's [[Sanford School of Public Policy]]. Noted for his research dealing primarily with obesity prevention, as well as the intersection of behavior, environment, and health with public policy, Brownell advised former [[First Lady of the United States|First Lady]] [[Michelle Obama]]'s initiatives to address childhood obesity<ref>{{cite news |last1= Gold |first1= Matea |last2= Hennessey |first2= Kathleen |date= July 21, 2013 |title= Michelle Obama's nutrition campaign comes with political pitfalls |url= http://www.baltimoresun.com/la-na-michelle-food-20130712-story.html |work= The Baltimore Sun |access-date= January 25, 2018}}</ref> and has testified before [[United States Congress|Congress]].<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.agriculture.senate.gov/imo/media/doc/brownell.pdf |title= TESTIMONY OF KELLY D. BROWNELL, Ph.D. |date= March 6, 2007 |website= United States Senate Committee On Agriculture, Nutrition, & Forestry |publisher= United States Senate |access-date= January 25, 2018}}</ref> He is credited with coining the term "[[yo-yo dieting]]",<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.nbcnews.com/id/36716808/ns/health-diet_and_nutrition/t/when-you-lose-weight-gain-it-all-back |title= When you lose weight - and gain it all back |date= June 6, 2010 |website= NBC News |publisher= NBC |access-date= January 26, 2018}}</ref> and was named as one of "The World's 100 Most Influential People" by ''[[Time (magazine)|Time Magazine]]'' in 2006.<ref>{{cite news |title= Kelly Brownell |url= http://ideas.time.com/contributor/kelly-brownell |work= Time Magazine |access-date= January 25, 2018}}</ref>

==Personal background==
Brownell was born in 1951 and was raised in [[Indiana]]. After receiving his [[Bachelor of Science]] degree in Psychology from [[Purdue University]] in 1973,<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.purdue.edu/hhs/psy/giving/distinguished-alumni.html |title= Meet our Distinguished Alumni |website= Psychological Sciences, College of Health and Human Sciences |publisher= Purdue University |access-date= January 26, 2018}}</ref> he was awarded a [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D]] in Psychology from [[Rutgers University]] in 1977.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://gsnb.rutgers.edu/distinguished-alumniae-awardees |title= Distinguished Alumni/ae Awardees |website= School of Graduate Studies |publisher= Rutgers University |access-date= January 26, 2018}}</ref> His advisor was Oscar Krisen Buros Professor G. Terence Wilson.

==Career==
In 1977, Brownell became a member of the faculty at the [[University of Pennsylvania]]'s [[Penn Med|medical school]]. He began as an assistant professor of psychology in psychiatry, was subsequently promoted to associate professor, and finally to full professor. During this period, he also served one year as a visiting scientist at the [[National Institutes of Health]] (NHI) [[National Cancer Institute]] (NCI).{{citation needed|date=August 2018}}

In 1991, he joined Yale University, where he held positions as the [[James Rowland Angell]] professor of psychology,<ref>{{cite web |url= https://medicine.yale.edu/news/article.aspx?id=4483 |title= Kelly Brownell named the James Rowland Angell Professor |website= Yale School of Medicine |publisher= Yale University |access-date= January 26, 2018}}</ref> professor of epidemiology and public health, director of the Rudd Center for Food Policy and Obesity, chair of the department of psychology, and head of the undergraduate dormitory [[Silliman College]].{{citation needed|date=August 2018}}

Brownell left Yale in 2013 to join Duke University as Dean of its Sanford School of Public Policy, in which role he continued until the end of the 2018 academic year.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://today.duke.edu/2013/01/brownell |title= Kelly Brownell Named Dean of Sanford School |website= Duke Today |publisher= Duke University |access-date= January 26, 2018}}</ref> He holds academic appointments as the Robert L. Flowers Professor of Public Policy, Professor of Psychology and [[Neuroscience]], Director of the World Food Policy Center, and is a faculty affiliate of the Duke Global Health Institute.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://duke.edu/about/leadership/brownell |title= Kelly Brownell |website= Duke University |publisher= Duke University |access-date= February 6, 2018}}</ref>

In 2017, backed by funding from the [[Duke Endowment]], [[William R. Kenan Jr.]] Charitable Trust, and [[Blue Cross Blue Shield Association|Blue Cross and Blue Shield]] of North Carolina Foundation, Duke University announced the formation of its new World Food Policy Center (WFPC), based at the Sanford School of Public Policy. Brownell is the center's founder and director.<ref>{{cite news |last= Stancill |first= Jane |date= July 10, 2017 |title= How Duke and NC State may play a role in what and how we eat |url= http://www.newsobserver.com/news/local/education/article160582804.html |work= News & Observer |access-date= January 30, 2018}}</ref>

To date, he has authored 15 books and more than 350 scientific articles, papers, and chapters.<ref name ="Sanford">{{cite web|url=https://sanford.duke.edu/people/faculty/brownell-kelly-d |title=Kelly D. Brownell |publisher=Sanford School of Public Policy |accessdate=January 29, 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine |url= https://www.theatlantic.com/author/kelly-brownell |title= Kelly Brownell |magazine= The Atlantic |access-date= January 29, 2018}}</ref> He has also contributed to [[mainstream media]] outlets.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://ideas.time.com/2011/10/24/meet-big-soda-as-bad-as-big-tobacco |title= Meet Big Soda — as Bad as Big Tobacco |website= Time Magazine |publisher= Time, Inc. |access-date= August 9, 2018}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |last= Brownell |first= Kelly D. |date= January 23, 2004 |title= The Sweet And Lowdown On Sugar |url= https://www.nytimes.com/2004/01/23/opinion/the-sweet-and-lowdown-on-sugar.html |work= New York Times|access-date= August 9, 2018}}</ref>

Brownell was previously president of the Society of Behavioral Medicine;<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.sbm.org/about/board-councils-and-committees/past-presidents |title= Past Presidents |website=Society of Behavioral Medicine |access-date= January 31, 2018}}</ref> Association for the Advancement of Behavior Therapy;<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.abct.org/About/?m=mAbout&fa=PastPresidents |title= Past Presidents |website= Association for the Advancement of Behavior Therapy |access-date= January 31, 2018}}</ref> and American Psychological Association, Division 38: Society for Health Psychology.<ref>{{cite web |url= http://www.apa.org/monitor/nov01/people.aspx |title= People |website=SAmerican Psychological Association |access-date= January 31, 2018}}</ref>

==Impact==
Brownell's 1986 paper, ''Understanding and Preventing Relapse'', published in ''American Psychologist'', was recognized at the time as one of the most frequently cited papers in psychology.<ref>{{cite book |last=Garfield |first=Eugene |series=Essays of an Information Scientist Series, Volume15: Of Nobel Class, Women in Science, Citation Classics and Other Essays |title=Psychology Research, 1986-1990: A Citationist Perspective on the Highest Impact Papers, Institutions, and Authors |url=http://garfield.library.upenn.edu/essays/v15p155y1992-93.pdf |publisher=Institute for Scientific Information (ISI) |date=October 12, 1992 |isbn=978-0894950933 |author-link=Eugene Garfield}}</ref>

Recognized for introducing the idea of food taxes as a means of improving public health in 1994,<ref>{{cite news |last1= O'Connor |first1= Anahad |last2=Sanger-Katz |first2=Margot |date= November 26, 2016 |title= As Soda Taxes Gain Wider Acceptance, Your Bottle May Be Next |url= https://www.nytimes.com/2016/11/26/well/eat/as-soda-taxes-gain-wider-acceptance-your-bottle-may-be-next.html |work= New York Times |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref> his work on [[soda tax]]es<ref>{{cite magazine |last=Park |first= Alice |date= December 13, 2010 |title= Study: Soda Taxes May Not Be Enough to Curb Obesity |url=http://healthland.time.com/2010/12/13/study-sugar-tax-may-lead-to-only-modest-weight-loss |magazine= TIME Magazine |publisher= Time, Inc. |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref> has been used by cities, states, and countries seeking to implement them as a public policy tool and tax revenue strategy.<ref>{{cite magazine |last1=Taubes |first1= Gary |last2=Kearns Couzens |first2=Cristin |date= September 14, 2012 |title= Why Mike Bloomberg's 'Soda Ban' Could Actually Work |url=https://www.thedailybeast.com/why-mike-bloombergs-soda-ban-could-actually-work |magazine= Daily Beast |publisher= IAC Publishing |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref> In commentary for ''Time Magazine's'' "Time 100 of 2006", former Arkansas governor and presidential candidate [[Mike Huckabee]] commented that Brownell had "helped set the U.S. agenda by calling for a ban on sweetened-cereal ads aimed at kids and a tax on high-fat, low-nutrition food."<ref>{{cite magazine |last=Huckabee |first= Mike |date= May 8, 2006 |title= Kelly Brownell |url=http://content.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,1975813_1975844_1976430,00.html |magazine= TIME Magazine |publisher= Time, Inc. |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref>

Brownell has also influenced popular culture. In addition to having coined the term "yo-yo dieting", he is also credited with introducing the phrase "[[toxic food environment]]" in his 2004 book, ''[[Food Fight: The Inside Story of the Food Industry]]''.<ref>{{cite web |url= https://www.huffingtonpost.com/dr-susan-albers/chocolate-favored-toddler_b_563685.html |title= Chocolate Flavored Toddler Formula. Yikes! |website= Huffington Post Lifestyle |publisher= Huffington Post |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref> A frequent radio<ref>{{cite interview |last=Brownell |first=Kelly D. |interviewer= Mary Harris |title= The Big Business of The Obesity Crisis |work=[[The Takeaway]] |publisher=[[Public Radio International]] and [[WNYC]] |location=New York |date=June 7, 2017}}</ref> and television guest,<ref>{{cite news |last= Neal |first= Rome |date= November 26, 2016 |title= 'Super-Sizing' America's Kids |url= https://www.cbsnews.com/news/super-sizing-americas-kids |work= CBS News |access-date= February 5, 2018}}</ref> he is the host of the ''Policy 360'' podcast, and has appeared in a variety of feature films and documentaries:
