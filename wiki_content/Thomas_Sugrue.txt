'''Thomas J. Sugrue''' (born 1962, [[Detroit]], [[Michigan]]) is an [[United States|American]] [[historian]] of the 20th-century United States at [[New York University]].<ref name="NYU">{{cite web|url=https://as.nyu.edu/content/nyu-as/as/faculty/thomas-sugrue.html |title=New York University Homepage|access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> From 1991 to 2015, he was the David Boies Professor of History and Sociology at the [[University of Pennsylvania]]<ref name="Penn Almanac">{{cite web|url=https://almanac.upenn.edu/archive/volumes/v56/n25/sugrue.html|title=Thomas Sugrue, David Boies Professor of History and Sociology|access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> and founding director of the Penn Social Science and Policy Forum.<ref name="PennSSPF">{{cite web|url=https://penntoday.upenn.edu/news/provost-announces-penn-social-science-and-policy-forum|title=Provost Announces Penn Social Science and Policy Forum |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> His areas of expertise include American urban history, American political history, housing and the history of race relations. He has published extensively on the [[Liberalism#Development of liberal thought|history of liberalism]] and [[conservatism]], on [[housing]] and [[real estate]], on [[poverty]] and [[public policy]], on [[civil rights]], and on the history of [[affirmative action]].

==Early life==

Sugrue was born in 1962 in Detroit, Michigan and lived there until the age of ten, when his family moved to the suburbs.<ref name="PennGazette">{{cite web|url=https://www.upenn.edu/gazette/0509/feature1_1.html|title=The Vital Thread of Tom Sugrue, Penn Gazette, May/June 2009 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> He graduated from [[Brother Rice High School (Michigan)]] in 1980 and from [[Columbia University]] (''Summa Cum Laude'', [[Phi Beta Kappa]]) in 1984, with a degree in History. From 1984-1986, Sugrue attended [[King's College, Cambridge|King's College]], [[Cambridge University]] on a [[Kellett Fellowship]] and earned a B.A. (honours) in British History and the Doncaster History Prize of King's College. He earned his Ph.D. in history from [[Harvard University]] in 1992 working with [[Stephan Thernstrom]] and [[Barbara Guttmann Rosenkrantz]].<ref>{{cite book|last1=Sugrue|first1=Thomas J.|title=The Origins of the Urban Crisis Race and Inequality in Postwar Detroit|url=https://archive.org/details/originsofurbancr00thom|url-access=registration|date=1996|publisher=Princeton University Press|location=Princeton, N.J.|isbn=978-0691162553|page=li}}</ref>

==Academic==
Sugrue began his teaching career at the University of Pennsylvania in 1991. He has also been a visiting faculty member at [[New York University]], Harvard University, and the [[Ecole des Hautes Etudes en Sciences Sociales]] in Paris. Sugrue's first book, ''[[The Origins of the Urban Crisis]]'' ([[Princeton University Press]], 1996) was widely acclaimed. It won the prestigious [[Bancroft Prize]] in History,<ref name="Bancroft">{{cite web|url=https://library.columbia.edu/about/awards/bancroft/previous_awards.html|title=Columbia University Libraries, Bancroft Prizes: Previous Awards |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> the President's Book Award of the Social Science History Association, the Philip Taft Prize in Labor History, the Urban History Association Prize for Best Book in North American Labor History,<ref name="UrbanHistory">{{cite web|url=https://www.urbanhistory.org/Past-Awards|title=Urban History Association Past Awards |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> and was selected as a Choice Outstanding Book. In 2005, Princeton University Press selected ''Origins of the Urban Crisis'' as one of its 100 most influential books of the preceding century [http://press.princeton.edu/titles/7937.html] and issued it as a Princeton Classic. Sugrue has also edited two other books, ''W.E.B. DuBois, Race, and the City'' (University of Pennsylvania Press, 1998), with Michael B. Katz, and ''The New Suburban History'' ([[University of Chicago Press]], 2005), with Kevin M. Kruse. His 2008 book ''Sweet Land of Liberty: The Forgotten Struggle for Civil Rights in the North'' was a finalist for the [[Los Angeles Times Book Prize]] in History <ref name="LATimes">{{cite web|url=https://latimesblogs.latimes.com/jacketcopy/2009/03/los-angeles-tim.html |title=Los Angeles Times Announces 2008 Book Prize Nominees |date=2 March 2009 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> and a main selection of the [[History Book Club]]. He is also author of ''Not Even Past: Barack Obama and the Burden of Race.''<ref>Diamond A.J., "Colorblindness and Racial Politics in the Era of Obama," Review of 'Not Even Past,' Books and Ideas, December 2010. [http://www.booksandideas.net/Color-Blindness-and-Racial.html?lang=fr] and McNeil D. Review of 'Not Even Past: Barack Obama and the burden of race'. Social Identities 2010, 16(6).</ref> His most recent book is ''These United States: The Making of a Nation, 1890 to the Present'' with [[Glenda Gilmore]]. He is currently writing a history of the rise and transformation of the real estate industry in modern America. He has also published essays and reviews in the ''[[Wall Street Journal]]'', ''[[New York Times]]'', ''[[Washington Post]]'', ''[[The Nation]]'', ''[[London Review of Books]]'', ''[[Chicago Tribune]]'', ''[[Philadelphia Inquirer]]'', and ''[[Detroit Free Press]]''. In 2010 he served as a guest-blogger for [[Ta-Nehisi Coates]] at ''[[The Atlantic]]''.<ref name="Atlantic">{{cite web|url=https://www.theatlantic.com/national/archive/2010/08/school-daze/61526/|title=School Daze, Atlantic, August 2010 |date=16 August 2010 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref>

Sugrue has won fellowships and grants from the [[Brookings Institution]], the Social Science Research Council, the [[John Simon Guggenheim Memorial Foundation|Guggenheim Foundation]], the American Council of Learned Societies, the American Philosophical Society, and the [[Institute for Advanced Study]] in Princeton.<ref name="IAS">{{cite web|url=https://www.ias.edu/scholars/thomas-j-sugrue
|title=Institute for Advanced Study Scholars 2005-06 |date=9 December 2019 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> He was an inaugural Alphonse [[Fletcher Foundation]] Fellow,<ref name="Fletcher">{{cite web|url=https://penntoday.upenn.edu/news/penn-professor-thomas-sugrue-wins-fellowship-civil-rights-research
|title=Penn Professor Thomas Sugrue Wins Fellowship for Civil Rights Research, Penn Today, April 22, 2005 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref>
and was in the first class of Andrew Carnegie Fellows in 2015.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.carnegie.org/interactives/acfellows/#!/|title=Andrew Carnegie Fellows}}</ref> He is an elected Fellow of the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]],<ref name="AmAcad">{{cite web|url=https://www.amacad.org/person/thomas-j-sugrue/|title=Thomas J. Sugrue, American Academy of Arts and Sciences |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> the [[New York Institute for the Humanities]],<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://nyihumanities.org/all-features/new-fellows-2016|title=Meet the New Fellows of 2016}}</ref> and is the Walter Lippmann Fellow of the [[American Academy of Political and Social Science]].<ref name="AAPSS">{{cite web|url=https://www.aapss.org/fellow/thomas-sugrue/|title=Thomas J. Sugrue, 2016 Walter Lippmann Fellow of the American Academy of Political and Social Science |date=9 August 2016 |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> In 2013-14, he served as President of the Urban History Association.<ref name="UHA">{{cite web|url=https://www.urbanhistory.org/Past-Leadership
|title=Past Presidents Urban History Association |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> In 2016, he received an honorary doctorate from [[Wayne State University]].<ref name="WSU">{{cite web|url=https://today.wayne.edu/news/2016/04/21/wayne-state-university-commencement-ceremonies-celebrate-class-of-2016-may-5-5858

==Background==

Sugrue is active in civic affairs. Most notably, he served as an expert for the [[University of Michigan]] in two federal court cases regarding affirmative action in the undergraduate and law school admissions--''[[Grutter v. Bollinger]]'' and ''[[Gratz v. Bollinger]]'', decided by the [[Supreme Court of the United States|U.S. Supreme Court]] in 2003.<ref name="UMich">{{cite web|url=https://diversity.umich.edu/admissions/research/expert/|title=University of Michigan Admissions Lawsuits, Expert Testimony: The Compelling Need for Diversity in Higher Education |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref> He served as vice chair of the City of [[Philadelphia Historical Commission]] from 2001-2008.<ref name="PHC">{{cite web|url=https://penntoday.upenn.edu/2003-10-16/latest-news/history-written-stone-brick-and-steel
|title=History Written in Stone, Brick, and Steel, Penn Today |access-date=3 March 2021}}</ref>
Sugrue is a popular teacher—winner of two teaching awards—and mentor to many dissertation students. He is also a well-regarded public speaker, having given more than 300 talks to audiences at universities, foundations, community groups, and religious congregations throughout the United States and in Canada, Britain, France, Argentina, Japan, Israel, and Germany. Sugrue has appeared in several television series and documentary films.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.imdb.com/name/nm1489521/|title=Thomas Sugrue IMDb Page |access-date=9 March 2021}}</ref>
