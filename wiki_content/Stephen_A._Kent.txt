'''Stephen A. Kent''',  is a Professor in the Department of Sociology at the [[University of Alberta]] in [[Edmonton]], [[Alberta]], [[Canada]].<ref name="goodyear">{{cite web | first = Dana | last = Goodyear | title = Château Scientology | url =http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2008/01/14/080114fa_fact_goodyear?printable=true| work = Letter from California | publisher = New Yorker | date =2008-01-14 | access-date = 2009-06-06 }}</ref> He researches [[new religious movements]],<ref name="goodyear" /> and has published research on several such groups including the [[Family International|Children of God]] (also known as The Family),<ref name="white">{{cite news   | last =White  | first =Gayle   | title ='Heaven's Harlot' looks back on Jesus freak days  | work =The Atlanta Journal and The Atlanta Constitution  | page =D01  | date =July 25, 1998 }}</ref> the [[Church of Scientology]],<ref>{{cite web | author = Douglas Frantz | title = Distrust in Clearwater -- A special report.; Death of a Scientologist Heightens Suspicions in a Florida Town | url =https://query.nytimes.com/gst/fullpage.html?res=9E07E2DE1F3AF932A35751C1A961958260&sec=health&spon=&pagewanted=all| work = New York Times | date = 1997-12-01 | access-date =2009-06-06 }}</ref> and newer [[faith]]s operating in Canada.

==Education==
Kent graduated from the [[University of Maryland, College Park]] in 1973, with a [[Bachelor of Arts|B.A.]] degree in [[Sociology]] and a [[minor (academic)|minor]] in Social and Political Theory<ref name="zablocki">{{cite book  | last =Zablocki  | first =Benjamin David   |author2=Thomas Robbins (sociologist)   |author2-link=Thomas Robbins (sociologist)   | title =Misunderstanding Cults: Searching for Objectivity in a Controversial Field | publisher =University of Toronto Press  | year =2001  | page =523 | isbn = 0-8020-8188-6}}</ref><ref name="Faculty page">{{cite web|title=University of Alberta Faculty Page|url=http://www.sociology.ualberta.ca/FacultyStaffandGraduateStudent/AcademicStaff/KentStephen.aspx|access-date=26 August 2015|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150924085951/http://www.sociology.ualberta.ca/FacultyStaffandGraduateStudent/AcademicStaff/KentStephen.aspx|archive-date=24 September 2015}}</ref> In 1978, he was awarded a [[Master's Degree]] in the History of Religions, from [[American University]].<ref name="zablocki" /> Kent was also awarded an M.A. in 1980 from [[McMaster University]] with a focus in religion and modern Western society and a minor in Indian Buddhism; he was granted a [[Ph.D.]] in [[religious studies]] in 1984 from the same institution.<ref name="zablocki" /><ref>{{cite news | title = Kent, Stephen; Professor, Theory/Culture; PhD, McMaster | work = Department of Sociology; Faculty of Arts | publisher = [[University of Alberta]] | year = 2010 | url = http://www.uofaweb.ualberta.ca/sociology/kent.cfm | access-date = 2010-06-04 | url-status = dead | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20120726051637/http://www.sociology.ualberta.ca/ | archive-date = 2012-07-26 }}</ref> From 1984 to 1986, Kent held an Izaac Walton Killam Postdoctoral Fellowship in the sociology department at the [[University of Alberta]].<ref name="zablocki" />

==Research==
John H. Simpson writes in a chapter of [[Lori G. Beaman]]'s 2006 book ''Religion and Canadian Society'' that Kent "finds himself on the cult side of the cult/new religious movement divide."<ref name="beaman">{{cite book  | last =Beaman  | first =Lori G. |author-link=Lori G. Beaman  | title =Religion and Canadian Society: Traditions, Transitions, and Innovations  | publisher =Canadian Scholars' Press  | year =2006  | page =272  | isbn = 978-1-55130-306-2}}</ref> Simpson recommends Kent for further reading on the group [[Family International|Children of God]], also referred to as "The Family", and notes: "He has done extensive research on new religious movements and argues that we need to be careful about minimizing the risks of involvement with such groups. His work is a good example of the issues taken up by scholars who focus on 'cults.'"<ref name="beaman" /> Kent has devoted significant study to the Children of God, and the group's founder [[David Berg]].<ref name="white" /> He has researched testimony of individuals that have alleged [[Satanic ritual abuse]], in a period from the 1930s to 1980s.<ref name="christiano">{{cite book  | last =Christiano  | first =Kevin J.  | author2 =William H. Swatos  | author3 =Peter Kivisto  | title =Sociology of Religion: Contemporary Developments  | publisher =[[Rowman Altamira]]  | year =2002  | pages =[https://archive.org/details/sociologyofrelig0000chri/page/68 68, 319, 321, 344]  | isbn =0-7591-0035-7  | url =https://archive.org/details/sociologyofrelig0000chri/page/68  }}</ref> Kevin J. Christiano notes in the book ''Sociology of Religion: Contemporary Developments'' that Kent's research "shows that the Bible and biblical themes provide the primary references for the articulation of abuse", noting that "purported cult ceremonies particularly used biblical references and metaphors."<ref name="christiano" /> [[Irving Hexham]] commented about Kent in a 2001 article in ''[[Nova Religio]]'', "The one exception to the generally neutral tone of Canadian academics and their rejection of anticult rhetoric is Stephen Kent, who has been outspoken in his criticism of many new religions, particularly Scientology, and who works closely with various anticult groups. Although Kent's views are widely known, few Canadian academics agree with his findings and most disagree quite strongly because of his tendency to use the testimony of ex-members."<ref name="hexham">{{cite journal|title=New Religions and the Anticult Movement in Canada|journal=[[Nova Religio]]|date=April 2001|first=Irving|last=Hexham|volume=4|issue=2|pages=286|doi=10.1525/nr.2001.4.2.281|url=http://caliber.ucpress.net/doi/pdfplus/10.1525/nr.2001.4.2.281|format=PDF|access-date=2010-04-21|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120226185436/http://caliber.ucpress.net/doi/pdfplus/10.1525/nr.2001.4.2.281|archive-date=2012-02-26|url-status=dead}}</ref> Sociologist [[Anson Shupe]] and Susan J. Darnell characterised Kent as "eccentric", stating that research and police enquiries into the allegations of satanic ritual human sacrifice had proved them to be unfounded.<ref name="shupe">{{cite book  | last =Shupe  | first =Anson  | author-link =Anson Shupe  | author2 =Susan E. Darnell  | title =Agents of Discord: Deprogramming, Pseudo-Science, and the American Anticult Movement  | publisher =[[Transaction Publishers]]  | year =2006  | pages =[https://archive.org/details/sociologyofrelig0000chri/page/135 135–136]  | isbn =0-7591-0035-7  | url =https://archive.org/details/sociologyofrelig0000chri/page/135  }}</ref> Criminologist Michael Salter, however, views Kent's work on ritual abuse more favorably.<ref>{{cite book  | last =Salter  | first =Mark.  | title =Organised sexual abuse  | publisher =[[Routledge]]  | year =2012}}</ref>

With fellow sociologist Theresa Krebs, Kent has written about instances of "when scholars know sin".<ref>{{cite journal  | last =Kent  | first =Stephen A.  |author2=Theresa Krebs   | title =When Scholars Know Sin: Alternative Religions and Their Academic Supporters   | journal =[[Skeptic Magazine]]  | volume =6  | issue =3  | year =1998  | url =http://www.arts.ualberta.ca/~skent/Linkedfiles/When%20Scholars%20Know%20Sin%20.htm  | access-date =  2009-06-12}}</ref> In their book ''Denying History'', authors [[Michael Shermer]] and [[Alex Grobman]] note "Sociologists are aware of the problem of a researcher's 'co-option' by a group&ndash;a cult or New Age religion, perhaps&ndash; whereby the scholar, in entering a group and spending considerable time with its members, publishes a paper or book that is not as objective as he or she may believe."<ref name="shermer">{{cite book  | last =Shermer  | first =Michael   |author2=Alex Grobman   | title =Denying History  | publisher =University of California Press | year =2009  | page =57  | isbn = 978-0-520-26098-6}}</ref> Shermer and Grobman cite Kent and Krebs' work, commenting "In fact, the sociologists Stephen Kent and Theresa Krebs have identified numerous cases of 'when scholars know sin,' where allegedly nonpartisan, unbiased scholars find themselves the unwitting tools of religious groups striving for social acceptance and in need of the imprimatur of an academic."<ref name="shermer" />

==''From Slogans to Mantras''==
Kent's book ''[[From Slogans to Mantras|From Slogans to Mantras: Social Protest and Religious Conversion in the Late Vietnam Era]]'' was published in 2001 by [[Syracuse University Press]].<ref>{{cite book  | last =Kent  | first =Stephen A.  | title =From Slogans to Mantras: Social Protest and Religious Conversion in the Late Vietnam Era  | publisher =[[Syracuse University Press]]  | year =2001   | isbn =0-8156-2948-6 }}</ref> In the book, Kent explored how political activists from the period of the [[Vietnam War]] later turned to alternative religious movements including [[Hare Krishnas]], [[Transcendental Meditation]], [[Scientology]], and the [[Unification Church]].<ref name="publishers">{{cite journal  | title =From Slogans to Mantras  | journal =[[Publishers Weekly]]  | volume =248  | issue =42  | page =66  | publisher =Cahners Business Information   | date =October 15, 2001 }}</ref>   In 2003 it was cited by ''Choice'' as an outstanding academic title that should be owned by every library.<ref>{{cite book

==Awards and recognition==
In 2003, Kent's book ''[[From Slogans to Mantras]]'' was cited by ''[[Choice: Current Reviews for Academic Libraries]]'' as an "Outstanding Academic Title" that should be owned by every library.<ref name="bartlett">{{cite book  | last = Bartlett  | first = Rebecca Ann  | title = Choice's Outstanding Academic Titles, 1998-2002  | publisher = [[Association of College & Research Libraries]]  | year = 2003  | pages = 571, 601, 621  | isbn = 0-8389-8232-8 }}</ref> Kent was recognized by his students at the [[University of Alberta]] in 2009.<ref name="gsaaward" /> He received a "Graduate Student Supervisor Award" from the Graduate Students' Association on March 12, 2009.<ref name="gsaaward">{{cite web  | title =2009 GSA Award Recipients  | work =Department of Sociology, Faculty of Arts  | publisher =[[University of Alberta]]  | date =March 16, 2009  | url =http://www.uofaweb.ualberta.ca/sociology/news.cfm?story=89063  | access-date =2009-06-12  }}{{dead link|date=March 2018 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref> In April 2010, Kent received the "Bill Meloff Memorial Teaching Award" given by the Department of Sociology at the University of Alberta.<ref name="cwhalen">{{cite news | last = Whalen | first = C | title = Stephen Kent receives the 2010 Bill Meloff Memorial Teaching Award | work = News: Department of Sociology, Faculty of Arts | publisher = [[University of Alberta]] | date = April 14, 2010 | url = http://www.uofaweb.ualberta.ca/sociology/news.cfm?story=98469 | access-date = 2010-06-02 }}{{dead link|date=March 2018 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref> He stated he would utilize the [[United States dollar|US$]] 1,000 award to "update the department's DVD collections in the sociology of deviance and the sociology of religion".<ref name="cwhalen" />
