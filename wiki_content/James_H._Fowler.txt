'''James H. Fowler''' (born February 18, 1970)<ref name=Congress>{{cite web |title= Fowler, James H., 1970- |url= http://id.loc.gov/authorities/names/n2006058420.html |publisher= Library of Congress |access-date= 5 August 2014 |quote= ecip t.p. (James H. Fowler) data view (b. 2/18/1970) }}</ref> is an American [[social sciences|social scientist]] specializing in [[social networks]], [[cooperation]], [[political participation]], and [[genopolitics]] (the study of the genetic basis of political behavior). He is currently Professor of Medical Genetics in the [[UC San Diego School of Medicine|School of Medicine]] and Professor of Political Science in the Division of Social Science at the [[University of California, San Diego]].  He was named a [[List of Guggenheim Fellowships awarded in 2010|2010 Fellow of the John Simon Guggenheim Foundation]].<ref>http://www.gf.org/fellows/16759-james-fowler</ref>

==Background==
Fowler earned a bachelor's degree from [[Harvard College]] in 1992, a master's degree in International Relations from [[Yale University]] in 1997, and a Ph.D. in Government from the [[Harvard Graduate School of Arts and Sciences|Harvard University]] in 2003.  He was also a [[Peace Corps]] volunteer in [[Ecuador]] from 1992 to 1994. In 2010, he was named by Foreign Policy magazine to its list of top global thinkers.<ref>[https://foreignpolicy.com/articles/2010/11/29/the_fp_top_100_global_thinkers?page=0,44 "Foreign Policy Magazine"]</ref>

==Research==
Fowler's research centers on [[social networks]].<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.seedmagazine.com/news/2009/01/seed_salon_albertlaszlo_baraba.php |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090129053308/http://seedmagazine.com/news/2009/01/seed_salon_albertlaszlo_baraba.php |url-status=unfit |archive-date=2009-01-29 |title=Seed Salon: Albert-László Barabási and James Fowler |work=Seed |date=February 2009}}</ref> He is best known for his studies of the social spread of [[obesity]],<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.cbsnews.com/stories/2007/07/25/eveningnews/main3097768.shtml |title=Are Friends And Family Making You Fat? In addition to his research on social networks, Fowler has become known for his work on [[genopolitics]], showing that genes influence voting and other forms of political participation.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/12/14/magazine/14Ideas-Section2-B-t-007.html |title=Eighth Annual Year in Ideas: Genopolitics |work=New York Times Magazine |date=December 12, 2008 | first=Emily | last=Biuso | access-date=May 5, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB122047003725696177 |title=The Biology of Ideology |work=Wall Street Journal |date=May 27, 2008}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/05/27/opinion/27tue4.html |title=It's the Genes Stupid |work=New York Times |date=Sep 4, 2008}}</ref>  Fowler used [[twin studies]] of voter turnout in Los Angeles and the [[National Longitudinal Study of Adolescent Health]] to establish that the decision to vote in the United States has very strong [[heritability]].<ref>{{cite journal|url=http://www.sciam.com/article.cfm?id=the-genetics-of-politics |title=The Genetics of Politics |journal=Scientific American |date=November 2007}}</ref>  He has also identified three genes that are associated with voter turnout and partisan attachment, specifically those regulating the [[serotonin]] and [[dopamine]] systems in the brain via the production of [[monoamine oxidase]], 5HTT, and DRD2.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.cnn.com/2008/HEALTH/02/11/politics.genes/index.html |title=Are Politics Rooted in Your Genes? |work=CNN |date=February 11, 2008}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2008/05/27/opinion/27tue4.html |title=It's the Genes, Stupid |work=New York Times |date=May 27, 2008}}</ref>

More recently, he has shown evidence that [[social networks]] have a partly genetic basis.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB123302040874118079 |title=Genes and the Friends You Make  |work=Wall Street Journal |date=January 27, 2009 | first=Philip | last=Shishkin}}</ref>  In 2010, he published a paper regarding the use of social networks as 'sensors' for forecasting epidemics.<ref>{{cite journal | author = Christakis NA, Fowler JH | year = 2010| title = Social Network Sensors for Early Detection of Contagious Outbreaks | journal = PLOS ONE | volume = 5 | issue = 9| page = e12948 | doi = 10.1371/journal.pone.0012948 | pmid=20856792 | pmc=2939797| arxiv = 1004.4792| bibcode = 2010PLoSO...512948C| doi-access = free}}</ref>

In other research, Fowler is known for his theoretical and experimental work on [[egalitarianism]]<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/connected/main.jhtml?xml=/connected/2007/04/12/echood12.xml |title=The Robin Hood impulse |work=The Daily Telegraph |date=12 April 2007 |page=8 | location=London | first=Roger | last=Highfield | access-date=May 5, 2010}}{{dead link|date=July 2021|bot=medic}}{{cbignore|bot=medic}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|title=Making the Paper: James Fowler |journal=Nature |volume=446 |issue=7137 |pages=xiii |date=12 April 2007| doi= 10.1038/7137xiiia|s2cid=49907006 }}</ref> and the evolution of [[cooperation]],<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.usnews.com/usnews/culture/articles/050613/13behavior.htm |title=Why We Need Nosy Parkers |work=U.S. News and World Report |date=June 13, 2005}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=5328549 |title=Groups Unite in Dislike of Freeloaders |work=National Public Radio |date=April 6, 2006}}</ref> with related work on [[altruism]], [[Overconfidence effect|overconfidence]],<ref>{{cite news|url=http://news.nationalgeographic.com/news/2011/09/110914-optimism-narcissism-overconfidence-hubris-evolution-science-nature/ |title=Evolution of Narcissism: Why We're Overconfident, and Why It Works |work=National Geographic |date=September 14, 2011}}</ref> and political participation.<ref>{{cite journal|title=Political Scientists Convene to Probe and Predict U.S. Elections |journal=Chronicle of Higher Education|date=September 17, 2004}}</ref>

Fowler's newest research focuses on Facebook friendship as a predictor of voter mobilization, and the use of the social network medium, to mobilize voters in American elections. Based on a large manipulation of friendship cues in the Facebook social network, Fowler demonstrates that being presented with indications of friends having voted is a strong predictor of individual voting.

==Books==
In September 2009, [[Little, Brown & Co]]. published ''Connected: The Surprising Power of Our Social Networks and How They Shape Our Lives'' by [[Nicholas A. Christakis]] and James H. Fowler.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/10/04/books/review/Stossel-t.html |title=You and Your Friend's Friend's Friends  |work=New York Times Book Review |date=October 4, 2009 | first=Scott | last=Stossel | access-date=May 5, 2010}}</ref> ''Connected'' draws on previously published and unpublished studies, including the [[Framingham Heart Study]] and makes several new conclusions about the influence of social networks on human health and behavior.<ref>http://www.connectedthebook.com/ "Connected The Book" Official Website</ref> In ''Connected'', they put forward their "[[three degrees of influence]]" rule about human behavior, which theorizes that each person's individual social influence stretches three degrees before it fades out.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.wired.com/medtech/health/magazine/17-10/ff_christakis |title= The Buddy System: How Medical Data Revealed Secret to Health and Happiness |work=Wired |date= October 2009}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/09/13/magazine/13contagion-t.html |title= Is Happiness Catching?  |work=New York Times Magazine |date= September 14, 2009 | first=Clive | last=Thompson | access-date=May 5, 2010}}</ref>

==''The Colbert Report''==
On February 28, 2008, the [[Los Angeles Times]] published an op-ed that summarized Fowler's research showing that Democratic candidates who come on ''[[The Colbert Report]]'' receive 44% more in campaign donations in the first 30 days after appearing on the show.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.latimes.com/news/opinion/la-oe-fowler28feb28,0,1569470.story |title=Sharing the wealthiness |work=Los Angeles Times |date=February 28, 2008 |last=Fowler |first=James H.}}</ref><ref>{{cite journal|url=http://jhfowler.ucsd.edu/colbert_bump.pdf |title=The Colbert Bump in Campaign Donations: More Truthful than Truthy |journal=[[PS Political Science & Politics]] |issue=3 |volume=41 |pages=533–539 |date=July 2008 |last=Fowler |first=James H.|doi=10.1017/s1049096508080712|s2cid=153952070 }}</ref> Colbert pointed out the op-ed on his [[List of The Colbert Report episodes (2008)|March 3, 2008 show]].  Fowler also appeared during the [[Threatdown]] on his [[List of The Colbert Report episodes (2008)|December 10, 2008 show]], describing his work on the spread of happiness in social networks, and again on the [[List of The Colbert Report episodes (2010)|January 7th, 2010 show]] to discuss the sociological impact of social networking.
