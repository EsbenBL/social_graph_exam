'''Patrick Thomas Riley''' (October 27, 1941 – March 10, 2015)<ref>http://polisci.wisc.edu/content.aspx?cmspageid=796#riley</ref> was [[Michael Oakeshott]] Professor of Political Science at the [[University of Wisconsin-Madison]]. He is notable for his translations of the political writings of [[Gottfried Leibniz]] and his research on [[social contract theory]], the general will, and the history of universal jurisprudence. His first book, ''Will and Political Legitimacy'',<ref>Harvard: Harvard University Press, 1982</ref> offered "a critical exposition of social contract theory in Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel."<ref>{{Cite book| url=https://books.google.com/books?id=bx6DAAAAMAAJ | title=Will and Political Legitimacy: A Critical Exposition of Social Contract Theory in Hobbes, Locke, Rousseau, Kant, and Hegel| isbn=9780674953161| last1=Riley| first1=Patrick| date=1982-01-01}}</ref>  In ''Leibniz' Universal Jurisprudence: Justice as the Charity of the Wise'',<ref>Harvard: Harvard University Press, 1996</ref> Riley detailed the social, moral, and political philosophy of Leibniz, arguing for the English-speaking world that Leibniz was the most important German philosopher before [[Immanuel Kant|Kant]]. He has also written extensively on the general will of [[Rousseau]] and [[Political philosophy of Immanuel Kant|Kant's political philosophy]]. He was the author of ''The General Will before Rousseau: The Transformation of the Divine into the Civic'' and the editor of the ''Cambridge Companion to Rousseau.''<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://polisci.wisc.edu/people/person.aspx?id=1105|title = Faculty}}</ref> Riley edited a volume of Leibniz's political writings, as well as produced translations of Malebranche and Fénelon for Cambridge University Press.<ref>http://www.cambridge.org/gb/knowledge/isbn/item1136502/?site_locale=en_GB {{Bare URL inline|date=November 2021}}</ref>

Riley received his undergraduate degree from [[Claremont McKenna College|Claremont Men's College]].<ref>{{Cite journal|jstor = 3329710|title = Martin Diamond's View of "The Federalist"|journal = Publius|volume = 8|issue = 3|pages = 71–101|last1 = Riley|first1 = Patrick|year = 1978}}</ref>  He then briefly pursued a career as a conductor, studying at the Mozarteum in [[Salzburg]], Austria. Shortly thereafter, he earned his M.Phil at [[The London School of Economics]] under the supervision of [[Michael Oakeshott]].  In 1968, he received his Ph.D.<ref>http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/003903538/catalog</ref> from [[Harvard University]] where he studied under [[Louis Hartz]], [[John Rawls]] and [[Judith Shklar]].<ref>http://www.gov.harvard.edu/people/faculty/visiting-faculty-appointments/patrick-riley{{dead link|date=July 2016 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref> While at Harvard, Riley won the Bowdoin Essay Prize for Graduate Students in 1966<ref>http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/006753613/catalog</ref> and 1967.<ref>http://id.lib.harvard.edu/aleph/006753585/catalog</ref>  After teaching at Harvard for several years, he moved to teach at the University of Wisconsin-Madison, where he retired from in 2007 after 36 years of teaching. In October 2008, a General Will Symposium was held on campus to honor Riley's career, including numerous former colleagues and students from throughout his career,<ref>{{Cite web |url=https://alumni.polisci.wisc.edu/newsletters/pdf/20131111-151441.pdf |title=Archived copy |access-date=2015-02-25 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140310124409/https://alumni.polisci.wisc.edu/newsletters/pdf/20131111-151441.pdf |archive-date=2014-03-10 |url-status=dead }}</ref> which was subsequently published by [[Cambridge University Press]] as [http://www.cambridge.org/us/academic/subjects/politics-international-relations/political-theory/general-will-evolution-concept ''The General Will: The Evolution of a Concept''], edited by James Farr and David Lay Williams, and it features two of Riley's essays. In 2011, the ''Leibniz Review'' was dedicated to Riley's career and his numerous contributions to the journal over an extended period.<ref>http://www.leibnizreview.com</ref>  The volume included David Lay Williams's tribute to Riley's interpretation of Leibniz as a philosopher of love, as well as an account of Riley's own commitment to charity as a way of life.<ref>{{Cite journal|url=http://www.pdcnet.org/pdc/bvdb.nsf/purchase?openform&fp=leibniz&id=leibniz_2011_0021_0001_0008|doi = 10.5840/leibniz2011211|title = Patrick Riley's Leibniz|year = 2011|last1 = Williams|first1 = David Lay|journal = Leibniz Society Review|volume = 21|pages = 1–8}}</ref>  In retirement, he taught courses at Harvard near his home in [[Cambridge, Massachusetts]].
