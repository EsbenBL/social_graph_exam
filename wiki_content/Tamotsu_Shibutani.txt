'''Tamotsu Shibutani''' (15 October 1920 â€“ 8 August 2004) was a [[Japanese Americans|Japanese American]] sociologist working on the tradition of [[symbolic interactionism]].

==Biography==

Born in 1920, Shibutani majored in sociology and philosophy at the [[University of California at Berkeley]]. He was sent with his family to the [[Tule Lake War Relocation Center|Tule Lake internment camp]] in 1942 during [[World War II]].<ref name="encyclopedia"/><ref name=archives>{{cite web | title=Japanese American Internee Data File: Tamotsu Shibutani|url=https://aad.archives.gov/aad/record-detail.jsp?dt=3099&mtch=4&tf=F&q=Shibutani&bc=&rpp=10&pg=1&rid=77593&rlst=77594,77593,77595,77596|publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]]| accessdate=August 18, 2019}}</ref>

After the war, Shibutani obtained his doctorate at [[University of Chicago]], completing his degree in 1948. He taught there for a few years and then moved to the University of California at Berkeley. While at Chicago and Berkeley, he published two influential books: ''Improvised News: A Sociological Study of Rumor'' (1966) and ''The Derelicts of Company K: A Sociological Study of Demoralization'' (1978). He later was a professor in the Department of Sociology at the [[University of California, Santa Barbara]].<ref name="encyclopedia"/>

In 2004, Shibutani died at the age of 83 in [[Santa Barbara, California]].<ref name="encyclopedia">{{Cite web|url=http://encyclopedia.densho.org/Tamotsu_Shibutani/|title=Tamotsu Shibutani {{!}} Densho Encyclopedia|website=encyclopedia.densho.org|language=en|access-date=2017-03-31}}</ref>
