'''Nadia Elizabeth Brown'''<ref>{{cite web |title=Brown, Nadia E. |url=https://id.loc.gov/authorities/names/no2014054596.html |website=id.loc.gov |access-date=December 1, 2020}}</ref> is an [[America]]n [[political scientist]]. She is a University Scholar and professor of Political Science and [[African American Studies]] at [[Purdue University]], where she is also affiliated with the department of [[women's studies|Women's, Gender, and Sexuality Studies]]. In 2020 she was appointed Director of the Women's and Gender Studies Program at [[Georgetown University]], with a term starting in August 2021.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://college.georgetown.edu/news-story/nadia-brown-appointed-as-new-director-of-women-and-gender-studies-program/ |title=Nadia Brown Appointed as New Director of Women and Gender Studies Program |publisher=Georgetown College |date=4 May 2020 |access-date=4 May 2020}}</ref> Brown is a scholar of [[American politics]] whose work focuses on [[identity politics]], legislative studies, and Black women's studies, using the theory of [[intersectionality]] to study topics across multiple disciplines.

==Education and early career==
Brown studied political science at [[Howard University]], obtaining a BA in 2004. She chose to study political science because of an interest in how power is distributed in society, and particularly how Black women engage in political activity.<ref name=member>{{cite web |url=https://politicalsciencenow.com/member-of-the-month-dr-nadia-e-brown-purdue-university/ |title=APSA Member of the Month: Dr. Nadia E. Brown, Purdue University |date=19 April 2019 |publisher=American Political Science Association |work=Political Science Now}}</ref> In 2010 she completed her PhD in political science at [[Rutgers University]], specializing in Women and Politics and American Politics.<ref name=profile>{{cite web |url=https://cla.purdue.edu/directory/profiles/nadia-brown.html |title=Nadia E. Brown Profile |publisher=Purdue University |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> She also received a [[Graduate certificate#United States|Graduate Certificate]] in Women's and Gender Studies.<ref name=member/> From 2010 to 2013, she was a professor of political science and African American studies, and affiliated with women's studies, at [[St. Louis University]].<ref name=profile/>

==Career==
In 2014, Brown published the book ''Sisters in the Statehouse: Black Women and Legislative Decision Making'',<ref>{{cite web |url=https://womenalsoknowstuff.com/profile/nadia-brown |title=Nadia E. Brown |publisher=Women Also Know Stuff |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> which studies how the policy preferences and legislative behavior of African American women legislators are influenced by experiences with racism and sexism during their lives, given the context that in 2013, only 239 of the 7,776 female legislators in the United States were African American women.<ref name=odwyer14>{{cite web |url=https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/lsereviewofbooks/2014/08/14/book-review-sisters-in-the-statehouse-black-women-and-legislative-decision-making/ |title=Book Review: Sisters in the Statehouse: Black Women and Legislative Decision Making by Nadia E. Brown |date=14 August 2014 |access-date=15 January 2020 |work=London School of Economics Review of Books |author=Muireann O'Dwyer |publisher=London School of Economics}}</ref> The book was reviewed positively not just for its substantive findings, but also for its analytical approach, with Muireann O'Dwyer writing that it "delivered an answer to the enduring question of how exactly intersectionality can be brought to bear on the empirical questions of social science".<ref name=odwyer14/> ''Sisters in the Statehouse'' won the 2015 W.E.B. DuBois Distinguished Book Award, as well as awards from Purdue University and the Association for the Study of Black Women and Politics.<ref name=member/>

In 2016, Brown was appointed a University Scholar at Purdue University,<ref name=scholars>{{cite web |url=https://www.purdue.edu/provost/faculty/facultyScholars.html |title=University Faculty Scholars |publisher=Purdue University |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> an honor which is intended "to recognize outstanding mid-career faculty who are on an accelerated path for academic distinction".<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.purdue.edu/provost/faculty/facultyInitiatives/scholarsProgram.html |title=Scholars Program |access-date=15 January 2020 |publisher=Purdue University}}</ref> Brown was chosen as the [[American Political Science Association]]'s Member of the Month for April 2019.<ref name=member/>

Brown is the lead editor of the journal ''Politics, Groups, and Identities'', which she has noted may make her the first Black woman to be the sole lead editor of a political science journal.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.tandfonline.com/action/journalInformation?journalCode=rpgi20 |title=Politics, Groups, and Identities |publisher=Taylor & Francis Online |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> She has also been a member of the editorial board of the political science expert database [[Women Also Know Stuff]].<ref name=apsa16>{{cite web |url=https://politicalsciencenow.com/nadia-e-brown/ |title=Nadia E. Brown Encourages Women in Politics to Practice Mentorship & Self-Advocacy |date=17 May 2016 |publisher=American Political Science Association |work=Political Science Now}}</ref>

Brown is an advocate for political scientists to communicate about their discipline with the media.<ref name=apsa16/> She has published in ''[[The Washington Post]]'',<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2019/08/30/how-metoopolisci-collective-is-making-difference-political-science/|title=How the #MeTooPoliSci Collective is making a difference in political science|last=Brown|first=Nadia E.|date=30 August 2019|work=The Washington Post|access-date=15 January 2020|url-status=live}}</ref><ref>{{cite news |title=Virginia Democrats' political problems show us why intersectionality is so important |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/localopinions/virginia-democrats-political-problems-show-us-why-intersectionality-is-so-important/2019/03/04/25730e7c-3e86-11e9-922c-64d6b7840b82_story.html |last1=Brown |first1=Nadia E. |first2=Melissa R. |last2=Michelson |author-link2=Melissa R. Michelson |first3=Libby |last3=Sharrow |first4=Dara |last4=Strolovitch |date=4 March 2019 |access-date=15 January 2020 |work=The Washington Post}}</ref> the ''[[Huffington Post]]'',<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.huffpost.com/entry/experts-weigh-in-women-al_b_9404388 |title=Experts Weigh In: Women Also Know Stuff |work=Huffington Post |display-authors=1 |author1=Emily Beaulieu |author2=Amber Boydstun |date=8 March 2016 |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> and ''[[Ozy (magazine)|Ozy]]'',<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.ozy.com/opinion/making-black-women-not-girls-magic/89432/ |title=Making Black women, not girls, magic? |first1=Nadia E. |last1=Brown |first2=Aria S. |last2=Halliday |work=OZY magazine |date=10 October 2018 |access-date=15 January 2020}}</ref> and been cited in outlets like ''[[The New York Times]]''<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2019/02/06/us/politics/vanessa-tyson-justin-fairfax.html |title=Vanessa Tyson, Accuser of Lt.
