{{nihongo|'''Hirofumi Uzawa'''|宇沢 弘文|Uzawa Hirofumi|extra= July 21, 1928 – September 18, 2014}} was a [[Japanese people|Japanese]] [[economist]].

==Biography==
Uzawa was born on July 21, 1928 in [[Yonago, Tottori]] to a farming family.

He attended the Tokyo First Middle School (currently the [[Hibiya High School]] ) and the First Higher School, Japan (now the University of Tokyo's College of Arts and Sciences faculty).{{citation needed|date=March 2018}}
 
He graduated from the Mathematics Department of the [[University of Tokyo]] in 1951; he was a special research student from 1951 to 1953. At that time, he discovered the true nature of economics in the words of [[John Ruskin]], “There is no wealth, but life.” which was quoted in the foreword to {{Nihongo|''Tale of Poverty''|貧乏物語|binbō monogatari}} by [[Hajime Kawakami]], and decided to study economics.<ref name="2009lect-uzawa" />

A paper on [[Decentralized planning (economics)|decentralized economic planning]] written by him caught the eye of [[Kenneth Arrow]] at the [[Stanford University]], he went to study Economics at Stanford University in 1956 with [[Fulbright Program|Fulbright fellowship]], and became a [[research assistant]], then [[assistant professor]]  in 1956, then [[assistant professor]] at the [[University of California, Berkeley]] in 1960, and then [[associate professor]] at Stanford in 1961.<ref name="2009lect-uzawa" />  Meanwhile, in 1962, he received a [[Ph.D.]] from [[Tohoku University]].<ref>{{Cite thesis|url=http://ci.nii.ac.jp/naid/500000318269?l=en |publisher=[[CiNii]]|title=CiNii Dissertations - レオン・ワルラスの一般均衡理論に関する諸研究 |access-date= 22 May 2016|year=1962|type=PhD Thesis|last1=宇沢|first1=弘文}}</ref> He afterwards was [[professor]] at the [[University of Chicago]] in 1964, and later assumed the position of professor of the Department of Economics at the University of Tokyo in 1969. He also taught at [[Niigata University]], [[Chuo University]], and [[United Nations University]].<ref name="AS-obit">{{cite news|url=http://ajw.asahi.com/article/behind_news/social_affairs/AJ201409260062|title=Leading Japanese economist Hirofumi Uzawa dies at 86|date=26 September 2014|publisher=[[Asahi Shimbun]]|access-date=27 September 2014|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141014072808/http://ajw.asahi.com/article/behind_news/social_affairs/AJ201409260062|archive-date=14 October 2014}}</ref> [[Joseph E. Stiglitz]] and [[George A. Akerlof]] did research under Uzawa at the [[University of Chicago]] and David Cass studied under Uzawa at Stanford University.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/economics/laureates/2001/stiglitz-autobio.html |title=Joseph E. Stiglitz: The Sveriges Riksbank Prize in Economic Sciences in Memory of Alfred Nobel 2001 |publisher=Nobelprize.org |access-date=May 12, 2016}}</ref><ref name="2009lect-uzawa" />

Uzawa was a [[senior fellow]] at the social, commonness, and capital research center of [[Doshisha University]]. He held the position of the president of the [[Econometric Society]] from 1976 to 1977. He also held the position of Counsel for the [[Development Bank of Japan]]'s Research Institute of Capital Formation (RICF) from 1968 until his passing.<ref>{{Cite web | url=http://www.dbj.jp/ricf/information/distinguished.html | title=下村治博士・宇沢弘文先生について &#124; 研究所案内 &#124; 設備投資研究所 &#124; 日本政策投資銀行（Dbj）}}</ref>

== Contributions ==
Uzawa initiated the field of [[mathematical economics]] in postwar days and formulated the [[growth theory]] of [[neoclassical economics]]. This is reflected in the [[Uzawa–Lucas model]], the [[Uzawa iteration]], the [[Uzawa condition]], and [[Uzawa's Theorem]], among others.

In his 1962 paper,<ref>{{Cite book|title=The new Palgrave dictionary of economics|date=2008|publisher=Palgrave Macmillan|others=Durlauf, Steven N., Blume, Lawrence.|isbn=9780333786765|edition= 2nd|location=Basingstoke, Hampshire|pages=608|oclc=181424188}}</ref> Uzawa proved that the two of [[Competitive equilibrium|Walrasian equilibrium]] and [[Brouwer fixed-point theorem|Brouwer's fixed-point theorem]] are equivalent.
