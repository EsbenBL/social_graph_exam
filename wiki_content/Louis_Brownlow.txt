'''Louis Brownlow ''' (August 29, 1879 – September 27, 1963) was an [[United States|American]] author, [[political scientist]], and [[consultant]] in the area of [[public administration]]. As chairman of the Committee on Administrative Management (better known as the [[Brownlow Committee]]) in 1937, he co-authored a report which led to passage of the [[Reorganization Act of 1939]] and the creation of the [[Executive Office of the President]].<ref>Mosher, ''American Public Administration: Past, Present, Future,'' 1975, p. 72–73; Dickinson, ''Bitter Harvest: FDR, Presidential Power, and the Growth of the Presidential Branch,'' 1999, p. 112.</ref> While chairing the Committee on Administrative Management, Brownlow called several of [[President of the United States|President]] [[Franklin D. Roosevelt]]'s advisors men with "a passion for anonymity"—which later became a popular phrase.<ref name="Obit">"Louis Brownlow, 84, Writer on Politics." ''[[The New York Times|New York Times]].'' September 28, 1963.</ref><ref>Safire, ''Safire's Political Dictionary,'' 2008, p. 523; "Louis Brownlow," ''Washington Post,'' September 30, 1963.</ref>

==Early life and career==
Louis Brownlow was born in [[Buffalo, Missouri]], in August 1879.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair89">Trahair, ''From Aristotelian to Reaganomics: A Dictionary of Eponyms With Biographies in the Social Science,'' 1994, p. 89.</ref><ref name="Karl512" /> His parents were Robert Sims and Ruth Amis Brownlow.<ref name="WPObit" /> His father had been a soldier in the [[Confederate States Army]], serving in the [[Tennessee]], [[Missouri]], and [[Arkansas]] area, and had been wounded in the hip by a [[minié ball]].<ref name="Karl512">Karl, "Louis Brownlow," ''Public Administration Review,'' November–December 1979, p. 512.</ref><ref name="Bruce" /> His parents, each of whom had taught school at some time, moved from [[Giles County, Tennessee]], to Missouri some time between 1877 and 1879 after Robert Brownlow was appointed postmaster for the town of Buffalo.<ref name="Karl512" /><ref name="Stillman">Stillman, ''The Rise of the City Manager: A Public Professional in Local Government,'' 1974, p. 47.</ref> Louis was frequently ill as a child, and educated at home.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair89" /><ref name="Stillman" /> He was unable to attend college due to his family's poverty, but read books extensively.<ref name="Karl512" /><ref name="Stillman" />

In 1900, Brownlow was hired by the ''Nashville Banner'',<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Bruce" /> and over the next several years wrote for the ''Louisville Courier-Journal'', ''Louisville Times'', and several other newspapers in Tennessee as well.<ref name="Obit" /><ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Bruce" /> He also worked for the Haskin Syndicate as a political writer and later as a correspondent in [[Europe]], the [[Middle East]], and the [[Far East]] from 1906 to 1915.<ref name="Obit" /><ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair89" /> He [[ghostwriter|ghost-wrote]] Haskin's 1911 book ''The American Government,'' which was an influential treatise on [[Progressivism in the United States|Progressive]] ideas about public administration.<ref name="Karl512" />

He married the former Elizabeth Sims (daughter of Congressman [[Thetus W. Sims]]) in December 1909.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Bruce">Bruce, Tyler, and Morton, ''History of Virginia,'' 1924, p. 18.</ref><ref>Sims, ''The Pariss Sims Family and Related Families, 1765–1965,'' 1965, p. 29; "Thetus W. Sims, 87, Long in Congress," ''New York Times,'' December 18, 1939.</ref> The couple had no children.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Karl512" /> Brownlow was a member of the [[Democratic Party (United States)|Democratic Party]] and a [[Methodism|Methodist]], and belonged to the [[Cosmos Club]] and [[National Press Club (USA)|National Press Club]].<ref name="Bruce" />

==Political and academic career==
[[File:1721 19th Street, N.W..JPG|thumb|Brownlow's former residence in the [[Dupont Circle, Washington, D.C.|Dupont Circle]] neighborhood of Washington, D.C.]]
Brownlow came to Washington, D.C., as a reporter for two Tennessee newspapers, and made the acquaintance of President [[Theodore Roosevelt]].<ref name="International320">"Deaths," ''International Institute of Administrative Sciences,'' 1963, p. 320.</ref> He caught the attention of President [[Woodrow Wilson]] in 1914 after being one of the few newspaper reporters to correctly predict that the [[German Empire]] would go to war with [[Serbia]] over the assassination of [[Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria|Archduke Franz Ferdinand]] (which caused the start of [[World War I]]).<ref name="WPObit" /> Expressing a desire to put into practice many of the administrative practices he had reported on from Europe,<ref name="International320" /> Brownlow sought and won from President Wilson appointment in 1915 as a commissioner of the [[Washington, D.C.|District of Columbia]], serving until 1920.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair89" /><ref name="Karl512" /><ref>The [[Constitution of the United States]] gives the [[United States Congress]] the authority to govern the District of Columbia. Between 1874 and 1952, Congress delegated that authority to a three-person Board of Commissioners.  One of the Commissioners would serve as President of the Board.  See: Chiorazzi and Most, ''Prestatehood Legal Materials: A Fifty-State Research Guide, Including New York City and the District of Columbia,'' 2006, pp. 234—235; Crew, Webb, and Wooldridge, ''Centennial History of the City of Washington, D.C.,'' 1892, p. 159.</ref> From 1917 to 1920, he was president of the commissioners, and a vocal proponent of [[District of Columbia home rule|home rule]].<ref name="Obit" /><ref name="WPObit" /> During this period, the [[Metropolitan Police Department of the District of Columbia]] [[trade union|unionized]], and Brownlow supported its unionization (although not its affiliation with the [[American Federation of Labor]]).<ref>"Police Unions in Thirty-Seven Cities," ''New York Times,'' September 14, 1919.</ref> He helped guide the city through the [[1918 flu pandemic]], closing schools and businesses and banning all public gatherings.<ref>Persico, "The Great Swine Flu Epidemic of 1918," ''American Heritage,'' March 1976.</ref> He also served on the District of Columbia Public Utilities Commission and the District Zoning Commission from 1917 to 1919.<ref name="WPObit" /> He was City Manager of [[Petersburg, Virginia]], from 1920 to 1923; City Manager of [[Knoxville, Tennessee]], from 1924 to 1926; and City Manager of [[Radburn, New Jersey]], from 1927 to 1931.<ref name="Obit" /><ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair89" /><ref name="Karl512" /> He briefly worked for the ''United States Daily'' newspaper in 1927.<ref name="WPObit" /> He was a consultant to the City Housing Corporation in New York City from 1928 to 1931, and was elected a director of the corporation in 1931.<ref>"Elects Louis Brownlow," ''New York Times,'' March 19, 1931.</ref>

Brownlow began teaching political science at the [[University of Chicago]] in 1931, and later that year was appointed director of the Public Administration Clearing House (which he had helped organize in 1930) at the university.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Karl512" /><ref name="Trahair90">Trahair, ''From Aristotelian to Reaganomics: A Dictionary of Eponyms With Biographies in the Social Science,'' 1994, pp. 89–90.</ref><ref>"Agency Aims to Aid All Governments," ''New York Times,'' December 28, 1930.</ref> He remained the Clearing House's director until 1945.<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Trahair90" /><ref name="Relyea478">Relyea, ''The Executive Office of the President: A Historical, Biographical, and Bibliographical Guide,'' 1997, p. 478.</ref> Brownlow became chairman of the Committee for Public Administration of the [[Social Science Research Council]] in 1933, where he worked to bridge the gap between academics and practitioners.<ref name="International320" /> He was also chairman of the National Institute of Public Affairs from 1934 to 1949.<ref name="Relyea478" />

===Brownlow Committee===
On March 22, 1936, Roosevelt established the Committee on Administrative Management (commonly known as the [[Brownlow Committee]]) and charged it with developing proposals for reorganizing the executive branch.<ref>"President Orders Own Survey to Cut New Deal Activity," ''New York Times,'' March 23, 1936; Calabresi and Yoo, ''The Unitary Executive: Presidential Power From Washington to Bush,'' 2008, pp. 291–301; see, generally, Karl, ''Executive Reorganization and Reform in the New Deal: The Genesis of Administrative Management, 1900–1939,'' 1963.</ref> The three-person committee consisted of Louis Brownlow, [[Charles Edward Merriam|Charles Merriam]], and [[Luther Gulick (social scientist)|Luther Gulick]].<ref>Dickinson, ''Bitter Harvest: FDR, Presidential Power, and the Growth of the Presidential Branch,'' 1999, p. 93; Felbinger and Haynes, ''Outstanding Women in Public Administration: Leaders, Mentors, and Pioneers,'' 2004, p. 13.</ref><ref name="Shafritz28">Shafritz, "The Brownlow Committee," in ''The Dictionary of Public Policy and Administration,'' 2004, p. 28.</ref>

On January 10, 1937, the Committee released its report.<ref>Catledge, "Capitol Startled," ''New York Times,'' January 13, 1937; Dickinson, ''Bitter Harvest: FDR, Presidential Power, and the Growth of the Presidential Branch,'' 1999, p. 110.</ref> Famously declaring "The President needs help,"<ref name="Shafritz28" /><ref>U.S. President's Committee on Administrative Management, ''Report of the President's Committee,'' 1937, p. 5; Rudolph and Rudolph, ''Making U.S. Foreign Policy Toward South Asia,'' 2008, p. 230; Maisel, ''The Parties Respond: Changes in American Parties and Campaigns,'' 2002, p. 277.</ref><ref name="Ruda43">Rudalevige, ''The New Imperial Presidency: Renewing Presidential Power After Watergate,'' 2005, p. 43.</ref> the Committee's report advocated a strong chief executive, including among its 37 recommendations significant expansion of the presidential staff, integration of managerial agencies into a single presidential office, expansion of the merit system, integration of all independent agencies into existing [[United States Cabinet|Cabinet]] departments, and modernization of federal accounting and financial practices.<ref name="Ruda43" /><ref>Dickinson, ''Bitter Harvest: FDR, Presidential Power, and the Growth of the Presidential Branch,'' 1999, p. 104-11; Sundquist, ''The Decline and Resurgence of Congress,'' 1981, pp. 49–50; Calabresi and Yoo, ''The Unitary Executive: Presidential Power From Washington to Bush,'' 2008, pp. 292–295.</ref>

While he was a member of the Committee on Administrative Management, Brownlow was named an official delegate to the Sixth International Congress of Administrative Sciences in [[Warsaw, Poland]].<ref name="International320" /> Although he left government service after the termination of the Committee, Brownlow continued to be an advisor to presidents Franklin Roosevelt and [[Harry S. Truman]].<ref name="International320" /> He left that position in 1939.<ref name="Relyea478" />

Brownlow received an honorary Doctor of Law degree from [[American University]] in 1938.<ref name="WPObit" /> He suffered a heart attack in December of that year.<ref>Relyea, ''The Executive Office of the President: An Historical Overview,'' 2008, p. CRS-7.</ref>

===Post-Brownlow Committee===
Brownlow helped co-found the [[American Society for Public Administration]] in 1940, serving in various executive and advisory capacities to it until 1945.<ref name="International321">"Deaths," ''International Institute of Administrative Sciences,'' 1963, p. 321.</ref> Brownlow was also director of the Franklin D. Roosevelt Memorial Foundation in 1947,<ref>"Roosevelt Memorial Set," ''New York Times,'' March 31, 1947.</ref> and director of the [[Woodrow Wilson Foundation]] from 1948 to 1953.<ref name="International321" /> He retired from the University of Chicago in 1949,<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="International320" /><ref name="Relyea478" /> and served as a visiting professor at the [[University of Washington]] in 1957<ref name="WPObit" /><ref name="Relyea478" /> and the [[Maxwell School of Citizenship and Public Affairs]] at Syracuse in 1958 and 1959.<ref name="Relyea478" /><ref name="International321" /><ref>"Syracuse Professor Named," ''New York Times,'' February 16, 1958.</ref>

==Death==
Louis Brownlow died in [[Arlington, Virginia]], in September 1963 after delivering a speech at the Army Navy Country Club.<ref name="Obit" /> The cause of death was a [[Myocardial infarction|heart attack]].<ref name="WPObit">"Louis Brownlow, Ex-Commissioner of D.C., Dies at 84," ''Washington Post,'' September 28, 1963.</ref> A memorial service was held at St. Thomas Episcopal Church.<ref>"Obituary," ''Washington Post,'' September 29, 1963.</ref>

==Honors named for Brownlow==
Since 1968, the [[National Academy of Public Administration (United States)|National Academy of Public Administration]] has recognized outstanding contributions to the literature of public administration through presentation of the Louis Brownlow Book Award.<ref>"The Louis Brownlow Book Award: 2009 Nominations," National Academy of Public Administration, no date.</ref> The award is given to a book published in the previous two years which has made an exceptional contribution to the study of governmental institutions or public administration problems.

The [[American Society for Public Administration]] also bestows its Louis Brownlow Award on the best article written by a public administrator to appear in the journal ''[[Public Administration Review]]'' in the past year.<ref>"ASPA's Awards Program," American Society for Public Administration, no date.</ref>
