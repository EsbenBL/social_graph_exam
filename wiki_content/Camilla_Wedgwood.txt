'''Camilla Hildegarde Wedgwood''' (25 March 1901 – 17 May 1955) was a British [[anthropology|anthropologist]] and [[academic administrator]]. She is best known for her research in the Pacific and her pioneering role as one of the [[Commonwealth of Nations|British Commonwealth]]'s first female anthropologists.

==Early life and education==
Wedgwood was born on 25 March 1901 in [[Newcastle upon Tyne]], England.<ref name="ODNB">{{cite ODNB|last1=Maddock|first1=Kenneth|last2=Wetherell|first2=David|title=Wedgwood, Camilla Hildegarde (1901–1955)|url=http://www.oxforddnb.com/view/article/65556|accessdate=26 February 2017|date=May 2009|doi=10.1093/ref:odnb/65556}}</ref> Her father was [[Josiah Wedgwood, 1st Baron Wedgwood|Josiah Wedgwood]] later the first Baron Wedgwood. Her mother, Ethel Bowen Wedgwood, was the daughter of a Lord Justice of Appeal, [[Charles Bowen, 1st Baron Bowen|Charles Bowen]]. She was a member of the extensive [[Darwin–Wedgwood family|Wedgwood family]].<ref name="Who Was Who">{{cite web|title=WEDGWOOD, Hon. Camilla Hildegarde|url=http://www.ukwhoswho.com/view/article/oupww/whowaswho/U244277|website=Who Was Who|publisher=Oxford University Press|accessdate=26 February 2017|date=April 2014}}</ref> Her parents separated in 1914 and divorced in 1919.<ref name="ADNB">{{cite book|last1=Wetherell|first1=David|title=Australian Dictionary of Biography, Volume 16|date=2002|publisher=Manchester University Press|location=Manchester|url=http://adb.anu.edu.au/biography/wedgwood-camilla-hildegarde-11992|accessdate=26 February 2017|chapter=Wedgwood, Camilla Hildegarde (1901–1955)}}</ref>

Wedgwood was educated at two [[Independent school (United Kingdom)|independent schools]]: [[Newcastle-under-Lyme School|Orme Girls' School]] in [[Newcastle-under-Lyme]], Staffordshire, and at [[Bedales School]] in [[Steep, Hampshire|Steep]], Hampshire.<ref name="ODNB" /> She studied at [[Bedford College, London|Bedford College]], London and at [[Newnham College, Cambridge]].<ref name="Who Was Who" /> At the [[University of Cambridge]], she studied for both the English and anthropology [[Tripos]].<ref name="ODNB" /> She completed both, leaving with [[first class honours]] but no degree (women were not awarded degrees by Cambridge until 1948).<ref name="ODNB" /> She was awarded [[Master of Arts (Oxbridge and Dublin)|Master of Arts status]] by Cambridge in 1927.<ref name="Who Was Who" /> She studied under [[Bronisław Malinowski]] at Bedford College and [[Alfred Cort Haddon]] at Cambridge.<ref name="ODNB" />

==Career==
After leaving the University of Cambridge, Wedgwood returned to [[Bedford College, London|Bedford College]], London as an assistant lecturer in its Department of Social Studies.<ref name="ODNB" /><ref name="ADNB" /> After [[Bernard Deacon (anthropologist)|Arthur Bernard Deacon]] death in 1927, she was invited to move to the [[University of Sydney]] to replace him as lecturer in anthropology.<ref name="ODNB" /><ref name="Who Was Who" /> She was also asked by [[Alfred Radcliffe-Brown]] to edit Deacon's remaining field notes in preparation for publication:<ref name="ODNB" /> they were published as "Malekula: A Vanishing People in the New Hebrides" in 1934.<ref>{{cite news|last1=Marett|first1=Robert Ranulph|title=A Vanishing People|work=The Times Literary Supplement|issue=1696|date=2 August 1934|page=536}}</ref> In 1930, she held a temporary lectureship in the Department of African Life and Languages at the University of Cape Town.<ref name="Who Was Who" /> From 1930 to 1932, having returned to England, she was a lecturer at the [[London School of Economics]] and personal assistant to [[Bronisław Malinowski]].<ref name="ODNB" /><ref name="ADNB" />

In 1932, Wedgwood was awarded a fellowship by the [[Australian Research Council]] to conduct fieldwork on Manam Island off the north coast of [[Papua New Guinea]] on the border of what are today Madang and East Sepik provinces.<ref name="Who Was Who" /><ref name="ADNB" /> Also in 1932, she became a Member of Council of the [[Royal Anthropological Institute]].<ref name="Who Was Who" /> In June 1935, she was appointed [[Principal (academia)|Principal]] of [[The Women's College, University of Sydney|The Women's College]], University of Sydney.<ref name="ADNB" /> During this time, she became an active member of Sydney [[High society (social class)|high society]].<ref name="ADNB" /> She left the appointment in 1944 to join the military.<ref name="Who Was Who" />

During [[World War II]], Wedgwood was involved in formulating policy on education and administration in [[Papua New Guinea]].<ref name="ODNB" /> Having renounced her [[pacifism]], she volunteered for the [[Australian Army Medical Women's Service]] and was [[commissioned officer|commissioned]] as a [[Temporary rank|temporary]] [[lieutenant colonel]] in January 1944.<ref name="Who Was Who" /><ref name="ADNB" /> After two years of service, she was demobilised in 1946.<ref name="Who Was Who" />

After the war Wedgwood took a position at the [[Australian School of Pacific Administration]], which was responsible for training Australian colonial officers and administrators.<ref name="ADNB" /> She continued in this role until her death. She had developed lung cancer after being a life-long heavy smoker, and died on 17 May 1955 at [[Royal North Shore Hospital]], Sydney.<ref name="ADNB" />

Wedgwood Close in the Canberra suburb of [[Chisholm, Australian Capital Territory|Chisholm]] is named in her honour.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Schedule 'B' National Memorials Ordinance 1928–1972 Street Nomenclature List of Additional Names with Reference to Origin: Commonwealth of Australia Gazette. Special (National: 1977–2012) – 8 Feb 1978|url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article240628906|last=|first=|date=|website=Trove|page=14|language=en|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=2020-04-02}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Wedgwood was a member of the [[Religious Society of Friends]] (the Quakers) and as such was a [[pacifist]]: during the Second World War she was the president of the [[Federal Pacifist Council]].<ref>{{cite news |url=http://nla.gov.au/nla.news-article74627996 |title=PACIFISM AND PEACE |newspaper=[[The Advertiser (Adelaide)]] |location=South Australia |date=3 June 1943 |accessdate=6 August 2021 |page=4 |via=National Library of Australia}}</ref> However, she was increasingly attracted to Anglicanism during her time in Australia and particularly to the [[Anglo-Catholic]] wing of the church. She was received into the [[Church of England in Australia]] in 1944.<ref name="ODNB" />
