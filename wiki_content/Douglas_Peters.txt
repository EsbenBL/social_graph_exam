'''Douglas Dennison Peters''', {{Post-nominals|country=CAN|PC}} (March 3, 1930 – October 7, 2016) was a [[Canadians|Canadian]] [[banker]], [[economist]], and [[politician]].

==Life and career==
Peters was born in [[Brandon, Manitoba]], the son of Mary Gladys (née Dennison) and Dr. Wilfrid Seymour Peters.<ref name=ohwl>{{cite book |last=Lumley |first=Elizabeth |date=2004 |title=Canadian Who's Who 2004 |publisher=University of Toronto Press |pages=1030 |isbn=0-8020-8892-9}}</ref> In 1954, he married Audrey Catherine Clark (December 2, 1928 – August 2, 2007).<ref name=lew>{{cite web |title=Mrs. Audrey Catherine Clark Peters Dec. 2, 1928 - Aug. 2, 2007 |publisher=The DOWSWELL Family of North America |date=2007 |url=http://dowswell.heritagedb.org/d5.htm#i30547 |access-date=2010-09-03 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110726145412/http://dowswell.heritagedb.org/d5.htm#i30547 |archive-date=2011-07-26 }}</ref> He had two children, including professor [[David Wilfrid Peters]], and two grandchildren, including actor [[Keir Gilchrist]].<ref name=lew/>

He received a Bachelor of Commerce degree from [[Queen's University at Kingston|Queen's University]] in 1963 and a Ph. D. from the [[Wharton School]] at [[University of Pennsylvania]] in 1969 where he was classmates with two other well-known Canadian economists, [[Arthur Donner]] and [[Robert Rabinovitch]].

After serving as chief economist and senior vice-president of the [[Toronto-Dominion Bank]], Peters entered politics in the [[1993 Canadian federal election|1993 election]]. He was elected as the [[Liberal Party of Canada|Liberal]] [[Member of Parliament (Canada)|Member of Parliament]] for [[Scarborough East]]. [[Prime Minister of Canada|Prime Minister]] [[Jean Chrétien]] appointed Peters to the position of [[Secretary of State (Canada)|Secretary of State]] for International Financial Institutions too. Peters retired from politics at the [[1997 Canadian federal election|1997 election]].

In 1979, Peters and [[Arthur Donner]] wrote a book titled ''The Monetarist Counter-revolution: A Critique of Canadian Monetary Policy, 1975-1979''. Douglas Peters and [[David Wilfrid Peters]] authored an article titled "Reforming Canada's Financial Services Sector: What Needs to Follow from Bill C8", that appeared in the December 2001 issue of the ''Canadian Public Policy'' journal.

According to author [[Linda McQuaig]], Peters took a [[Keynesian]] economic prescription to government, and decided to leave politics when he found that his views were largely ignored.
