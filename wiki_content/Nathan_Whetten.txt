'''Nathan Laselle Whetten''' (1900–1984) was an American academic who served as professor of [[sociology]] (1932–1971) and dean of the Graduate School at the [[University of Connecticut]] (1940–1970).<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|last=Roy|first=Mark J.|date=1998-02-16|title=A Piece of UConn History/Whetten Graduate Center|url=http://www.advance.uconn.edu/1998/980216/021698hs.htm|access-date=2020-09-15|website=UConn Advance}}</ref> UConn's Nathan L. Whetten Graduate School was named in his honor in 1971.<ref>{{Cite web|last=University of Connecticut|title=Nathan L. Whetten Graduate Center Mobile Map|url=https://maps.uconn.edu/m/info/WGC|access-date=2020-09-15|website=maps.uconn.edu}}</ref> 

== Biography ==
Whetten was born on July 20, 1900 in Colonia García, a [[Mormons|Mormon]] colony in [[Chihuahua (state)|Chihuahua]], [[Mexico]]. His [[Anglo-Americans|Anglo-American]] parents ran a cattle ranch. They moved back to [[Arizona]] in 1912 during the [[Mexican Revolution]], returning to Mexico in 1914, where Whetten attended high school in [[Ciudad Juárez]].<ref name=":0" /> 

Whetten earned his bachelor's degree in languages in 1926 and his master's degree in sociology in 1928, both from [[Brigham Young University]]. After teaching at BYU and the [[University of Minnesota]], he earned his doctorate in sociology from [[Harvard University]] in 1932.<ref name=":4">{{Cite book|last=Nelson|first=Lowry|title=Rural Sociology: Its Origin and Growth in the United States.|publisher=University of Minnesota Press|year=1969|isbn=9780816605323|location=Minneapolis}}</ref> In the fall of 1932, Whetten began his postdoctoral career as a statistician in the University of Connecticut's sociology department, then part of the Storrs [[Agricultural experiment station|Agricultural Experiment Station]]. He led the first study of suburbanization in Connecticut, focusing on the communities of [[Windsor, Connecticut|Windsor]], [[Wilton, Connecticut|Wilton]] and [[Norwich, Connecticut|Norwich]].<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|date=1984-06-26|title=Obituaries: Nathan L. Whetten|url=https://www.upi.com/Archives/1984/06/26/Obituaries/6974457070400/|access-date=2020-09-15|website=UPI|language=en}}</ref> He was promoted to assistant professor of [[rural sociology]] in 1935 and to full professor and sociology department chair in 1938.<ref>{{Cite web|last=University of Connecticut Archives and Special Collections|date=2012|title=Collection: Nathan L. Whetten Papers|url=https://archivessearch.lib.uconn.edu/repositories/2/resources/747|access-date=2020-09-15|website=archivessearch.lib.uconn.edu}}</ref> Whetten also served as an editor of [[Rural Sociology (journal)|''Rural Sociology'']] and president of the [[Eastern Sociological Society]] and of the New England Conference on Graduate Education.<ref name=":1">{{Cite journal|last=University of Connecticut|date=1969-03-20|title=Nathan L. Whetten|url=http://hdl.handle.net/11134/20004:20074679|journal=The President's Newsletter|volume=7|issue=10|pages=1|via=Connecticut Digital Archive (CTDA)}}</ref>

Starting in the 1940s, Whetten's research interests shifted toward [[Latin American studies]]. From 1942 to 1945, he and his family lived in [[Mexico City]], where he was on temporary assignment to the [[United States Department of State]] and the [[Foreign Agricultural Service|Office of Foreign Agricultural Relations]].<ref name=":3">{{Cite news|date=2008-08-08|title=Whetten, Theora|work=The Hartford Courant|url=https://www.courant.com/news/connecticut/hc-xpm-2004-08-08-0408070721-story.html|access-date=2020-09-14}}</ref> He was charged with studying rural society in countries that provided raw materials (e.g., rubber) for the US war effort.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Delpar|first=Helen|title=Looking South: The Evolution of Latin Americanist Scholarship in the United States, 1850-1975|publisher=University of Alabama Press|year=2008|isbn=9780817354640|location=Tuscaloosa, AL|pages=127}}</ref> He later conducted multi-month research trips to Guatelama in 1944, 1952, and 1955. ''Guatemala: The Land and the People'' (1961) and ''Rural Mexico'' (1948) were products of these expeditions.<ref name=":4" />

In 1940, [[Albert N. Jorgensen]] appointed Whetten the first Dean of the Graduate School, succeeding [[George C. White]] as coordinator of [[postgraduate education]]. As dean, Whetten oversaw all graduate degrees and courses offered by hundreds of faculty and dozens of departments. During his three-decade tenure, the Graduate School grew from nine master's and doctoral degree recipients in 1941 to 1075 in 1970.<ref name=":0" /> He retired on July 1, 1970.<ref name=":1" /> In November 1971, UConn's Board of Trustees voted to name the center for Whetten—the first time a building was named for a living person.<ref name=":0" />

Whetten married Theora Lucile Johnson (1904–2004) of [[Salt Lake City]] in 1926. They met in college.<ref name=":3" /> Whetten lived in Storrs from 1932 until he died at a care home in [[Willimantic, Connecticut]], on June 26, 1984. He was 83 years old. He was survived by his wife and two sons, Nathan and John.<ref name=":2" />

In 1998, Theora Whetten donated 24 acres of undeveloped land to [[Joshua's Tract Conservation and Historic Trust|Joshua's Trust]], which now forms part of the Nate and Theora Whetten Woods nature preserve.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Nate & Theora Whetten Woods – Joshua's Trust|url=https://joshuastrust.org/nate-theora-whetten-woods-2/|access-date=2020-09-15|language=en-US}}</ref> She also created the [[Homer D. Babbidge Library|Homer Babbidge Library]]'s Whetten Endowment to purchase research materials on Latin America.<ref name=":3" />

== Publications ==

* ''Guatemala: The Land and the People'' (Yale University Press, 1961)
* ''Rural Mexico'' (University of Chicago Press, 1948; translated into Spanish in 1953)
* With [[Henry Riecken|Henry W. Riecken]], ''Rural Social Organization in Litchfield County, Connecticut'' (Storrs Agricultural Experiment Station, 1948)
* With Arnold W. Green, ''Ethnic Group Relations in a Rural Area of Connecticut'' (Storrs Agricultural Experiment Station, 1943)
* With Henry W. Riecken, ''The Foreign-Born Population of Connecticut'' (Storrs Agricultural Experiment Station, 1940)
* With [[Carle C. Zimmerman]], ''Rural Families on Relief'' ([[Works Progress Administration]], 1938)
* With Edward C. Devereux and Raymond F. Field, ''Studies of Suburbanization in Connecticut'' (Storrs Agricultural Experiment Station, 1936–1939)
* With Victor A. Rapport, ''The Recreational Uses of Land in Connecticut'' (Storrs Agricultural Experiment Station, 1934)
