'''Cornelius O'Leary''' (30 September 1927 â€“ 7 September 2006) was an [[Irish people|Irish]] [[historian]] and [[political scientist]].

O'Leary was born in Limerick but was raised in [[Cork (city)|Cork]],<ref name="News">{{cite news|url=http://www.irishnews.com/searchlog.asp?reason=denied_empty&script_name=/pageacc.asp&path_info=/pageacc.asp&tser1=ser&par=ben&sid=535482|title=Obituary - Intellectual who was also willing to banter|date=2006-09-18|work=Irish News|page=20|accessdate=2008-08-21|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303170242/http://www.irishnews.com/searchlog.asp?reason=denied_empty&script_name=%2Fpageacc.asp&path_info=%2Fpageacc.asp&tser1=ser&par=ben&sid=535482|archive-date=2016-03-03|url-status=dead}}</ref> where he attended University College Cork, gaining a [[British undergraduate degree classification|first-class]] [[honours degree]] in [[history]] and [[Latin]] in 1949.<ref name="Guardian">{{cite news|url=https://www.theguardian.com/news/2006/oct/12/guardianobituaries.obituaries|title=Obituary: Cornelius O'Leary|last=Crick|first=Bernard|authorlink=Bernard Crick|date=2006-10-12|work=The Guardian|page=36|accessdate=2008-08-21}}</ref> He subsequently studied for a [[Doctor of Philosophy|DPhil]] at [[Nuffield College, Oxford]] as the first student to be supervised by the [[psephology|psephologist]] [[David Butler (academic)|David Butler]].<ref name="Times">{{cite news|url=http://www.irishtimes.com/newspaper/obituaries/2006/0923/1158590926487.html|title=Cork academic with deep religious belief|date=2006-09-23|work=The Irish Times|page=14|accessdate=2008-08-21}}</ref> While researching his PhD, he worked at a number of [[secondary school]]s in [[London]].

His thesis was published as ''The Elimination of Corrupt Practices in British Elections, 1868-1911'' ([[Oxford University Press|Clarendon Press]], 1962) and in the same year he was appointed [[lecturer]] at [[Queen's University Belfast]] in [[Northern Ireland]].<ref name="Guardian"/> He was made [[professor]] of [[political science]] in 1979, having been controversially denied such a post previously.<ref name="Guardian"/> He was the first [[Catholic]] to hold such a chair at the university<ref name="Guardian"/><ref name="Telegraph">{{cite news|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/uknews/1531486/That-was-the-Week-that-Was%E2%80%A6.html?pageNum=2|archive-url=https://archive.today/20130422061530/http://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/uknews/1531486/That-was-the-Week-that-Was%E2%80%A6.html?pageNum=2|url-status=dead|archive-date=2013-04-22|title=That was the week that was...|last=Conway|first=Anne-Marie|work=Daily Telegraph|page=39|date=2006-10-14|accessdate=2008-08-21}}</ref> and explained that, when he was appointed in 1960, he saw Queen's as part of the [[Unionism in Ireland|Unionist]] establishment.<ref name="Times"/> According to an [[obituary]] written by [[Bernard Crick]], O'Leary suffered from [[alcoholism]], which resulting in him often being absent from the university and colleagues having to cover for him.<ref name="Guardian"/> At one point he lived in [[hotel]]s and [[lodging house]]s rather than at a fixed address.<ref name="Guardian"/>

Aside from his PhD thesis, O'Leary's main publications were ''Belfast: Approach to Crisis. A Study of Belfast Politics, 1613-1970'' (with Ian Budge, [[Macmillan Publishers|Macmillan]], 1973), ''The Northern Ireland Assembly, 1982-1986: A Constitutional Experiment'' (with Sydney Elliott and R.A. Wilford, Hurst, 1988) and ''Controversial Issues in Anglo-Irish Relations, 1910-1921'' (with Patrick Maume, Four Courts, 2004). He had planned a work on 20th century [[Irish politics]] but this never materialised.<ref name="Guardian"/>

Although from the [[Republic of Ireland]], O'Leary developed an interest in [[Unionism in Ireland|Unionism]], and was an advisor to the [[Ulster Defence Association]].<ref name="Times"/> He subsequently wrote a paper on Northern Irish independence at the request of [[John McMichael]].<ref name="Times"/> During the 1960s and 1970s he was a regular commentator on Northern Ireland in the media.<ref name="Letter">{{cite news|title=Political professor dies|date=2006-09-13|work=News Letter|pages=4}}</ref> In his later life, he served as the vice-chairman of the [[anti-abortion movements|anti-abortion]] campaign surrounding the [[Eighth Amendment of the Constitution of Ireland]],<ref name="Times"/> which introduced a constitutional ban on [[abortion]]. This brought him into opposition with many [[feminism|feminists]].<ref name="Guardian"/>

O'Leary died on 7 September 2006<ref name="Guardian"/> after a short illness.<ref name="Times"/> He is buried at Timoleague Abbey Cemetery in [[County Cork]].<ref name="Times"/>
