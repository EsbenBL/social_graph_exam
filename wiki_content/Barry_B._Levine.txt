== Background==
Barry B. Levine was born on January 22, 1941, in Brooklyn, New York.  His parents were labor lawyer [[Nathan Levine]] and Miriam Margolies; he had a younger brother David (who also became an academic).  He attended [[Brooklyn Tech]].  He received his B.A. in 1961 from the [[University of Pennsylvania]] and his M.A. in 1965 and Ph.D. in 1973 from the [[New School for Social Research]].<ref name=MHobit-Levine/><ref name=FIU-Levine/>

==Career==
Levine held a teaching position at the [[University of Puerto Rico]] for seven years before leaving for FIU, of which he was a founding professor in 1972 through his retirement in 2007,<ref name=MHobit-Levine/> after which he was professor emeritus of sociology.<ref name=FIU-Levine/>

In 1969, he co-founded and edited ''Caribbean Review'', an English-language journal focused on the culture and ideals of the [[Caribbean]], [[Latin America]] and their [[emigrant]] groups; subscribers included the [[White House]] under Presidents [[Ronald Reagan]] and [[George Herbert Walker Bush]].  In 1981, ''Caribbean Review'' was a finalist for the National Magazine Award.<ref name=MHobit-Levine/>

==Personal life and death==
Levine married Rosario Aycardi; they had two sons.<ref name=MHobit-Levine/>

Barry B. Levine died aged 79 on August 10, 2020, in Miami.<ref name=MHobit-Levine/>

==Works==

Levine is perhaps best known for penning ''Benjy Lopez'' which received much acclaim; most recently in a February, 2008, ''[[Newsweek Magazine|Newsweek]]'' article written by art historian [[Robert Farris Thompson]].<ref>[http://www.newsweek.com/id/112774 My Turn: Mambo On My Mind]</ref>

In 2014, he was writing on the topic of "The Impermanence of Industry: Lessons Learned from the Last Great American Garment Maker," a first-person testimonial about a 77-year-old garment manufacturer who has had factories throughout the US, the Caribbean, Asia, and Latin America.<ref name=FIU-Levine-CV>
