[[Image:Jean Blondel in 2008.jpg|frame|Jean Blondel receiving an honorary degree in Political Sciences from the University of Siena, 9 October 2008]]'''Jean Blondel''' (born [[Toulon]], 26 October 1929) is a French [[Political science|political scientist]] specialising in [[comparative politics]]. He is currently [[Emeritus Professor]] at the [[European University Institute]] in [[Florence]], and visiting professor at the [[University of Siena]]. He lives in London.

He graduated from the [[Institut d'Etudes Politiques|Institut d'Études Politiques]] of Paris in 1953. He studied at [[St Antony's College]] (Oxford) from 1953 to 1955,<ref name=Bio>{{cite web|url=http://www.iue.it/SPS/People/Faculty/FormerProfessors/bioBlondel.shtml|title=Profile: Jean Blondel|publisher=European University Institute|access-date=2008-05-13|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080412220232/http://www.iue.it/SPS/People/Faculty/FormerProfessors/bioBlondel.shtml|archive-date=2008-04-12|url-status=dead}}</ref> graduating with a B.Litt. He returned to France for military service, returning to Britain to study the relations between central and local government at [[Manchester University]]. He was a lecturer at the University College of North Staffordshire (now [[Keele University]]) from 1958 to 1963,<ref>J. Blondel, ''Voters, Parties and Leaders'', Pelican 1963, frontispiece</ref> a fellow at [[Yale University]] in 1963-4 and then moved to the [[University of Essex]] in 1964, where he founded the Department of Government.<ref name=Bio/> He started the [[European Consortium for Political Research|European Consortium for Political Research (ECPR)]] in 1969 and directed it for ten years following its foundation meeting in 1970.<ref name=Bio/><ref>{{cite journal|last=Budge|first=Ian|year=2006|title=Blondel and the development of European political science|journal=European Political Science|volume=5|issue=3|pages=315–327|url=http://www.palgrave-journals.com/eps/journal/v5/n3/abs/2210084a.html|doi=10.1057/palgrave.eps.2210084|s2cid=143794197}}</ref> Having left Essex in 1984, he was appointed scholar of the [[Russell Sage Foundation]] in New York in 1984 before becoming professor of political science at the European University Institute in Florence from 1985 to 1994.<ref name=Bio/> He holds honorary doctorates from the Universities of [[University of Salford|Salford]] and [[University of Essex|Essex]] in the [[United Kingdom]], [[Université catholique de Louvain|Louvain-la-Neuve]] in [[Belgium]], [[University of Turku|Turku]] in [[Finland]], and [[University of Siena|Siena]] in [[Italy]].<ref name=Bio/>

He is a member of the [[Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences]] and of the [[Academia Europaea]].<ref name=Bio/> In 2004 he was awarded the [[Johan Skytte Prize in Political Science]] "for his outstanding contribution to the professionalisation of European political science, both as a pioneering comparativist and an institution builder".<ref>{{cite web|url=http://skytteprize.statsvet.uu.se/PrizeWinners/tabid/1953/language/en-US/Default.aspx|title=Prize Winners|publisher=The Johan Skytte Prize in Political Science|access-date=2011-07-01|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20120314212229/http://skytteprize.statsvet.uu.se/PrizeWinners/tabid/1953/language/en-US/Default.aspx|archive-date=2012-03-14}}</ref>

Blondel is particularly noted for the contributions he has made to the theory of party systems, the comparative study of cabinets, and the relations between parties and governments.

In honour of his work, the [[European Consortium for Political Research|ECPR]] has since October 2003 awarded the annual Jean Blondel PhD Prize for the best thesis in politics nominated by a member institution in a given year.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://ecpr.eu/Prizes/PrizeDetails.aspx?PrizeID=4|title=Jean Blondel PhD Prize - ECPR Prizes and Awards|website=ecpr.eu|access-date=2017-07-20}}</ref>

His work has focused recently in a comparison of different presidential systems across the globe, with a particular emphasis on Latin America, Africa and the ex-Soviet republics. It seeks to be amongst the first comprehensive studies of different presidential systems. His new book is "Presidents and Democracy in Latin America" edited with Manuel Alcantara and Jean-Louis Thiebault.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.routledge.com/Presidents-and-Democracy-in-Latin-America/Alcantara-Blondel-Thiebault/p/book/9781138082090|title = Presidents and Democracy in Latin America}}</ref>

* ''An Introduction to Comparative Government''. London: Weidenfeld & Nicolson, 1969.
* ''Comparative legislatures''. Englewood Cliffs, N.J : [[Prentice-Hall]], 1973.
* ''Political parties. A genuine case for discontent?''. London : Wildwood House, 1978.
* ''The Discipline Of Politics''. London & Boston : Butterworths, 1981.
* ''Political leadership : towards a general analysis''. London & Beverly Hills : SAGE, 1987.

* Blondel, Jean and Ferdinand Müller-Rommel (eds.) ''Cabinets in Eastern Europe ''. Basingstoke : Macmillan, 2001.
* Blondel, Jean. ''The Presidential Republic''. Palgrave Macmillan, 2015.
