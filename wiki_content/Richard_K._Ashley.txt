'''Richard K. Ashley''' is a [[Postmodernism (international relations)|postmodernist]] scholar of [[International relations theory|International relations]]. He is an [[associate professor]] at the [[Arizona State University]]'s School of Politics and Global Studies.

Ashley studied at the [[University of California, Santa Barbara]] and at [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology]] (MIT). He was research assistant to [[Hayward Alker]]. Initially, Ashley's research was on the [[Balance of power (international relations)|balance of power in international relations]], particularly in his ''The Political Economy of War and Peace'' (1980). He soon began to shift his approach to [[metatheoretical]] questions and [[Critical Theory]]. By the mid-1980s, Ashley had adopted a postmodernist and subversive approach to [[international relations theory]], exemplified by his influences: [[Jacques Derrida]], [[Michel Foucault]], and [[Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak]].

Ashley was one of the first to challenge the position of mainstream [[Realism (international relations)|realism]] and [[Liberalism (international relations)|liberalism]]. In "The Poverty of Neorealism" (1984), he coined the term "[[neorealism (international relations)|neorealism]]" to describe the work of [[Kenneth Waltz]].

==Early life==
Ashley received his [[Bachelor of Arts]] degree from [[University of California, Santa Barbara]] in 1970,<ref name="asu._Rich">{{Cite web | title = Richard Ashley — Arizona State University | author =  | work = asu.pure.elsevier.com | date =  | access-date = 6 April 2017 | url = https://asu.pure.elsevier.com/en/persons/richard-ashley | quote =  }}</ref> after which he entered [[graduate school]] in [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology]] (MIT) studying political science.<ref name="WillickAshley1971">{{cite journal|last1=Willick|first1=Daniel H.|last2=Ashley|first2=Richard K.|title=Survey Question Order and the Political Party Preferences of College Students and Their Parents|journal=Public Opinion Quarterly|volume=35|issue=2|year=1971|pages=189|issn=0033-362X|doi=10.1086/267890}}</ref> He received his [[Doctorate of Philosophy]] from MIT in 1977,{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=207}} with a dissertation titled ''Growth, Rivalry, and Balance: The Sino-Soviet-American Triangle of Conflict'' (1976),<ref name="Rosenau1976"/> supervised by Nazli Choucri.<ref name="worl_Grow">{{Cite document | title = Growth, rivalry, and balance : the Sino-Soviet-American triangle of conflict (Book, 1976) [WorldCat.org] | author =  | work = worldcat.org | date =  | quote =  | oclc = 4200282}}</ref>

==Career==

Ashley studied under [[Hayward Alker]] and served as his research assistant. This relationship influenced Ashley's approach to international relations.<ref name="Patomäki2005">{{cite book|editor-last1=Neumann|editor-first1=Iver B.|editor-last2=Wæver|editor-first2=Ole|last=Patomäki|first=Heikki|title=The Future of International Relations: Masters in the Making?|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=BQOGAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA229|year=2005|publisher=Routledge|location=London|isbn=978-1-134-76219-4|page=229|chapter=Hayward Alker: an exemplary voyage from quantitative peace research to humanistic, late-modern globalism}}</ref> Other influences include [[Jacques Derrida]], [[Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak]], [[Foucauldian]] [[discourse analysis]],{{sfn|Pettiford|2015|p=26}} and [[Jürgen Habermas]].{{sfn|Jarvis|2000|p=107}} For some time in the 1970s, Ashley was [[assistant professor]] of international relations at the [[University of Southern California]].<ref name="Rosenau1976">{{cite book|last=Rosenau|first=James N.|title=In Search of Global Patterns|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=ksyOAAAAMAAJ|year=1976|publisher=Free|isbn=978-0-02-927050-9|page=377}}</ref>

Early in his career, with ''The Political Economy of War and Peace'' (1980), Ashley focused on conventional analysis of [[Balance of power (international relations)|balance of power]]. He soon began to focus on [[metatheoretical]] issues instead.{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=207}} Before turning to [[Postmodernism (international relations)|postmodernist international relations]], Ashley's early work moved to the direction of [[Critical Theory]].<ref name="IkenberryMastanduno2005">{{cite book|editor-last1=Ikenberry|editor-first1=G. John.|editor-last2=Mastanduno|editor-first2=Michael|last=Berger|first=Thomas U.|title=International Relations Theory and the Asia-Pacific|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=sxed958YJhIC&pg=PA412|year=2005|publisher=Columbia University Press|location=New York|isbn=978-0-231-50092-0|page=412|chapter=Power and Purpose in Pacific East Asia: A Constructivist Interpretation}}</ref> He became the first scholar to introduce the thought of Habermas to international relations.<ref name="CarlsnaesRisse2012">{{cite book|editor-last1=Carlsnaes|editor-first1=Walter|editor-last2=Risse|editor-first2=Thomas|editor-last3=Simmons|editor-first3=Beth A|title=Handbook of International Relations|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=M_NcCwAAQBAJ&pg=PT140|edition=2nd|year=2012|publisher=SAGE Publications|last=Zehfuss|first=Maja|isbn=978-1-4739-7119-6|page=140|chapter=Critical Theory, Poststructuralism, and Postcolonialism}}</ref> All Ashley's major writings from this phase of the first half of the 1980s can be characterized as a critique of [[technical rationality]] in the study of international relations and advocacy of emancipatory ways of knowing. This approach is evident in his debates concerning Habermas with [[John H. Herz]]. Since the mid 1980s, his critique has become a self-confessed subversive dissidence of the discipline.{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=208}} Ashley has since distanced himself from his early work, considering it too ideological in its epistemology.{{sfn|Jarvis|2000|p=106}}

Ashley become one of the first to challenge the predominance of mainstream [[Realism (international relations)|realism]] and [[Liberalism (international relations)|liberalism]] in the 1980s.{{sfn|Pettiford|2015|p=25}}

Ashley retired in 2018. Prior he taught at [[Arizona State University]]'s Department of Political Science (now School of Politics and Global Studies) since 1981,{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=207}} as an [[associate professor]].<ref name="pgs._Rich">{{cite book|editor-last1=Soguk|editor-first1=Nevzat|editor-last2=Nelson|editor-first2=Scott G|title=The Ashgate Research Companion to Modern Theory, Modern Power, World Politics: Critical Investigations|chapter=List of Contributors|date=2016|publisher=Routledge|location=London|isbn=978-1-317-19585-6|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=GwERDAAAQBAJ&pg=PR13}}
</ref>

and others.<ref name="Wæver2005">{{cite book|editor-last1=Neumann|editor-first1=Iver B.|editor-last2=Wæver|editor-first2=Ole|last=Wæver|first=Ole|title=The Future of International Relations: Masters relations theory]].{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=208}} "Untying the Sovereign State: A Double Reading of the Anarchy Problematique" (1988)<ref name="BickertonCunliffe2006">{{cite is a [[Derridan]] [[double reading]] of the concept of [[Anarchy (international relations)|international anarchy]] in traditional international relations literature.<ref>{{cite He received the [[Karl Deutsch Award (international relations)|Karl Deutsch Award]] of the [[International Studies Association]] in 1985.{{sfn|Griffiths|1999|p=207}}
