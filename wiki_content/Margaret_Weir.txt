'''Margaret M. Weir''' (born July 17, 1952) is an American [[Political science|political scientist]] and [[Sociology|sociologist]], best known for her work on [[social policy]] and the politics of poverty in the United States, particularly at the levels of [[state government|state]] and [[local government|local]] government.

==Career==
Weir is currently a [[professor]] at [[Brown University]]. She was a [[professor]] of political science and sociology at the [[University of California, Berkeley]],<ref>{{Cite web|title=Margaret Weir|url=https://polisci.berkeley.edu/people/person/mweirberkeleyedu|url-status=live|access-date=2021-04-25|website=polisci.berkeley.edu}}</ref> where her research and teaching fields included American political development, [[Urban politics in the United States|urban politics]] and policy, [[political sociology]], and comparative studies of the [[welfare state]]. She is also a nonresident senior [[fellow]] at the [[Brookings Institution]], where she had served previously as a senior fellow in governmental studies, from 1992 to 1997. From 1985 to 1992, she was a faculty member at [[Harvard University]] in the Department of Government.<ref>{{Cite web|title=UC Berkeley Department of Sociology profile. Accessed 6/7/14|url=http://sociology.berkeley.edu/faculty/margaret-m-weir|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140714185313/http://sociology.berkeley.edu/faculty/margaret-m-weir|archive-date=July 14, 2014|access-date=2014-06-07|website=sociology.berkeley.edu}}</ref>

Weir is currently involved in a number of organizations. She is director of the Building Resilient Regions Network, which is funded by the [[John D. and Catherine T. MacArthur Foundation|MacArthur Foundation]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Building Resilient Regions profile - Margaret Weir|url=http://brr.berkeley.edu/2011/01/margaret-weir/|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140714171942/http://brr.berkeley.edu/2011/01/margaret-weir/|archive-date=2014-07-14|access-date=2014-06-09|website=brr.berkeley.edu}}</ref> At the [[Scholars Strategy Network]], she is co-director of the Bay Area regional network and a regular contributor of briefs.<ref>{{Cite web|title=Margaret Weir - Contributions - In the News - Publications|url=https://scholars.org/scholar/margaret-weir|url-status=live|access-date=2021-04-25|website=scholars.org|publisher=[[Scholars Strategy Network]]}}</ref> She also serves on the advisory board at the Center for Labor Research and Education (UC Berkeley Labor Center).<ref>{{Cite web|title=UC Berkeley Labor Center: Advisory Board|url=http://laborcenter.berkeley.edu/about/board.shtml|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140714214325/http://laborcenter.berkeley.edu/about/board.shtml|archive-date=2014-07-14|website=laborcenter.berkeley.edu}}</ref>

Weir has written widely on social policy and politics in the United States. In ''Politics and Jobs: The Boundaries of Employment Policy in the United States'' ([[Princeton University Press]], 1992), she addresses the power of ideas in policymaking and the politics of interest formation in order to explain the persistence of lacking employment policy in the United States.<ref>{{Cite book|last=Weir|first=Margaret|title=Politics and Jobs: The Boundaries of Employment Policy in the United States|date=January 25, 1993|publisher=[[Princeton University Press]]|isbn=978-0691024929|edition=Revised}}</ref> With [[Ira Katznelson]], Weir coauthored ''Schooling for All: Class, Race, and the Decline of the Democratic Ideal'' ([[Basic Books]], 1985),<ref>{{Cite book|last=Katznelson|first=Ira|title=Schooling For All : Class, Race, and the Decline of the Democratic Ideal|last2=Weir|first2=Margaret|date=1985-04-25|publisher=[[Basic Books]]|isbn=978-0465072330|location=New York|oclc=558401195}}</ref> which focuses on [[Education in the United States|public school]] systems in Chicago and San Francisco in order to examine equal access to education as a dwindling [[civil right]]. Weir has also edited several volumes, including ''The Politics of Social Policy in the United States'' with [[Ann Shola Orloff]] and [[Theda Skocpol]] (Princeton University Press, 1988).<ref>{{Cite book|title=The Politics of Social Policy in the United States|date=May 21, 1988|publisher=[[Princeton University Press]]|isbn=978-0691028415|editor-last=Weir|editor-first=Margaret|editor-last2=Orloff|editor-first2=Ann Shola|editor-last3=Skocpol|editor-first3=Theda}}</ref>

Weir's chapter "Creating Justice for the Poor in the New Metropolis," from ''Justice and the American Metropolis'' ([[University of Minnesota Press]], 2011)<ref>{{Cite web|date=2011|editor-last=Hayward|editor-first=Clarissa Rile|editor2-last=Swanstrom|editor2-first=Todd|title=Justice and the American Metropolis|url=https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/justice-and-the-american-metropolis|url-status=live|access-date=2021-04-25|website=[[University of Minnesota Press]]|page=237|isbn=978-0-8166-7613-2}}</ref> was the topic of discussion on the radio show ''Against the Grain'' on January 10, 2012.<ref>{{Cite web|date=2012-01-10|title=Against the Grain - January 10, 2012 - 12:00PM|url=https://kpfa.org/archives/2012/1/10/|url-status=live|access-date=2021-04-25|website=kpfa.org}}</ref>

In 2004, Weir received an Investigator Award from the [[Robert Wood Johnson Foundation]] for work on American [[health care reform|health policy reform]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Margaret M. Weir PhD - Investigator Award in Federalism and Strategies for Reform in American Health Policy (2004)|url=http://investigatorawards.org/investigators/margaret-weir|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140714201417/http://investigatorawards.org/investigators/margaret-weir|archive-date=2014-07-14|access-date=2021-04-25|website=investigatorawards.org|publisher=[[Robert Wood Johnson Foundation]]}}</ref>
