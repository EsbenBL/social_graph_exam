'''Nelson Woolf Polsby''' (October 25, 1934 &ndash; February 6, 2007) was an American [[political science|political scientist]].  He specialized in the study of the [[President of the United States|United States presidency]], the [[United States Congress]] and how governmental policies and practices evolve.<ref name="Bernstein">{{cite news |last1=Bernstein |first1=Adam |title=Nelson W. Polsby, 72 |url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/local/2007/02/08/nelson-w-polsby-72/b0f7b16a-b37b-4737-81ca-68dbe3b6f7a9/ |access-date=15 October 2021 |work=The Washington Post |date=February 8, 2007}}</ref><ref name="Utter">{{cite book |last1=Utter |first1=Glenn H. |last2=Lockhart |first2=Charles |title=American political scientists : a dictionary |date=2002 |publisher=Greenwood Press |location=Westport, Conn. |isbn=9780313319570 |pages=319-321 |edition=2nd}}</ref><ref name="Cain"/>

Polsby was the Heller Professor of Political Science at the [[University of California, Berkeley]].<ref name="Memoriam"/>  He was the editor of the ''[[American Political Science Review]]'' from 1971–77<ref name="Cain"/> and the founding editor of the  ''[[Annual Review of Political Science]]'' from 1998 until his death in 2007.<ref>{{cite journal|doi=10.1146/annurev.pl.1.010198.100001|title=Preface by Nelson W. Polsby|year=1998|last1=Polsby|first1=Nelson W.|journal=Annual Review of Political Science|volume=1}}</ref><ref name="Levi">{{cite journal |last1=Levi |first1=Margaret |title=Nelson W. Polsby (1934–2007) |journal=Annual Review of Political Science |date=June 2007 |volume=10 |issue=1 |doi=10.1146/annurev.pl.10.041807.100001 |url=https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/full/10.1146/annurev.pl.10.041807.100001 |doi-access=free |access-date=15 October 2021 |language=en |issn=1094-2939}}</ref>

==Education==
Polsby was born in [[Norwich, Connecticut]].<ref name="Kreisler">{{cite web |last1=Kreisler |first1=Harry |title=Institutional Change in the U.S. Congress: Conversation with Nelson  W. Polsby, Heller Professor of Political Science, University of California, Berkeley |url=http://globetrotter.berkeley.edu/people2/Polsby/polsby-con0.html |website=Conversations with History: Institute of International Studies, UC Berkeley |publisher=University of California, Berkeley |access-date=15 October 2021|date=September 4, 2002}}</ref>  
He attended [[Pomfret School]]. a private school in [[Pomfret, Connecticut]].<ref name="Bernstein"/>  His family also spent time in [[Washington, D.C.]], where Polsby would sit and observe sessions of Congress.<ref name="Memoriam"/> Polsby became interested in public opinion and its influence on elections as [[Joseph R. McCarthy]] became more powerful in Washington in the 1940s and early 1950s.<ref name="Bernstein"/><ref name="Kreisler"/>
 
Polsby earned his undergraduate degree from [[Johns Hopkins University]] in 1956<ref name="Bernstein"/> and a master's degree from [[Brown University]] in 1957.
He then joined [[Yale University]], where he studied with [[Robert Dahl]]. Fellow students included [[Raymond Wolfinger]] and [[Aaron Wildavsky]].<ref name="Cain">{{cite journal |last1=Cain |first1=Bruce E. |title=Nelson Polsby |journal=PS: Political Science & Politics |date=July 2007 |volume=40 |issue=3 |pages=597–599 |doi=10.1017/S1049096507260895 |url=https://doi.org/10.1017/S1049096507260895 |access-date=15 October 2021 |language=en |issn=1537-5935}}</ref> 
He earned a master's and a doctoral degree from Yale  in 1958 and 1961.<ref name="Gale">{{cite book |title=Contemporary authors new revision ser vol 05 a bio-bibliographical guide to current writers in fiction, general nonfiction, poetry, journalism, drama, motion pictures, television. |date=1982 |publisher=Gale Research Co.  |series= New Revision  Series  |volume=  5 |chapter=POLSBY,  Nelson  W(oolf) 1934- |isbn= 9780810319349 |page=425 |url=http://digitalcollections.library.cmu.edu/awweb/awarchive?type=file&item=407775}}</ref><ref name="Memoriam"/>  Polsby later sat on Yale's  University Council (1978-2000) and was its president from 1986-1993.<ref name="President">{{cite news |title=In Memoriam: Nelson Polsby  Former president of the University Council |url=http://archives.news.yale.edu/v35.n18/story17.html |access-date=15 October 2021 |work=Yale Bulletin and Calendar |date=February 16, 2007|volume=35|issue=13}}</ref>

==Career==
Polsby taught at the [[University of Wisconsin–Madison]] (1960-61) and [[Wesleyan University]] (1961-1968)<ref name="Encyclopedia">{{cite web |title=Polsby, Nelson W. 1934–2007 |url=https://www.encyclopedia.com/arts/educational-magazines/polsby-nelson-w-1934-2007 |website=Encyclopedia.com}}</ref> before moving to the [[University of California, Berkeley]] in 1967; from 1988 to 1999, he was director of Berkeley's [[Institute of Governmental Studies]] (IGS).<ref name="Bernstein"/>  He was a member of the [[Council on Foreign Relations]] and a fellow of the [[American Academy of Arts and Sciences]], the [[American Association for the Advancement of Science]], and the [[National Academy of Public Administration (United States)|National Academy of Public Administration]].<ref name="Gilmore"/>

During the 1960s  Polsby did extensive field work and examined the "human nature" of  Congress and the historical implications of recurrent calls for change on the institution.  He was one of several influential political scientists who changed the field.  His paper "The Institutionalization of the U.S. House of Representatives" (1968) was considered "one of the 20 most influential articles published in the ''American Political Science Review''."<ref name="Memoriam"/> 

Polsby was the author of numerous articles on American politics, both papers for academic journals and political commentary for newspapers and magazines.<ref name="Bernstein"/><ref name="Memoriam"/> He sometimes wrote under the [[pseudonym]] Arthur Clun.<ref name="Encyclopedia"/>
He wrote or edited more than 20 books, including ''Political Innovation in America'' (1984), ''Congress and the Presidency'' (1986), and ''How Congress Evolves'' (2004).<ref name="Bernstein"/><ref name="Memoriam"/>
In 1997, he was commissioned by the non-partisan [[Twentieth Century Fund]] to write ''The New Federalist Papers: Essays in Defense of the Constitution'', with [[Alan Brinkley]] and [[Kathleen M. Sullivan]].<ref name="Bernstein"/>
In addition to his work on American politics, he cowrote ''British Government and its Discontents' (1981) with [[Geoffrey Peter Smith]].<!-- born 1930 --><ref name="Memoriam"/>

Polsby often used humor to make his political writing more accessible and was frequently quoted by reporters.<ref name="Bernstein"/> He is credited with reshaping both academic understanding and public awareness of governmental institutions.<ref name="Memoriam">{{cite web |title=In Memoriam  Nelson W. Polsby  Professor of Political Science  UC Berkeley  1934 – 2007 |url=https://senate.universityofcalifornia.edu/_files/inmemoriam/html/nelsonpolsby.html |website=University of California, Berkeley |access-date=15 October 2021}}</ref>

Polsby died February 6, 2007, in [[Berkeley, California]], from heart disease.<ref name="Martin">{{cite news |last1=Martin |first1=Douglas |title=Nelson W. Polsby, 72, Author and a Scholar of Politics, Dies |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2007/02/09/obituaries/09polsby.html |access-date=15 October 2021 |work=The New York Times |date=9 February 2007}}</ref><ref name="Gilmore">{{cite news |last1=Gilmore |first1=Janet |title=Nelson Polsby, distinguished scholar of American politics, dies at 72  |url=https://www.berkeley.edu/news/media/releases/2007/02/07_polsby.shtml |access-date=15 October 2021 |work=UCBerkeley News |date=7 February 2007}}</ref><ref name="Find-A-Grave">{{cite web |title=Nelson Woolf Polsby |url=https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/170358925/nelson-woolf-polsby |website=Find-A-Grave |access-date=15 October 2021}}</ref>
