'''Stephen David Krasner''' (born February 15, 1942) is an American academic and former diplomat. Krasner has been a professor of [[international relations]] at [[Stanford University]] since 1981, and served as the [[Director of Policy Planning]] from 2005 to April 2007 while on leave from Stanford.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://2001-2009.state.gov/outofdate/bios/k/42800.htm|title=Krasner, Stephen|last=Department Of State. The Office of Electronic Information|first=Bureau of Public Affairs|website=2001-2009.state.gov|language=en|access-date=2020-03-07}}</ref> 

A [[Realism (international relations)|realist]], he is known for his contributions to International Relations and International Political Economy.<ref name=":9">{{Cite book|last=Cohen|first=Benjamin J.|url=https://books.google.com/books/about/International_Political_Economy.html?id=H79WVDwMzCEC|title=International Political Economy: An Intellectual History|date=2008|publisher=Princeton University Press|isbn=978-0-691-13569-4|pages=72–74|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Citation|last=Keohane|first=Robert O.|title=Stephen Krasner: Subversive Realist|date=2013|url=https://oxford.universitypressscholarship.com/view/10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199970087.001.0001/acprof-9780199970087-chapter-3|work=Back to Basics|pages=28–52|editor-last=Finnemore|editor-first=Martha|publisher=Oxford University Press|doi=10.1093/acprof:oso/9780199970087.003.0003|isbn=978-0-19-997008-7|editor2-last=Goldstein|editor2-first=Judith}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Keohane|first=Robert O.|date=1997|title=Problematic Lucidity: Stephen Krasner's "State Power and the Structure of International Trade"|url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/25054030|journal=World Politics|volume=50|issue=1|pages=150–170|issn=0043-8871}}</ref>

== Education ==

Krasner was born on February 15, 1942 in [[New York City]].<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://globetrotter.berkeley.edu/people3/Krasner/krasner-con0.html|title=Conversation with History - Stephen Krasner, 2003|website=globetrotter.berkeley.edu|access-date=2020-03-07}}</ref> He was raised in Manhattan.<ref name=":922">{{Cite book|last=Cohen|first=Benjamin J.|url=https://books.google.com/books/about/International_Political_Economy.html?id=H79WVDwMzCEC|title=International Political Economy: An Intellectual History|date=2008|publisher=Princeton University Press|isbn=978-0-691-13569-4|pages=97–99|language=en}}</ref> He received his bachelor's degree from the [[Cornell University Department of History|Department of History]] at [[Cornell University]] in 1963, where he was a member of the [[Quill and Dagger]] society. He earned his master's degree from [[Columbia University]]. In 1972, he completed his PhD at [[Harvard University]].<ref name=":922" /> He did his PhD dissertation on the international coffee market.<ref name=":922" /> At Harvard University, he was influenced by [[Albert O. Hirschman|Albert Hirschman]].<ref name=":922" />

== Career ==
Before coming to [[Stanford University]] in 1981, Krasner taught at [[Harvard University]] and [[University of California, Los Angeles]]. He was the editor of [[International Organization (journal)|''International Organization'']] from 1986 to 1992.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://cddrl.fsi.stanford.edu/people/stephen_d_krasner|title=FSI {{!}} CDDRL - Stephen D. Krasner|website=cddrl.fsi.stanford.edu|access-date=2020-03-07}}</ref><ref name=":922" />

Krasner is the author of six books and over ninety articles. He has taught courses on [[international relations]], [[international political economy]], international relations theory, policy making, and state-building at Stanford University. He received a dean’s award for excellence in teaching in 1991. At Stanford University, Krasner has been an advisor to Daniel Drezner<ref>{{Cite book|last=Drezner|first=Daniel W.|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=N_7N_7hAHL4C&newbks=0&hl=en|title=The Sanctions Paradox: Economic Statecraft and International Relations|date=1999|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=978-0-521-64415-0|pages=xv|language=en}}</ref> and  

Krasner was a key figure in establishing [[Regime theory]] as a prominent topic of study in IR, in part through the 1983 edited collection ''International Regimes''.<ref name=":92">{{Cite book|last=Cohen|first=Benjamin J.|url=https://books.google.com/books/about/International_Political_Economy.html?id=H79WVDwMzCEC|title=International Political Economy: An Intellectual History|date=2008|publisher=Princeton University Press|isbn=978-0-691-13569-4|pages=96, 100|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Martin|first=Lisa L.|last2=Simmons|first2=Beth A.|date=1998|title=Theories and Empirical Studies of International Institutions|url=https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-organization/article/abs/theories-and-empirical-studies-of-international-institutions/0B3DE504D305E7A7191F70EA1B086733|journal=International Organization|language=en|volume=52|issue=4|pages=729–757|doi=10.1162/002081898550734|issn=1531-5088}}</ref> Krasner is a key figure in the development of [[Hegemonic stability theory]].<ref name=":9" /><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Webb|first=Michael C.|last2=Krasner|first2=Stephen D.|date=1989|title=Hegemonic Stability Theory: An Empirical Assessment|url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/20097178|journal=Review of International Studies|volume=15|issue=2|pages=183–198|issn=0260-2105}}</ref> Krasner was influenced by [[Robert Gilpin]].<ref name=":922" /> 

He has written extensively about statehood and [[sovereignty]].

Krasner is credited with incorporating the idea of [[punctuated equilibrium]] into the social sciences and contributing to [[critical juncture theory]].<ref>Stephen D. Krasner, "Approaches to the State: Alternative Conceptions and Historical Dynamics." ''Comparative Politics'' 16(2)(1984): 223–46; Stephen D. Krasner, "Sovereignty: An Institutional Perspective." ''Comparative Political Studies'' 21(1)(1988): 66–94.</ref>

Krasner is a senior fellow at Stanford's [[Hoover Institution]].

Krasner was named Director of Policy Planning in the State Department by his former Stanford University colleague [[Condoleezza Rice]].<ref name=":922" />
