'''Gesine Schwan''' (born 22 May 1943) is a German [[political science]] professor and member of the [[Social Democratic Party of Germany]]. The party has nominated her twice as a candidate for the federal [[President of Germany|presidential elections]]. On 23 May 2004, she was defeated by the [[Christian Democratic Union (Germany)|Christian Democrat]] [[Horst Köhler]]. On 23 May 2009, Köhler beat her again to win his second term.<ref name=dw>{{cite web

==Personal life==
Born in [[Berlin]] on 22 May 1943, Schwan was [[baptism|baptized]] in the [[Roman Catholic Church|Roman Catholic]] faith<ref>{{cite web| last = Polzar | first = Wolfgang | title = German Evangelical elected President | publisher = Spero News | date = 2004-05-25 | url = http://www.speroforum.com/site/print.asp?idarticle=586 | access-date = 2008-06-22}}</ref> as the daughter of ''Oberschulrat'' (Senior School Inspector) Hans R. Schneider. During the [[Third Reich]] dictatorship her parents were members of the passive [[German Resistance|resistance]],<ref name=who>{{cite web
  | title = Gesine Schwan: Biografie
  | publisher = [[International Who's Who|Who's Who. The People-Lexicon]].
  | url = http://www.whoswho.de/templ/te_bio.php?PID=2086&RID=1
  | access-date = 2008-07-21|language=de}}</ref> offering protection to a Jewish girl by hiding her. After World War II, the family engaged actively in the reconciliation of [[#Category:Polish-German relations|Polish–German relations]].

In 1969, Schwan married her first husband, Professor Alexander Schwan, with whom she had two children and who died in 1989.<ref name=who/> In 2004, Gesine Schwan celebrated her second wedding with longtime companion [[Peter Eigen]] in Berlin. He is a former [[World Bank]] manager as well as a founder and current Chair of the Advisory Council of [[Transparency International]]. Schwan is very engaged in German and Polish mutual understanding and therefore supports, in numerous ways, the work of the Freya von Moltke Stiftung for the New Kreisau. She has given numerous presentations on this topic.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.fvms.de/de/schirmherr-und-gremien/kuratorium.html |title=Archived copy |access-date=2014-10-05 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141007053628/http://www.fvms.de/de/schirmherr-und-gremien/kuratorium.html |archive-date=2014-10-07 }}</ref>

==Academic life==

In the same year, she began her studies in history, philosophy, [[romance languages]], and political science at the [[Free University of Berlin]] and later at [[University of Freiburg]].<ref name="CV">{{cite web

===Early career===
After research stays in  [[Warsaw]] and [[Kraków]], she obtained her [[Doctor of Philosophy|Ph.D.]] in 1970 from FU Berlin for a [[dissertation]] on the Polish philosopher [[Leszek Kołakowski]].<ref name=diss>{{cite book
  | last = Schwan
  | first = Gesine
  | title = Leszek Kołakowski. A Political Philosophy of Liberty according to Marx.
  | year = 1971
  | location = Stuttgart/Berlin/Köln
  }}</ref> After that, she became assistant professor at the same university. Here, she continued her works on the [[critique]] of [[Karl Marx|Marx]]<ref name=habil>{{cite book
  | last = Schwan
  | first = Gesine
  | title = The Social Critique of Karl Marx. Philosophical and Economical Bases.
  | year = 1974
  | location = Stuttgart/Berlin/Köln/Mainz
  }}</ref> for which she received the ''[[habilitation]]'' in 1975.

===Professorship===
Schwan was appointed full professor at the Department of Political Science at FU Berlin in 1977. At the time, her research fields encompassed ''political theory, philosophy, psychology, and culture'' as well as ''theories of democracy and socialism''. In 1980/81, she had a research stay at the [[Woodrow Wilson International Center for Scholars|Wilson Center for Scholars]] in the USA; another research stay in 1984 at the [[Robinson College]] at [[Cambridge University]]; in 1998, she was visiting professor at the [[New School for Social Research]] in New York. From 1993 to 1995, she was [[Dean (education)|dean]] of the Political Science Faculty at FU Berlin.<ref name="CV"/>

In 1999, Gesine Schwan competed for the post of [[University president|president]] of FU Berlin, but was defeated by [[Peter Gaehtgens]].<ref name=InternationalesProfil>

==Political career==
Schwan belongs to the German tradition of social democratic opposition to Karl Marx's theories. In September 1974, she was one of the founding members of the ''[[Seeheimer Kreis]]'', a conservative [[think tank]] within the SPD.<ref name=seeheim>{{cite web

 |archive-date = 2012-02-03
}}</ref> She became a member of the SPD ''Grundwertekommission'' (Commission for Fundamental Values) in 1977. In 1984, she was removed from this position after criticizing the party's ''[[Ostpolitik]]'' as cooperating too closely with communist governments, while not engaging enough with [[dissident]]s.<ref>{{cite web

  | first = David

  | url = http://www.spiegel.de/international/germany/0,1518,554875,00.html
  | access-date = 2008-06-22}}</ref> Schwan was reinstated in the ''Grundwertekommission'' in 1996. Since 2002, she has presided over the ''German-Polish Forum'' together with [[Janusz Reiter]]. In November 2004, the German government under [[Gerhard Schröder]] appointed her to the newly created office of the co-ordinator for cooperation with Poland (her counterpart on the Polish side was then [[Irena Lipowicz]]).<ref name="CV"/> She retained this position when the government changed and became headed by Christian Democrat [[Angela Merkel]]. In February 2009, she criticized Schröder for being too uncritical towards [[Vladimir Putin]] and too insensitive about Polish concerns about closer German-Russian ties.<ref>

On 15 September 2010, Schwan supported the new initiative [[Spinelli Group]] in the [[European Parliament]], which was founded to reinvigorate the strive for federalisation of the European Union (EU). Other prominent supporters are: [[Jacques Delors]], [[Daniel Cohn-Bendit]], [[Guy Verhofstadt]], [[Jo Leinen]], [[Andrew Duff]], and [[Elmar Brok]].

===Presidential elections===
On 23 May 2004, Schwan was the SPD candidate for the [[2004 German presidential election|German presidential election]]. She received 589 votes in the ''[[Bundesversammlung (Germany)|Bundesversammlung]]'' (Federal Assembly) but was defeated by Horst Köhler who won 604 votes from the 1205 delegates.<ref name=rulers>{{cite web

===Later political career===
In the [[2019 Social Democratic Party of Germany leadership election|2019 SPD leadership election]], Schwan announced her intention to run for the position as the party's co-chair, together with [[Ralf Stegner]].<ref>[https://www.spiegel.de/politik/deutschland/spd-vorsitz-gesine-schwan-und-ralf-stegner-treten-als-team-an-a-1281876.html Nahles-Nachfolge: Schwan und Stegner bewerben sich für SPD-Vorsitz] ''[[Der Spiegel]]'', August 14, 2019.</ref>

==Controversy==
Ahead of the 2009 presidential election, Schwan was criticized by the [[Federal Commissioner for the Stasi Records]], [[Marianne Birthler]], for statements on the political regime of [[East Germany]]. Reportedly, several electors from the Green Party refused to vote for Schwan due to her statements regarding the communist state.<ref name=stasi>{{cite web
