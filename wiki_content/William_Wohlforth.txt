'''William Curti Wohlforth''' (born 1959) is the Daniel Webster Professor of Government in the [[Dartmouth College]] Department of Government, of which he was chair for three academic years (2006-2009). He is the author of ''Elusive Balance: Power and Perceptions during the Cold War'' (Cornell, 1993) and editor of ''Witnesses to the End of the Cold War'' (Johns Hopkins, 1996) and ''Cold War Endgame: Oral History, Analysis, and Debates'' (Penn State, 2003). 
Wohlforth published a seminal article <ref>Wohlforth, William C.  "The Stability of a Unipolar World," International Security Vol. 21, No. 1 (Summer 1999), pp. 5-41.</ref> in 1999, challenging the common knowledge at the time that US supremacy following the end of the Cold War is expected to be short-lived.<ref>Dall'Agnol, Augusto C. {{Cite journal|title = Balancing in unipolarity: who is afraid of balance of power?|url = https://www.academia.edu/37951607|journal = Brazilian Journal of International Relations}} Vol. 7, No. 3 (2018), pp. 494-515, for further discussions and critics on Wohlforth "unipolar stability".</ref>
He is linked to the [[Neoclassical realism]]-school and known for his work on American unipolarity, especially in collaboration with [[Stephen Brooks (academic)|Stephen Brooks]]. Together they have published several articles and a book, ''World Out of Balance: International Relations Theory and the Challenge of American Primacy''.

==Education==

Wohlforth received his bachelor's degree in International Relations (summa cum laude) from [[Beloit College]]. He went on to receive his Master's and PhD from Yale University in International Relations as well.
