'''Joan Scott Wallace''' (8 November 1930 - 15 March 2018)  was the first Black female leader of the [[United States Department of Agriculture]], serving as Assistant Secretary.  Wallace also served as a [[diplomat]], and outside of government service was a [[psychologist]] and educator.<ref name=":4">{{Cite book|last=United States. Department of Agriculture|url=http://archive.org/details/usda36unit|title=USDA|date=1977|publisher=[Washington] : U.S. Dept. of Agriculture, Office of Governmental and Public Affairs|others=National Agricultural Library U. S. Department of Agriculture}}</ref>

== Early life and education ==
Joan Edair Scott Wallace was born in Chicago to painter and muralist [[William Edouard Scott]] and social worker Esther Fulks Scott on November 8, 1930.<ref name=":0">{{Cite book|last=Smith|first=Jessie Carney|url=http://archive.org/details/notableblackamer00jess_0|title=Notable Black American women|date=1992|publisher=Deroit : Gale Research|others=Internet Archive|isbn=978-0-8103-4749-6|pages=1195â€“1196}}</ref> Wallace graduated from [[Englewood High School (Chicago, IL)|Englewood High School]] as the first Black [[salutatorian]] in 1948.<ref name=":0" />  She completed a bachelor's degree in [[sociology]] at [[Bradley University]] in 1952, a master's in [[social work]] from [[Columbia University]] in 1954, and a [[doctorate]] from [[Northwestern University]] in experimental [[social psychology]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=Biographical Note|url=https://www.lib.uchicago.edu/bmrc/view.php?eadid=BMRC.HARSH.WALLACE|url-status=live|access-date=2021-03-29|website=www.lib.uchicago.edu}}</ref> Wallace also attended the Harvard Institute for Educational Management in [[Boston|Boston, MA]].<ref name=":4" />

In 1954, Wallace married John H. Wallace with whom she had three sons, Eric, Victor, and Marc.<ref>{{Cite web|last=admin|date=2015-04-14|title=Mark Wallace Obituary - Death Notice and Service Information|url=https://www.legacy.com/link.asp?i=ls000174635007|access-date=2021-08-05|website=Legacy.com|language=en}}</ref>  They later divorced and Wallace marred pastor and activist Maurice Dawkins in 1979.  The two remained married until Dawkins' death in 2001 and in 2003, Wallace remarried her first husband.<ref name=":5">{{Cite web|last=Loch|first=Beth|last2=Zerega|first2=Elise|date=December 26, 2013|title=Joan S. Wallace Biographical/Historical note|url=https://bmrcprocessingproject.uchicago.edu/sites/bmrcprocessingproject.uchicago.edu/files/Finding%20Aids/Joan%20S.%20Wallace%20Papers.pdf|url-status=live|access-date=April 2, 2021|website=Joan S. Scott Papers Finding Aid}}</ref>

== Career ==
From 1967 to 1973, Wallace was the Associate Professor of Psychology and Social Work and Director of Undergraduate School of Social Work and Department of Psychology at the [[University of Illinois at Chicago|University of Illinois, Chicago]].<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|title=Joan Scott Wallace Papers|url=https://www.chipublib.org/fa-joan-scott-wallace-papers/|access-date=2021-08-05|website=www.chipublib.org|language=en-US}}</ref> In 1970, while on leave from the University of Illinois, Chicago, Wallace served as Director of [[African American studies|Afro American Studies]] and Associate Professor of Sociology and Psychology at [[Barat College]].<ref name=":2">{{Cite web|title=Joan S. Wallace papers|url=https://www.lib.uchicago.edu/bmrc/view.php?eadid=BMRC.HARSH.WALLACE|url-status=live|access-date=2021-08-05|website=www.lib.uchicago.edu}}</ref> Then, in 1973 Wallace was named Dean of [[Howard University]]'s School of Social Work.  Between 1975 and 1976, she served at the [[National Urban League]] as Deputy Executive Director for Programs.<ref name=":2" /> The following year, Wallace was named Vice President of Administration at [[Morgan State University]] and then became Director of the [[Western Michigan University]] School of [[Social work|Social Work]].<ref name=":3">{{Cite web|title=Dr. Joan Edair Wallace - View Obituary & Service Information|url=https://www.mcculloughfuneralservices.com/obituaries/Joan-Wallace-6/|access-date=2021-08-05|website=Dr. Joan Edair Wallace Obituary|language=en}}</ref>

In 1977, Wallace was appointed Assistant Secretary for Administration in the Department of Agriculture by President [[Jimmy Carter]], the third woman and the first African American to hold the position.<ref name=":3" /> She was in the position until 1981, when she became head of the International Cooperation and Development Agency (ICDA). At ICDA, Wallace sent specialists to provide technical assistance in agriculture to 100 foreign countries and managed over 500 research programs.<ref>{{Cite web|title=The Honorable Joan Scott Wallace's Biography|url=https://www.thehistorymakers.org/biography/honorable-joan-scott-wallace|access-date=2021-08-05|website=The HistoryMakers|language=en}}</ref> In 1989, President [[George H. W. Bush|George H.W. Bush]] appointed her to the Inter-American Institute for Co-operation on Agriculture (IICA) Diplomatic Representative in the [[Trinidad and Tobago|Republic of Trinidad and Tobago]]; she held the position until 1993.<ref name=":1" /> Wallace retired from Government service in 1995 and became the chairman of Americans for Democracy in Africa, an organization that monitors elections.  In 1999, Wallace joined the faculty of [[Florida International University]] as Professor and Associate Director of the School of Social Work.<ref name=":5" />  The following year, she served as Commissioner of Volunteer Florida.<ref name=":3" />

Joan Scott Wallace has been awarded honorary degrees from the [[University of Maryland Eastern Shore|University of Maryland, Eastern Shore]], [[Bowie State University|Bowie State College]], and [[Alabama A&M University]].<ref name=":1" />
