'''Dorothy Agnes Bennett''' (August 31, 1909 – February 9, 1999) was an American anthropologist, astronomer, curator, publisher, and author. She was the first assistant curator of the [[Hayden Planetarium]], and co-created the [[Little Golden Books]] franchise.

== Early life and education ==
Born in [[Minneapolis]], Dorothy Bennett was the only child of architect Daniel Bennett (born 1869) and wife Marion (born 1879).<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://familysearch.org/ark:/61903/1:1:M2GX-QCZ|title=United States Census, 1910|date=1910|website=FamilySearch|publisher=[[National Archives and Records Administration]]|access-date=April 9, 2016}}</ref> Bennett studied astronomy and anthropology at the [[University of Minnesota]], where she also participated in the Women's Athletic Association.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|url=http://www.minnesotaalumni.org/s/1118/social.aspx?sid=1118&gid=1&pgid=3685&cid=5996&ecid=5996&crid=0&calpgid=3654&calcid=5955|title=Bennett's Feast|last=Brady|first=Tim|website=University of Minnesota Alumni|access-date=2016-04-09|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221535/http://www.minnesotaalumni.org/s/1118/social.aspx?sid=1118&gid=1&pgid=3685&cid=5996&ecid=5996&crid=0&calpgid=3654&calcid=5955|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> Upon graduating with a B.A. in English in 1930, she moved to New York, having been denied the chance to study in Africa with an anthropology professor on account of her gender.<ref name=":1">{{Cite web|url=http://daily.jstor.org/dorothy-bennett/|title=The Star-Studded Life of Ms. Dorothy Bennett|last=Sohn|first=Amy Daily|language=en-US|access-date=2016-04-10}}</ref>

== Career ==
=== The American Museum of Natural History ===
Having been given a timeframe of 30 days to find a job in New York by her mother, Bennett was hired as an assistant by the [[American Museum of Natural History]]'s Department of Public Education.<ref>{{Cite book|url=http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1930.pdf|title=62nd annual report of the trustees for the year 1930|publisher=The American Museum of Natural History|year=1931|location=New York|pages=97|access-date=April 10, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221723/http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1930.pdf|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> Within a year, she was promoted to Assistant Curator, and she organized high-attending programs for the Evening Elementary School Students' Association (which consisted of international adult students),<ref>{{Cite book|url=http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1931.pdf|title=63rd annual report of the trustees for the year 1931|publisher=The American Museum of Natural History|year=1932|location=New York|pages=50, 102|access-date=April 10, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221641/http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1931.pdf|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> as well as for the Museum's Junior Astronomy Club.<ref>{{Cite news|title=NAVY SETS UP BASE TO OBSERVE ECLIPSE|date=July 31, 1932|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|page=24|via=ProQuest Historical Newspapers}}</ref> Nobel Prize winner [[Roy J. Glauber]] was a member of the club and credited it for inspiring his passion in science.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.nobelprize.org/nobel_prizes/physics/laureates/2005/glauber-bio.html|title=Roy J. Glauber - Biographical|last=Glauber|first=Roy|author-link=Roy J. Glauber|year=2006|editor-last=Grandin|editor-first=Karl|website=Nobel Prize|publisher=[[Nobel Foundation]]|location=Stockholm|access-date=2016-04-10}}</ref> In the evenings, Bennett attended anthropology classes at [[Columbia University]], taught by [[Margaret Mead]], [[Ruth Benedict]], and [[Alfred Radcliffe-Brown]]. Bennett substitute-taught one of Mead's courses in 1931.<ref name=":0" />

Upon the opening of the [[Hayden Planetarium]] in 1935, Bennett was promoted to assistant curator of astronomy and the Hayden Planetarium, where she delivered over 1,000 lectures between 1935 and 1939.<ref>{{Cite book|url=http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1936.pdf|title=68th Annual Report for the year 1936|publisher=[[American Museum of Natural History]]|year=1937|location=New York|pages=106|access-date=April 10, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221618/http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1936.pdf|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref> In 1937, she created and spearheaded the Hayden Planetarium-Grace Peruvian Eclipse Expedition to [[Cerro de Pasco]], [[Peru]] to view a particularly long solar eclipse on June 8.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=K.|first=C. P.|date=1937-01-01|title=The Hayden Planetarium-Grace Peruvian Eclipse Expedition|jstor=16486|journal=The Scientific Monthly|volume=45|issue=2|pages=186–189|bibcode=1937SciMo..45..186K}}</ref><ref>{{Cite book|url=http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1937.pdf|title=69th Annual Report for the Year 1937|publisher=The American Museum of Natural History|year=1938|location=New York|pages=57–58|access-date=April 10, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221709/http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1937.pdf|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref>

=== Publishing ===
Bennett's success in the Museum's education department led her to writing books for children and adolescents, with her first co-authored book on astronomy being published in 1935, staying in print for 60 years.<ref>{{Cite journal|date=1941-01-01|title=First Glances at New Books|jstor=3918053|journal=The Science News-Letter|volume=39|issue=19|pages=304}}</ref> In 1939, she left the Museum for a position as sales and promotion manager at [[University of Minnesota Press]], publishing a bestselling biography of the [[Mayo brothers]].<ref>{{Cite book|url=http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1939.pdf|title=71st Annual Report for the year 1939|publisher=The American Museum of Natural History|year=1940|location=New York|pages=37|access-date=April 10, 2016|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161017221630/http://lbry-web-003.amnh.org/museum/annual_reports/source/R1939.pdf|archive-date=October 17, 2016|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news|title=150 Years of Memories|last=Smetanka|first=Mary Jane|date=October 22, 2000|newspaper=[[Star Tribune]]|location=Minneapolis, MN|page=01E|via=ProQuest Newspapers}}</ref>

Bennett returned to New York in 1941, where she worked with [[Georges Duplaix]] to develop the [[Little Golden Books]]. Under Duplaix, Bennett became the editor of the [[Golden Books]] franchise, producing books by such authors and illustrators as [[Margaret Wise Brown]], [[Clement Hurd]], [[Edith Thacher Hurd]], and [[Garth Williams]].<ref>{{Cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=Bj2NBpv_vpsC|title=Warman's Little Golden Books Field Guide: Values and Identification|last=Santi|first=Steve|date=2005-08-05|publisher=[[Krause Publications]]|isbn=0896892654|page=19|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|last=Stanton|first=Joseph|date=1993-01-01|title=Review of Margaret Wise Brown: Awakened by the Moon|jstor=23539995|journal=Biography|volume=16|issue=3|pages=276–278|doi=10.1353/bio.2010.0391|s2cid=161677858}}</ref> Bennett authored several Golden Books,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.worldcat.org/search?q=au%3A%22Bennett%2C+Dorothy+A.%22&qt=results_page|title=WorldCat, author Dorothy A. Bennett|publisher=[[OCLC]]|access-date=2016-04-09}}</ref> as well as introducing some of the first recorded books for children with [[Little Golden Records]] in 1948.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Cunningham|first=Virginia|date=1948-01-01|title=Other Publications|jstor=891520|journal=Notes|publisher=Music Library Association|volume=6|issue=1|pages=167–170|doi=10.2307/891519}}</ref> She was also involved with the production of the [[Golden Nature Guide]]s and regional guides in 1949.{{citation needed|date=May 2016}}

=== Leaving publishing ===
After leaving [[Simon & Schuster]] in 1954, Bennett enrolled in a Foreign Service Institute Course on the Middle East at the [[American University of Beirut]] and then in [[V. Gordon Childe]]'s archaeology courses at [[UCL Institute of Archaeology]].<ref name=":1" /> Bennett then moved to [[University of California, Berkeley]] to become senior anthropologist at the [[Lowie Museum of Anthropology]], where she managed collections.<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Epstein|first=Jeremiah F.|date=1958-01-01|title=Anthropological Activities in the United States 1958-1959|jstor=40974164|journal=Boletín Bibliográfico de Antropología Americana|volume=21/22|issue=1|pages=26–61}}</ref>

In the 1960s, Bennett worked with the [[Berkeley Unified School District]] to create an interdisciplinary multimedia course called ''Educational Programming of Cultural Heritage'' (EPOCH), which lasted until 1969.<ref>{{Cite journal|last1=Monfort|first1=Jay B.|last2=Others|first2=And|title=EPOCH--ESEA PROJECT FOR EDUCATIONAL PROGRAMMING OF CULTURAL HERITAGE. PLANNING PERIOD REPORT.|url=http://eric.ed.gov/?id=ED016741|language=en|publisher=Berkeley Unified School District|date=1967}}</ref><ref>{{Cite journal|date=1969|title=Audiovisual Instruction|department=Department of Audiovisual Instruction|journal=Audiovisual Instruction|publisher=National Endowment of the Arts}}</ref>

== Retirement ==
After the end of EPOCH, Bennett and her companion, child psychologist [[Rosamund Gardner]], moved to [[Taos, New Mexico]] where they built an adobe home. They associated with artists and painters like [[Erik Bauersfeld]], [[Dorothy Brett]], and others. Bennett died in Taos in 1999.<ref>{{Cite journal|date=2011|title=1935-2014|journal=Social Security Death Index|publisher=Social Security Administration}}</ref>
