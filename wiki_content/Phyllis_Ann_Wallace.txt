'''Phyllis A. Wallace''' (1921–1993) was a distinguished African American [[economist]] and [[activist]], as well as the first woman to receive doctorate of economics at Yale University.<ref name=Wayne>{{cite book|last1=Wayne|first1=Tiffany|title=American Woman of Science Since 1900|date=2011|publisher=ABC CLIO, LLC|isbn=978-1-59884-158-9|page=111}}</ref> Her work tended to focus on racial, as well as gender discrimination in the workplace.<ref name=Wayne/>

==Early life==

She was born Annie Rebecca Wallace in Calvert County, Maryland, on June 9, 1921 to John Wallace, a craftsman, and Stevella Wallace.<ref name=Malveaux2004>{{cite book|last1=Malveaux|first1=Julianne|editor1-last=Ware|editor1-first=Susan|editor2-last=Braukman|editor2-first=Stacy Lorraine|title=Notable American Women: Completing the Twentieth Century|date=2004|publisher=Harvard University Press|chapter-url=http://search.credoreference.com/content/entry/hupnawiii/wallace_phyllis_ann_june_9_1921_january_10_1993/0|chapter=Wallace, Phyllis Ann. June 9, 1921-January 10, 1993}}</ref><ref name=Cicarelli>{{cite book|last1=Cicarelli|first1=James|last2=Cicarelli|first2=Julianne|title=Distinguished Women Economists|date=2003|publisher=Greenwood Publishing Group|location=United States|isbn=978-0-313-30331-9|pages=206–208}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{Cite book|url=https://www.worldcat.org/oclc/24468213|title=Notable Black American women|date=©1992-©2003|publisher=Gale Research|others=Smith, Jessie Carney, 1930-, Phelps, Shirelle.|isbn=0-8103-4749-0|location=Detroit|oclc=24468213}}</ref> She attended a well ranked yet segregated high school, Frederick Douglass High School, graduating in 1939.<ref name=":0" />

Despite ranking first in her high school class, state law at that time would not allow her to attend the all-white University of Maryland.<ref name=":0" /> She attended New York University, receiving a bachelor's degree in economics in 1943, graduating magna cum laude and Phi Beta Kappa.<ref name="Malveaux2004" /><ref name=":0" />

She later attended [[Yale University]], earning a master's degree in 1944 and a PhD in 1948.<ref name="Malveaux2004" /> A mix of encouragement from her Yale economist professor and work at a federal-defense agency made her decide to pursue a career in international economics.<ref name="Cicarelli" />

==Career==

Her work began studying economic growth in the [[Soviet Union]], but later transferred to a focus in workplace economics, joining the senior staff of [[Equal Employment Opportunity Commission|Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC)]] in 1965.<ref name=Cicarelli/><ref name=Thomas>{{cite book|last1=Malveaux|first1=Julianne|title=A Different Vision: Volume 1|date=January 4, 2002|publisher=Thomas D Boston|location=29 West 35th Street New York NY 10001|isbn=9781134798605|pages=129–135}}</ref> She became a voice for anti-discrimination in the workplace, and was an important part of the anti-workplace-discrimination contingencies of the [[Civil Rights Act of 1964]].<ref name=Wayne/> Her work shifted again towards economic issues with urban minority youth when she began working for Metropolitan Applied Research Center (MARC).<ref name=Wayne/>

Wallace joined the faculty of [[Massachusetts Institute of Technology|MIT]] in 1972 as a visiting professor, and was tenured as full professor in 1974, in the [[MIT Sloan School of Management|Sloan School]].<ref name="AER">{{Cite journal|jstor = 2117809|title = Tilting against the wind: Reflections on the life and work of Phyllis Ann Wallace|last = Malveaux|first = Julianne|date = 1994|journal = The American Economic Review|volume = 84|issue = 2|pages = 93–97}}</ref> Her appointment made her the first woman to gain tenure at Sloan.<ref name=":1">{{Cite news|last=Daniels|first=Lee A.|title=Phyllis Wallace, 69, A labor economist in A.T.&T. lawsuit (obituary)|date=January 13, 1993|work=The New York Times}}</ref> Wallace retired from active teaching in 1986.<ref name=":1" />

==Achievements==

Wallace was the first African American and the first female president of the [[Industrial Relations Research Association]].<ref name="AER" /> She also garnered several awards for her accomplishments, including [[National Economic Association|National Economic Association's]] Westerfield Award in 1981,<ref name=Thomas/> and awards from several universities, including Yale (1980) and Brown (1986).<ref name=Cicarelli/>
