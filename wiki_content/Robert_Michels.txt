'''Robert Michels''' ({{IPA-de|ˈmɪçəls|lang}}; 9 January 1876 – 3 May 1936) was a [[Germany|German]]-born [[Italy|Italian]] [[sociologist]] who contributed to [[elite theory]] by describing the political behavior of intellectual elites. 

He belonged to the [[Italian school of elitism]].<ref>{{cite book|last=Nye |first=Robert A. |year=1977 |title=The Anti-Democratic Sources of Elite Theory: Pareto, Mosca, Michels |location=London |publisher=SAGE Publications |page=30}}</ref><ref>{{cite book|editor-last=Chambliss |editor-first=J. J. |year=2013 |title=Philosophy of Education: An Encyclopedia |location=London |publisher=Routledge |page=179}}</ref> He is best known for his book ''[[Political Parties (book)|Political Parties]]'', published in 1911, which contains a description of the "[[iron law of oligarchy]]." He was a friend and disciple of [[Max Weber]], [[Werner Sombart]] and [[Achille Loria]]. 

Politically, he moved from the [[Social Democratic Party of Germany]] to the [[Italian Socialist Party]], adhering to the Italian [[revolutionary syndicalist]] wing and later to [[Italian Fascism]], which he saw as a more democratic form of [[socialism]]. His ideas provided the basis of [[moderation theory]] which delineates the processes through which radical political groups are incorporated into the existing political system.

==Early life and education==
Michels born to a wealthy [[Germans|German]] family, studied in [[England]], [[Paris]] (at the [[University of Paris|Sorbonne]]), and at universities in [[Munich]], [[Leipzig]] (1897), [[Halle, Saxony-Anhalt|Halle]] (1898), and [[Turin]]. He became a [[socialist]] while teaching at the [[University of Marburg]] and became active in the [[Social Democratic Party of Germany]] for whom he was an unsuccessful candidate in the [[1903 German federal election]]. In Italy, he associated with [[:it:sindacalismo rivoluzionario#Il sindacalismo rivoluzionario in Italia|Italian revolutionary syndicalism]], a leftist branch of the [[Italian Socialist Party]] (PSI). He left both parties in 1907.<ref name="PC71">{{Cite journal |last=Cook |first=Philip |date=August 1971 |title=Robert Michels's Political Parties in Perspective |journal=Journal of Politics |volume=33 |issue=3 |pages=777–778 |doi=10.2307/2128281 |jstor=2128281|s2cid=153934725 }}</ref>

==Career==
He achieved international recognition for his historical and sociological study, ''Zur Soziologie des Parteiwesens in der modernen Demokratie. Untersuchungen über die oligarchischen Tendenzen des Gruppenlebens'', which was published in 1911; its title in English is ''[[Political Parties (book)|Political Parties: A Sociological Study of the Oligarchical Tendencies of Modern Democracy]]''. In it, he presented his theory of the "[[iron law of oligarchy]]" that political parties, including those considered socialist, cannot be democratic because they quickly transform themselves into bureaucratic oligarchies. 

Michels attended the [[First International Eugenics Congress]] in 1912 where he delivered a paper entitled "Eugenics in Party Organization".<ref name="PiE Vol. 1">{{Cite book |year=1913 |title=Problems in Eugenics: Papers communicated to the First International Eugenics Congress Held at the University of London, July 24th to 30th, 1912 |location=London |publisher=The Eugenics Education Society }}</ref>
Michels was considered a brilliant pupil of [[Max Weber]], who began publishing his writings in the ''[[Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik]]'' in 1906<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Scaff |first=Lawrence |date=May 1981 |title=Max Weber and Robert Michels |journal=American Journal of Sociology |volume=86 |issue=6 |pages=1269–1286 |doi=10.1086/227385 |jstor=2778815|s2cid=144580976 }}</ref> and appointed him as co-editor in 1913, but they disagreed over Michels' opposition to [[World War I]].<ref name="PC71" />

Michels criticized what he perceived to be [[Karl Marx]]'s [[Materialism|materialistic]] [[determinism]]. Michels borrowed from [[Werner Sombart]]'s historical methods. Because Michels admired Italian culture and was prominent in the [[social sciences]], he was brought to the attention of [[Luigi Einaudi]] and [[Achille Loria]]. They succeeded in procuring for Michels a professorship at the [[University of Turin]], where he taught [[economics]], [[political science]] and [[socioeconomics]] until 1914. He then became professor of economics at the [[University of Basel]], Switzerland, a post he held until 1928.<ref name="CA04">{{Cite news |title=Michels, Robert 1876–1936 |url=http://www.highbeam.com/doc/1G2-3412600198.html |date=January 2004 |work=Contemporary Authors {{Subscription required|via=[[HighBeam Research]]}}|access-date=26 December 2014 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150329104106/http://www.highbeam.com/doc/1G2-3412600198.html |archive-date=2015-03-29}}</ref>

In 1924, he joined the [[Fascist Party]], led by [[Benito Mussolini]], former director of the Italian Socialist Party's newspaper "[[Avanti! (Italian newspaper)|''Avanti!'']]". Michels was convinced that the direct link between [[Benito Mussolini]]'s [[charisma]] and the working class was in some way the best means to realize a real lower social class government without political bureaucratic mediation. In 1928, he became professor of economics and the history of doctrines at the [[University of Perugia]]<ref name="CA04" /> and occasionally lectured in [[Rome]] where he died on May 3, 1936.
