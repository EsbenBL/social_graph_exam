'''Liberty Walther Barnes''' is a [[sociologist]], [[ethnographer]], and writer. She holds affiliations at the [[University of Cambridge]] in the United Kingdom and the [[University of Oregon]] in the United States. Trained in [[medical sociology]] and [[gender studies]], Barnes's research examines how medical authorities serve as cultural authorities on family building, [[fatherhood]], [[motherhood]], and [[childhood]].

== Personal life and education ==
Barnes was born in Fort Leavenworth, Kansas, and raised in [[Federal Way, Washington|Federal Way]], Washington. She is the daughter of Larry Walther and Kathryn Walther (n√©e Hiersche) and the oldest of five children. Barnes graduated from Federal Way High School in 1993 and earned a B.A. in [[media arts]] from [[Brigham Young University]] (1999). She completed MA and PhD degrees in Sociology from the [[University of California at San Diego]] (2005, 2011).<ref>personal interview{{better source|date=September 2015}}</ref>

== Career ==
Barnes is the author of ''Conceiving Masculinity: Male Infertility, Medicine, and Identity'' (Temple University Press 2014), based on ethnographic observations in infertility clinics, laboratories, and medical conferences and interviews with medical practitioners and patients.<ref>{{Cite web|title = Liberty Walther Barnes: Conceiving Masculinity|url = http://www.temple.edu/tempress/titles/2277_reg.html|website = www.temple.edu|access-date = 2015-09-15}}</ref> In 2015, Barnes was awarded the Book of the Year Prize by the Foundation for the Sociology of Health and Illness for "making the most significant contribution to the sub-discipline of medical sociology."<ref>{{Cite web|url = http://www.britsoc.co.uk/groups/bsa-medical-sociology/foundation-for-the-sociology-of-health-and-illness-book-prize.aspx|title = Foundation for the Sociology of Health and Illness Book Prize|date = |website = |publisher = |last = |first = |archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20150913161452/http://www.britsoc.co.uk/groups/bsa-medical-sociology/foundation-for-the-sociology-of-health-and-illness-book-prize.aspx|archive-date = September 13, 2015|url-status = dead}}</ref> Previous winners of the prize include Nelly Oudshoorn, Annemarie Mol, and Margaret Lock.

Barnes's research has been featured by several media websites, including TheAtlantic.com,<ref>{{Cite web|title = Men Have Biological Clocks, Too|url = https://www.theatlantic.com/health/archive/2013/10/men-have-biological-clocks-too/280673/|access-date = 2015-09-15|first = Jacoba|last = Urist}}</ref> NBCNews.com, ABCNews.com, Slate.com, TheGuardian.com, and TheTelegraph.com. She has been interviewed by Texas Public Radio, WHYY Philadelphia, and Voice of Russia UK.

In 2014 a [[TEDx]] talk given by Barnes was featured as an "Editor's Pick" on the TEDx homepage. The same year Barnes received a Future Research Leaders Award from the [[Economic and Social Research Council]] in the UK.<ref>{{Cite web|url = http://www.britishandrology.org.uk/a-social-history-of-sperm/|title = A Social History of Sperm}}</ref>

In 2016, Barnes received a grant from the [[National Science Foundation]]'s Division of Social and Economic Sciences to write an [[ethnography]] of [[Children's hospital|children's medicine]].<ref>{{cite web|last1=Barlow|first1=Jim|title=UO sociologist wins major grant to explore U.S. pediatric care|url=http://around.uoregon.edu/content/uo-sociologist-wins-major-grant-explore-us-pediatric-care|website=University of Oregon|access-date=31 October 2016}}</ref>

Barnes has taught sociology courses at the University of Cambridge, [[Pacific University]], Brigham Young University, University of California at San Diego, and [[Portland Community College]].
