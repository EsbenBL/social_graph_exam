'''Mary Lindsay Elmendorf''' was an American applied anthropologist, recognized mainly for her work with the [[Maya women|Mayan women]] of [[Mexico]] and her application of [[anthropology]] in consultation with technology.<ref name=":0">{{Cite web|last1=Elmendorf|first1=Mary L. (Mary Lindsay)|last2=Elmendorf|first2=Mary L. (Mary Lindsay)|title=A Guide to the Mary L. Elmendorf Papers MS Group 093|url=http://www.library.ufl.edu/spec/manuscript/guides/Elmendorf.htm|access-date=2020-11-18|website=www.library.ufl.edu}}</ref>  Her early work involved rural south and the slums of [[Boston]] and [[New Haven, Connecticut|New Haven]] as well as in the Putney School in [[Vermont]] and Mexico.<ref name=":0" />  Her application of anthropology focused mainly on involving women with planning and implementation of suitable technologies for those women and others to choose and manage their development strategies.<ref name=":0" />

== Life and education ==
Elmendorf met her first husband, John Elmendorf, as a student at the [[University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill|University of North Carolina]] at [[Chapel Hill, North Carolina|Chapel Hill]]; they married in December 1937.<ref name=":0"/> John Elmendorf died in 1980 and she eventually remarried Dr. John Landgraf (1914 - 2010).<ref name=":0" /><ref>{{Cite web|title=John Landgraf - Obituary|url=https://www.legacy.com/amp/obituaries/heraldtribune/147165715|access-date=2020-10-07|website=www.legacy.com|language=en}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|title=NCFDC Home - John van G. Elmendorf, 1965-1972|url=http://ncf.sobek.ufl.edu/elmendorf|access-date=2020-10-07|website=ncf.sobek.ufl.edu}}</ref>

She attended St. Pauls High School in 1933 and was a valedictorian.<ref name=":122">{{Cite web|title=Mary Lindsay Elmendorf|url=https://www.womenshistory.org/mary-lindsay-elmendorf|access-date=2020-09-23|website=National Women's History Museum|language=en}}</ref><ref name=":32">{{Cite web|title=Webpage Temp|url=http://www.library.ufl.edu/spec/manuscript/Elmendorf/Vitae.htm#Joe%20McCarthy|access-date=2020-10-04|website=www.library.ufl.edu}}</ref> and earned her B.A. [[Psychology]] (1937) and her M.A. equivalent in [[Public administration|Public Administration]] and [[Social work|Social Work]] (1941) at [[University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill|UNC-Chapel Hill]].<ref name=":122"/>  She graduated from school in [[Anthropology]] (1946 - 1948) and then attended Union Graduate School in 1972 earning a Ph.D. in Anthropology.<ref name=":32" />

Elmendorf became affiliated with the [[American Anthropological Association]], the [[Society for Applied Anthropology]], the Association for the Advancement of Science, the [[American Association of University Women|AAUW]], the [[United Nations Association of the United States of America|United Nations Association of the US]], the [[Unifem|UNIFEM]]/Gulf Coast Chapter, and the Democratic Club.<ref name=":122"/> She volunteered with the [[American Friends Service Committee]] in 1944 - 1946.<ref name=":0" /> She was the head of the [[Care International|CARE]] office in Mexico (1952 - 1960) and became the first anthropologist hired by the [[World Bank]] (1975).<ref name=":0" /> Elmendorf was involved in various educator projects like [[Brown University]] (1962–65), [[Goddard College]] (1973), etc.<ref name=":0" /> Based on her work and application of anthropology, she aided at the [[:Category:United Nations conferences|United Nations Conferences]] on Women held in Mexico, [[Copenhagen]], [[Nairobi]], and [[Beijing]].<ref name=":0" />

Elmendorf died on September 15, 2017, at 100 years old<ref>{{Cite web|title=Mary Elmendorf (1917 - 2017) - Obituary|url=https://www.legacy.com/amp/obituaries/heraldtribune/186735078|access-date=2020-10-07|website=www.legacy.com|language=en}}</ref>

== Nine Mayan Women ==
Elmendorf's best-known work, ''Nine Mayan Women,'' illustrates her case studies in the village of Chan Kom of [[Maya peoples|Mayan]] women in the [[Yucatán Peninsula]].<ref name=":4">{{Cite web|title=Elmendorf, Mary L. (Mary Lindsay). Nine Mayan women: a village faces change|url=https://ehrafworldcultures.yale.edu/ehrafe/citation.do?method=citation&forward=browseAuthorsFullContext&id=nv10-017|url-status=live|archive-url=|archive-date=|access-date=|website=}}</ref> The book studies nine women who are spoken about in chapter two.<ref name=":5">{{Cite journal|last=Schmidt|first=Steffen W.|date=1983|editor-last=Lavrin|editor-first=Asuncion|editor2-last=Henderson|editor2-first=James D.|editor3-last=Henderson|editor3-first=Linda Roddy|editor4-last=Kelley|editor4-first=Jane Holden|editor5-last=Elmendorf|editor5-first=Mary Lindsay|editor6-last=Roberts|editor6-first=George W.|editor7-last=Sinclair|editor7-first=Sonja A.|editor8-last=de Leal|editor8-first=Magdalena Leon|editor9-last=Committee|editor9-first=Wellesley Editorial|title=Women, Politics and Development|url=https://www.jstor.org/stable/2502868|journal=Latin American Research Review|volume=18|issue=1|pages=210–227|jstor=2502868|issn=0023-8791}}</ref> She covered background information on Chan Kom as well as the study itself.<ref name=":5" /> The rest of the book is a broader reflection of the preceding vignettes interwoven with mentions and attribution of other sources that relate to Mayan communities and culture.<ref name=":5" /> The main focus of her study was on the quality of life issues and the life satisfaction of Mayan women.<ref name=":4" /> In studying these aspects, she was able to see the Mayan women are happy and content with their lives and appear to value work as they also share a mutual respect with their husbands.<ref name=":4" />

Her work has been reviewed by other scholars like Steffen W. Schmidt in ''[[Latin American Research Review]].'' <ref>{{Cite book|last=Schoultz|first=Lars|url=http://dx.doi.org/10.1515/9781400854295|title=Human Rights and United States Policy Toward Latin America|date=1981-12-31|publisher=Princeton University Press|isbn=978-1-4008-5429-5|location=Princeton|doi=10.1515/9781400854295}}</ref> And influences other scholar's work like John G. Frazier (and others) in their book, ''Rights, resources, culture, and conservation in the land of the Maya.''<ref>{{Cite journal|last=Gorenflo|first=Larry J.|date= 2004|title=Rights, Resources, Culture, and Conservation in the Land of the Maya edited by Betty B.
