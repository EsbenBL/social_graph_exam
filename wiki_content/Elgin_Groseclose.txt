'''Elgin Earl Groseclose''' (25 November 1899 &ndash; 7 April 1983) was an American economist, statesman, and author.

==Early life==
Elgin Groseclose was born in 1899 in [[Waukomis, Oklahoma]].<ref name="nytimesobitelgin">{{cite news|title=Elgin Groseclose, 83, Author and Economist|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1983/04/07/obituaries/elgin-groseclose-83-author-and-economist.html|accessdate=September 10, 2016|work=The New York Times|date=April 7, 1983}}</ref>

==Career==
After working as special assistant to [[Arthur Millspaugh]]'s economic mission in [[Persia]], he was appointed Treasurer-General of Persia by the order of the [[Majles|parliament of Iran]] in 1943.<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/> He also headed the ''Persian Relief Commission'' and wrote a book entitled ''Introduction to Iran''.

Groseclose was the author of many books. For ''Ararat'', an adventure novel set in [[Armenia]], he won a [[List of National Book Award winners#1935 to 1941|National Book Award]] as the Bookseller Discovery of 1939, voted by members of the [[American Booksellers Association]].<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/> The annual Discovery identified "outstanding merit which failed to receive adequate sales and recognition".<ref name=nyt1940>"1939 Book Awards Given by Critics: Elgin Groseclose's 'Ararat' is Picked as Work Which Failed to Get Due Recognition", ''The New York Times'', February 14, 1940, page 25. ProQuest Historical Newspapers The New York Times (1851-2007).</ref>

Groseclose was the co-founder of Groseclose, Williams and Associates, a consulting firm.<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/> He testified before the [[United States House of Representatives]] in favor of the [[silver standard]] and against [[United States foreign aid|foreign aid]].<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/>

Groseclose served as the president of the Washington City Bible Society.<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/>

==Personal life and death==
With his wife Louise, he had four daughters, Jane, Nancy, Hildegarde, and Suzy.<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/> He died on April 7, 1983.<ref name="nytimesobitelgin"/>
